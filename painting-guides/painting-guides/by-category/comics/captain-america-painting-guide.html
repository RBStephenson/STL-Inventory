Captain America — Steve Rogers is the embodiment of heroic idealism: broad-shouldered, fair-skinned, lit by conviction that reads as warmth from across the room. Paint this figure with a strong overhead zenithal — the chest scale mail catches directional light beautifully, and the metallic sheen on those scales should shimmer without overwhelming the pure red, white, and blue of the classic suit. The cold, shattered stone of the Hydra ruin base is your contrast anchor: make the figure above it sing.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal. Steve Rogers reads as a rugged, masculine hero — military, world-worn, but lit from within. The red zenithal builds a warm flush that suits hard facial planes and visible muscle definition. If you want the "luminous protagonist" look, switch to Method 2A (Pinkle) — the magenta underpainting adds sub-surface scattering that makes his skin glow.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze. Most predictable.

Easy to correct; works at any scale Can read flat without enough layers Speed priority; first-time painters; 28mm
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → warm red wash over all → thin flesh layers over top. Skip the magenta step.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; works beautifully on rugged masculine skin Zenithal quality matters — poor zenithal = muddy result Rugged male, military, survivor, heroic display figures — 1:6 / 75mm

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → warm red into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; "lit from within" heroic glow Magenta can overpower if too thick; harder to correct Alternative for a more idealized, luminous heroic Cap

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin seal → yellow wash (fatty zones) → seal → blue wash (veins) → seal → red wash (capillaries).

Most realistic tonal mapping; professional portrait results Slowest — 3 varnish layers; not suitable for 28mm Maximum realism; display portrait; time to invest
Freckling / Speckling: After skin is sealed, thin Burnt Sienna 059 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots, not lines. Focus on nose, cheeks, backs of hands, forearms. One pass → step back → assess. Easy to overdo.

Top & Suit

Two distinct surface areas: (1) the scale mail / chainmail chest armor with a special metallic sheen treatment, and (2) the red/white horizontal stripe panel on the abdomen. This is where the costume reads as graphic and iconic.

Scale Mail — Special Technique Overview
✦ METALLIC SHEEN APPROACH: Build the full blue value range first, exactly like the cowl. Seal with satin varnish. Then apply a thin filter of Metallic Medium 033 (1:4 thinned) over the scale surface from above — this adds a subtle reflective quality without changing the blue color. On the very final edge highlight pass, mix 15–20% Silver 025 into the highlight blue. The scales will catch light with a metallic shimmer while reading as blue at arm's length.

Metallic Medium Filter — Technique Detail
✦ THE METALLIC SHEEN PASS: This technique works because Metallic Medium 033 contains fine metallic particles that deposit on sealed surfaces without significantly changing the underlying color. Key rules: (1) always over a satin-sealed surface, (2) one thin pass from directly above, (3) do not go back over wet medium — it activates and streaks. Think of it as a varnish with shimmer, not a paint layer.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: PSI too high for the distance, or needle pulled too far back for the dilution. Effect: wet paint blows back off the surface. Fix: reduce PSI by 5, increase distance by 5cm, reduce needle pull. For the metallic medium filter pass especially — keep PSI at 12–15 and distance at 10cm minimum.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving too slowly across the surface, or trigger pull inconsistent between passes. Effect: visible stripes in the blue base coat. Fix: keep the airbrush moving at a steady pace before and after trigger pull. On scale mail, make long diagonal passes rather than short targeted bursts.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: over-thinned past 1:5, especially on the near-vertical suit surfaces. Effect: paint runs and obscures zenithal gradient. Fix: test ratio on a card before applying to figure. A well-thinned mix flows off a palette knife in a slow, unbroken stream — not a drip. If running, add one drop of paint to the cup and test again.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting on scale mail, layering on smooth surfaces (cowl, shield), wet blending for Expert Acrylics skin, drybrushing for concrete and leather.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Scale Mail — Painting Individual Scale Edges

Load a size 1 or 0 brush with thinned highlight mix (1:2). Wipe brush on palette until nearly dry — just a thin line of paint on the tip. Drag lightly along the top arc of each scale, following the curve. The arc of light should be thin, consistent, and sit on the upper 20% of each scale face. Work in rows from top to bottom — the upper rows catch more light and should be slightly brighter than lower rows. Let each row dry before starting the next.

✦ TIP: If individual scale edges are too small for a flat drag, try the "stipple-drag" — touch the tip of the brush to the scale top and drag toward you in a single smooth motion. A 5/0 or 10/0 liner works better than a flat brush for very small-scale chainmail.
Technique · Layering

Smooth Surface Layering (Cowl, Shield)

Unlike the scale mail, the cowl and shield are smooth surfaces — highlights should transition gradually, not edge-by-edge. Load brush fully, thin the paint to 1:2, and apply each layer leaving a small feathered band at the boundary. While the layer is still wet, take a clean damp brush and very lightly feather the edge. On the cowl, three highlight layers from the shadow base to specular will read as a smooth airbrushed transition — even if done entirely by brush.

Technique · Drybrushing

Concrete & Leather — Controlled Drybrush

For the base rubble and leather edges, drybrushing is your primary highlight technique. Load a flat brush or old stiff brush and wipe aggressively on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers. One stroke on the towel should leave almost no mark. Now drag across the textured surface — paint deposits only on the peaks. Multiple passes with light pressure build naturally and catch detail. Heavy drybrushing (too much paint) creates a chalky, dusty look — right for concrete, wrong for leather. For leather, err on the side of too little paint and build slowly.

✦ TIP: Pro Acryl Expert Acrylics (heavy-body) are exceptionally good for drybrushing — their dense pigment and slow dry time give clean, controlled deposits. Standard dropper-bottle Pro Acryl can be used near-undiluted for the same effect.
Technique · Targeted Wash

Recesses & Panel Lines — Capillary Flow

For panel lines in the suit, seams in the cowl, and carved lines in the Hydra stone: apply the wash with a fine (5/0 or 10/0) liner brush directly into the recess. The paint will flow via capillary action into the line — do not brush it around. Once placed, leave it. Any excess that escapes onto the flat surface should be lifted immediately with a clean near-dry brush before it dries. Working in small sections (5cm at a time) prevents the wash from drying mid-flow and leaving tide marks.
