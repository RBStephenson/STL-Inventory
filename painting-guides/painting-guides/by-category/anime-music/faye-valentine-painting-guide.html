Faye Valentine — a luminous, world-weary bounty hunter rendered as a display-grade 1:6 figure. Primary light source is zenithal (overhead), with a strong secondary amber OSL rising from the glowing LED crate base. The three material priorities are: smooth, warm Pinkle-method skin that reads as porcelain-to-warm-flesh across the large exposed skin area; the bright yellow of the outfit, which must hold saturated warm mid-tones while transitioning smoothly from warm ochre shadow to near-white highlight; and the amber OSL from the base panels, which needs to touch boots, lower legs, and the underside of the crate consistently so the lighting reads as coherent.

Method Selection
Recommended: Method 2A — The Pinkle Method. Faye is a fair-skinned female protagonist with a large exposed skin surface area at 1:6. The magenta underpainting will read as natural sub-surface scattering across her torso, arms, and legs — the scale rewards the luminosity payoff. The zenithal must be strong; poor coverage will produce muddy results across all that skin.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers at 1:6 scale Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, time-limited builds
Recommended

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → FW Crimson Ink into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; naturally warm shadows Poor zenithal = muddy across the large skin area Fair / feminine, display 1:6, large exposed skin surfaces

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Same as 2A but skip magenta — apply FW Crimson Ink directly over white zenithal.

Natural exertion flush; good for darker or weathered complexions Less luminosity than 2A; shadows read more earthy than pink Rugged / weathered figures; slightly faster than 2A

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (veins) → seal → red wash (capillary zones).

Most realistic tonal mapping; three distinct undertone zones Slowest — 3 varnish cycles; one wash too thick = muddy Historical portrait, max realism; combine with Method 2 for expert result
No Freckling: Smooth graphic highlight approach throughout. Faye's skin reads as clean and idealized per the source material. Bold, pushed value transitions rather than organic textural complexity.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

Full coverage black prime, undiluted. 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Every surface covered — no bare resin. This establishes your deepest shadow anchor for the entire figure.

Black Primer P-002

~4% value — shadow anchor
✦ TIP: Do NOT thin primer. Apply undiluted at 30+ PSI. Rotate the figure and hit all undercut surfaces — underarms, inside of jacket, under the chin.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal

Heavy from directly above, fading to nothing by mid-torso sides and undercuts. 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Top planes of head, shoulders, chest, and thighs should be near-white. Sides retain mid-grey. Recesses stay black. This zenithal IS your value map — invest time here.

White Primer P-003
Pro Acryl
~95% value — zenithal peak
✦ TIP: Photograph, desaturate, and check your zenithal before adding any color. Top planes bright, sides mid-grey, undersides dark. If it all reads mid-grey, push the white harder from directly above.
Underpainting
Step 3 · Airbrush

Magenta Wash — All Over Skin

The "pinkle." Thin Magenta 011 to 1:4 (paint:thinner) — near-transparent. Apply all over every skin surface in two passes. The white zenithal should still read clearly through it. This layer mimics sub-surface blood vessels and will glow through every flesh layer above it.

Magenta 011
Pro Acryl
~40% value — transparent underpainting
⚠ NOTE: The magenta must be transparent — if it reads as solid pink, it's too thick and will muddy every flesh layer above. Add more thinner and re-test on card first.
Step 4 · Brush

FW Crimson Ink — Recess Shadow

FW Crimson Ink thinned 1:2 with water. Flow into: underarm recesses, elbow creases, behind the knees, under the chin, eyelid folds, between the fingers, joint creases. Wipe back immediately with a barely-damp flat brush if it pools too widely.

FW Crimson Ink
FW Inks
~30% value — warm recess shadow
⚠ NOTE: Use FW Crimson Ink here — NOT Pro Acryl Transparent Red 047. PA Transparent Red shifts to magenta when thinned and will fight the flesh layers above it. FW Crimson Ink holds its red regardless of dilution.
Flesh Build

Base Flesh — Thin Pass

Shadow Flesh 042 thinned 1:4. Two thin passes over all skin — leave deepest recesses untouched. This is a translucent base coat establishing the shadow value, not full coverage. Magenta and FW Crimson should show through in deep recesses.

Shadow Flesh 042

Mid-Tone Flesh — Float

Warm Flesh 073 thinned 1:4. Float onto raised planes and top-lit surfaces only — chest peaks, shoulders, forehead, nose bridge, thighs. Leave Shadow Flesh showing in transitions. Should read ~54% value — the true skin color. Build in 3 passes, short trigger pull.

Heavy Warm White S18 on a small round brush, wiped to near-dry. Apply only to the very apex of each form: nose tip, center lower lip, highest cheekbone peak, center shoulder cap. Should read ~90% value. If it looks subtle at arm's length, push it brighter — at 1:6 scale you cannot over-highlight.

Unifying Warm Glaze (Optional)

FW Crimson Ink thinned 1:8 with water. Very thin pass over: cheeks, nose, ear rims, knuckle backs. Wipe back immediately with a barely-damp flat brush. Reintroduces warm blood-vessel redness at the skin surface, tying the underpainting through to the final layer.

Varnish — Satin

Satin varnish over all skin. Skin at 1:6 has a slight natural sheen — not matte, not glossy. After varnish, apply the boot and leg OSL glaze from the Base & OSL tab to any lower-leg skin surfaces facing the amber LED light.

Special Technique · OSL

Object Source Lighting — Approach and Execution

OSL is applied after base paint is complete and sealed. The goal: every surface that physically faces the amber LED light source receives a tinted glaze; surfaces facing away do not.

1 — Map the light. Before touching any paint, hold a warm amber phone flashlight under the base and photograph which surfaces it actually illuminates. That photograph is your OSL map.

2 — Seal first, always. Matte varnish every surface before any OSL glaze. Transparent glazes on unsealed acrylics lift the layer below.

3 — Build in passes. Start thin (1:6 Transparent Orange + thinner). Near the source: 3–4 passes for saturation. Far from source: 1 pass for a tint only. The falloff drops off fast — exponential, not linear.

4 — Make the strips brighter than the OSL falloff. The LED strips must be the brightest, most saturated element on the base. If the crate underside looks as bright as the strip, the strip isn't bright enough — push it harder.

5 — Tie to the figure. The lowest 15–20mm of the boots must receive the OSL glaze or Faye will visually "float" above the base light. One thin pass on the boot lower panel is enough to anchor her.

✦ TIP: The most common OSL mistake is a flat coat of warm color on every surface — that reads as "painted orange," not "lit by orange." OSL needs clear hotspots near the source and clear dark/cool areas on surfaces facing away. The contrast between glowing and non-glowing sells the effect.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling in Cup

Cause: needle not fully forward when releasing trigger, or paint cup cap too loose. Fix: always release trigger before lifting thumb from air button. Check needle packing nut is snug but not overtightened.

Uneven OSL Coverage

Cause: PSI too high or distance too close creates hard-edged OSL patches. Fix: drop to 12 PSI, increase distance to 18–20cm, and sweep more slowly. OSL mist needs a broad, soft falloff — not precision dots.

Transparent Glaze Lifting Base Layer

Cause: applying Transparent Orange or FW Crimson Ink over unsealed acrylics. Fix: always matte varnish before any transparent glaze pass. Once sealed, transparent glazes cannot lift the layer below.

Brush Skills

Techniques used: fine liner work for hair strands, edge highlighting on metal and costume, wash-and-wipe for OSL glazes, point-source ember effects.

Technique · Fine Liner Work

Hair Strand Highlights

Load a size 0 or 20/0 liner brush with thinned paint (1:2). Roll the tip on a paper towel until only a fine point remains. Press the tip to the sculpted hair root and pull with the sculpted flow direction in a single continuous stroke. If the line splits or blobs, the brush is over-loaded. Wipe back and try again. Commit to each stroke — hesitation creates wobble.

✦ TIP: Keep scrap card next to the figure. Test every liner brush load on card first. Only move to the figure once you see a clean, hair-width line on the card.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Gun, Boots, and Crate Edges

Near-dry brush: load a flat or filbert brush, wipe on paper towel until almost no paint transfers on a second wipe. Hold perpendicular to the edge and drag lightly along it. Paint deposits only on the apex. For straight machined edges (gun slide, crate panel corners): a chisel-edge brush dragged along the edge gives the cleanest line. One direction only — never stroke back and forth.

Technique · Wash and Wipe

Targeted Wash for Recesses

Touch the brush tip to the recess entry point — not to the panel face. Capillary action draws the wash in without flooding the surface. Immediately have a flat brush barely dampened with clean water in your other hand. If the wash spills onto the panel face, wipe back before it dries — most washes start bonding within 60–90 seconds.

Technique · Point Source Glow

Ember and LED Hot Spots

Start with the darkest ring and work inward toward the light source. Each successive ring is smaller than the last. The fluorescent center should be the smallest element — just a dot or short line. The concentric value gradient (dark surround → mid-orange → warm yellow → near-white hot core) reads as actual light even at 2mm scale.

✦ TIP: Fluorescent paints react under UV/blacklight — a fun photography option. Even under normal light they read as unusually vivid, making them ideal for any glowing element you want the eye drawn to first.
