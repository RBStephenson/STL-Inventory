Spike Spiegel — Space cowboy, Jeet Kune Do artist, and the most effortlessly cool bounty hunter in the solar system. His palette is deliberately understated: warm olive skin, dark grey-green hair with silver-tipped strands, a battered brown canvas coat, and a desaturated slate-blue suit. Nothing loud — except the lit cigarette and the gun aimed at your face. Zenithal light from above, warm right-side key light matching the cigarette ember. Priority materials: rough woven coat texture, blued gunmetal, and the cigarette ember OSL.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal — Spike is rugged, world-worn, and masculine. The red zenithal creates natural warmth and exertion flush without the pink softness of the Pinkle. His skin reads as lived-in and sun-touched, not luminous. Method 2B nails this with minimal steps.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → undercoat → base → mid-tone → highlight → optional glaze. Most predictable path.

Easy to correct; works at any skill level Can look flat without strong layering discipline First-time painters, speed priority, anime simplified (2–3 layers, no freckling)

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → Magenta 011 thinned 1:4 all over → Transparent Red / FW Crimson Ink undiluted in recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers needed Too soft/pink for Spike's character energy; better for fair or feminine figures Fair / feminine figures, protagonist heroes, display pieces
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal ✦

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink 513 thinned 1:2 over the full figure → Transparent Red 047 undiluted in recesses → thin flesh layers from zenithal direction only.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; reads as sun-touched and weathered Same zenithal dependency as 2A — poor zenithal = muddy result Rugged male, survivor, world-worn figures. Perfect for Spike.

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin seal → yellow wash (fatty zones) → seal → blue wash (vein zones) → seal → red accent (capillary zones) → final highlight.

Most realistic tonal mapping; three distinct undertone zones Slowest — 3 varnish dry cycles; overkill for this anime-stylized sculpt Historical, portrait, max-realism display. Not ideal for anime style.
No Freckling: Spike is anime-stylized — smooth graphic skin transitions only. No freckling or organic speckling. Bold value steps read better at arm's length for this aesthetic and scale.

Cheek Flush — Optional Warmth

FW Crimson Ink 513 at 1:6 (very thin), fine brush. Lightly glaze over cheek areas and nose tip after skin is dry. Wipe back immediately with a barely-damp brush. Adds the natural capillary flush visible in the reference render without overwhelming the olive tone.

✦ Cigarette — Special Technique

The cigarette is visible in the close-up renders: a white paper cylinder between Spike's fingers, with a glowing orange ember tip. At 1:6 scale this is a small detail — roughly 1–2mm in diameter — but it reads clearly in the reference. The ember adds optional subtle OSL warmth to the nearby fingers.

Selective Varnish

Matte varnish on the paper body and filter. Gloss varnish isolated to the ember tip only — this amplifies the wetness/heat read of the fluorescent orange and makes it pop further at arm's length. Do not gloss the ash zone — matte reads as dead ash correctly.

Red Zenithal Filter — Special Notes
Technique · Filter

FW Crimson Ink as Whole-Figure Skin Filter

FW Crimson Ink 513 at 1:2 dilution (ink:water) is the correct choice for the whole-figure red zenithal filter in Method 2B. Do not use PA Transparent Red 047 here — at 1:2+ dilution it shifts magenta. PA Transparent Red 047 is valid undiluted for localized recess work on the same figure. Using both correctly means: FW Crimson Ink for the broad zenithal pass; PA Transparent Red undiluted for punch-in accent work in the deepest recesses.

✦ TIP: The red zenithal filter should be light — barely visible from arm's length, clearly tinting lit areas warm pink-red when viewed close. If it's covering the white zenithal, you've gone too heavy. Thin it further or reduce pass count.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: airbrush trigger released before needle closes, or excess air pressure relative to paint viscosity. Fix: release the needle before the trigger. Reduce PSI by 3–5 and test. Clean the needle tip — dried paint near the tip causes turbulence.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving too slowly across the surface or too close to it. Effect: dense stripes of color. Fix: keep consistent 10–15cm distance, steady parallel passes at constant speed. Never stop moving while the trigger is open.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: over-thinning or too much PSI at close range. Effect: paint runs down the surface, pools in recesses. Fix: increase distance, reduce PSI, add a small amount of undiluted paint to the cup. Build up in more passes — thin and patient is always correct.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting, feathering, stippling, drybrushing for texture, liner work for hair strands and eyes.

Technique · Drybrushing for Woven Fabric

Drybrushing the Trench Coat Weave

Load a stiff flat brush with near-undiluted paint. Wipe on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers when you stroke it across. Then stroke lightly across the sculpted fabric weave surface — the raised knuckles of the weave catch pigment while the recesses stay dark. This is the primary technique for the coat texture. Key: the brush should feel almost dry. If paint smears into the recesses, your brush is too wet.

✦ TIP: Worn brushes with slightly splayed bristles are excellent for drybrushing textured surfaces — the irregular tip catches more surface area per stroke.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Edge Highlight — Suit & Coat Edges

Load a fine liner or size 0 round brush with thinned highlight mix. Rest the brush belly against the surface and drag along the edge — the tip should trace the exact edge line without going over it. For suit lapels and coat hem: one pass per edge, no cleanup. If it goes wrong, wait for it to dry and correct with the shadow color. Wet correction smears and lifts layers.

Technique · Liner Brush — Hair Strands

Painting Individual Hair Strands

Use a size 00 or 000 liner brush loaded with thinned paint (1:2 to 1:3). Hold the brush at the base of the handle for maximum control. Draw strokes from root toward tip, following the sculpted flow direction. Short controlled strokes — 2–5mm each at 1:6 scale. Work in the direction the hair flows. Do not cross grain. The strand highlight should land on the convex top surface of each sculpted strand cluster.

✦ TIP: Rest your wrist or elbow on the table for stability when doing liner work. Floating wrist = shaky lines. Physical support = confident strokes.
Technique · Eye Painting

Painting Small Eyes at 1:6 Scale

The canonical order for this guide: socket shadow → sclera → iris → pupil → iris highlight → catch-light → gloss. Use the finest brush you own for iris and pupil work — a 20/0 or 30/0 detail brush. Thin your paint to 1:2; too thick and it globulates on the surface. Always work both eyes simultaneously, alternating strokes, so they stay matched. A magnifier loupe or work lamp with magnification arm is extremely useful here.

Technique · Stippling — Ember & Effects

Stippling for Small Effects

For the cigarette ember OSL and any micro-texture work: load a fine round brush, wick off excess on the palette, then tap the brush tip perpendicular to the surface (not stroke — tap). Stippling deposits paint in small dots that read as heat, dust, or texture at arm's length. Used for the OSL tint glaze on fingers adjacent to the ember.
