Salamanders Mk VI Marine — Horus Heresy — The 30k Salamanders scheme is meaningfully different from their 40k counterparts: this is the Legion in the crucible of the Great Crusade and early Heresy, wearing a brighter, more vibrant forest green — not the near-black grimdark shade of the 41st Millennium. The armor should read as rich, saturated green with deep fire-lit shadows, not dark green with highlights. The iconic Mk VI "Beakie" has one of the most satisfying silhouettes in the game — the extended beak visor, ribbed backpack, and distinctive shoulder proportions reward clean edge work. Light source: zenithal from above. Priority: get the armor saturation right and honor every Beakie edge.
✦ 30k vs 40k DIFFERENCE: In the 40k guide, Black Green 057 is the main body color with Green 004 as a highlight layer. Here the relationship flips — Green 004 is the primary armor color and the dark base mix stays in recesses only. This single change is what makes the 30k scheme read as brighter and more vibrant.
✦ BATCH PAINTING: Same principle as the 40k guide — prime all models, paint one step across the whole batch, then move to the next. The speed track sub-tabs use white primer for a brighter Speedpaint result than the 40k dark-grey version.

Recess Shadow — Dark Green Mix

Black Green 057 thinned 1:2, applied ONLY to recesses, panel line areas, undersides of armor plates, and any surfaces facing strongly downward. Step 3 · Airbrush

Legion Green — Main Body (The Key Step)

Green 004 thinned 1:2.5, applied broadly from a zenithal angle to all upper and outward-facing armor surfaces. This is your main Legion color — the color a viewer sees when they look at this marine. Apply with enough coverage that the armor reads as clearly green, not dark green. Leave only the Black Green recess shadows visible in panel lines and deep undercuts. Two to three thin passes. This step is what the 30k scheme lives or dies on.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

White Prime — The Critical Difference

P-003 White Primer, full coverage over all surfaces. Undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle. Full opacity — no dark grey or black showing through. The white primer is the foundation for a bright Speedpaint result. If coverage is partial, the Speedpaint will read unevenly. Two passes at 90° to each other ensures even coverage on Mk VI's ribbed backpack and under-visor areas.

White Primer P-003
Pro Acryl
~95% value — bright prime base for vibrant Speedpaint result
⚠ NOTE: If you used black or dark grey primer on these models already, the Speedpaint approach produces a darker result — which is the 40k scheme. To get the brighter 30k result, you need white prime. Re-prime in white is the solution.
Step 2 · Brush

Absolution Green — Over White

Absolution Green (Speedpaint 2.0) applied undiluted or with a single drop of Speedpaint Medium over all armor. Over white primer, this Speedpaint produces a rich, vibrant forest green — considerably brighter than the dark grey primer version. Apply and leave it. No re-brushing while wet. The paint self-levels and naturally pools in recesses (the small amount of shadow you get for free). Work one panel group at a time, let gravity pull the excess into joints and recesses.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: PSI too high or nozzle cap over-tightened. Effect: paint bubbles back into the cup. Fix: Reduce PSI to 15–18. Finger-tight nozzle cap only. On Mk VI's ribbed backpack, work at lower PSI so paint doesn't blast into the rib recesses unevenly.

Armor Reading Too Dark

Cause: Black Green 057 applied too broadly in Step 2 (should be recesses only). Effect: armor looks like the 40k scheme, not the brighter 30k. Fix: A light zenithal pass of Green 004 over the whole model to lift the mid-tone back up. The green will partially cover the Black Green on flatter surfaces.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: Inconsistent airbrush movement speed or stopping mid-pass. Effect: visible stripes across armor plates. Fix: Keep moving at constant speed. Trigger off before you stop. Multiple thin passes at consistent distance rather than one slow heavy pass.

Technique · Edge Highlighting — Mk VI Specifics

Honoring the Beakie

The Mk VI silhouette is defined by its beak visor, ribbed backpack, and distinctive long curved surfaces. When edge highlighting, treat the following as distinct highlight targets that don't exist on Mk VII: the top ridge of the beak (one long, single confident stroke from left to right); the visor slot upper rim (follow the inner lip of the beak opening); the collar gorget ring where helmet meets chest — a visible circle of armor that benefits from a clean bright edge. The ribbed backpack pipe channels run vertically — the raised ridge of each rib gets a short highlight stroke. At 28mm, you don't need every rib — every second or third one is enough.

✦ TIP: The beak ridge line is the defining edge of the whole model. Nail that single confident stroke and the Beakie looks sharp. Hesitate and wobble on it and nothing else will save the face. Practice the motion on a scrap surface first.
Technique · Drybrushing

Bases and Metals

Drybrushing applies to base texture and metallic surfaces. For ash wastes or volcanic bases: heavy drybrush of the mid-tone, light drybrush of the highlight. For steel TMM: zenithal drybrush with AMP Steel 010 from above — treat the whole metal surface as one drybrush target, not individual elements. The zenithal direction is the same as your light source. Quick and produces good results at 28mm scale.

Technique · Targeted Washing

Panel Lines and Recesses

Apply washes to recesses only — never flood across flat armor surfaces at this scale. Fine brush, load with thinned wash, touch to the recess opening and let capillary action draw the liquid in. Remove the brush before overflow. For the Mk VI ribbed backpack, apply wash into each rib channel individually — a very fine brush is required. Two minutes of patience here produces significantly cleaner results than a flood and mop approach.
