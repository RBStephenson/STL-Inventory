Sheila the Thief — The heart of the party: protective, brave, and luminous. Her palette is deliberately vivid — hot pink against royal purple against copper-auburn hair, all grounded on an autumn stone base. Light source is zenithal from above; shadows go darker and highlights go lighter than feels comfortable. Priority materials: skin (Pinkle Method for the heroic glow), the cloak (largest single surface, value-critical), and leather boots (warm texture contrast). Keep everything saturated and slightly stylized — true to the 1983 animated aesthetic without going flat.

Method Selection
Recommended: Method 2A — The Pinkle Method. Sheila is fair-skinned, feminine, and the emotional core of the party. The Pinkle's magenta underpainting creates warm sub-surface scattering that makes heroic protagonist skin feel lit from within — critical for a display-scale figure where the face reads at close range. The zenithal gives you the value structure for free; flesh layers just confirm color on top.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct at any stage Can read flat without enough layers at 1:6 scale Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, anime/cartoon (2–3 layers, no freckling)

Method 2A — Pinkle ✓ Recommended

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over (thin, transparent) → FW Crimson Ink into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; warmth reads naturally in shadows Poor zenithal = muddy result; harder to correct mid-process Fair / feminine, protagonist / hero figures, display 1:6 / 75mm / busts

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Same as 2A but skip magenta — use FW Crimson Ink (1:2) or Flesh Wash 202 directly over the white zenithal.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; works on darker complexions Same zenithal dependency as 2A; less luminous for fair skin Rugged male, military, survivor, weathered figures

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty zones) → seal → blue wash (vein zones) → seal → red wash (capillary zones).

Most realistic tonal mapping; three distinct undertone zones Slowest — 3 varnish layers and dry times; one wash too thick = muddy Historical, portrait, display max-realism. Not suitable for 28mm or anime.
Freckling / Speckling: After skin is fully sealed, thin Burnt Sienna Expert Acrylic or Red Orange AMP 017 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots, not lines. Target nose bridge, cheeks, shoulders. One pass → step back → assess. Easy to overdo. Sheila's animated reference has clean skin — keep freckling very subtle or omit for a more cartoon-faithful result.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

Full coverage from all angles — no bare resin. 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. This is the shadow anchor for the entire figure; patches of bare resin will break the zenithal later.

Black Primer P-002
Pro Acryl
~5% value — shadow anchor
⚠ NOTE: Never thin primer. Use undiluted at 30+ PSI with a 0.5mm nozzle. Thinning degrades bonding properties and turns it into paint.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal

Heavy from directly above, fading to nothing by the mid-sides and undercuts. 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Top planes — top of head, shoulders, nose bridge, knuckles — should reach near-white. Recesses stay black. Photograph and desaturate before adding any color; you should see a clear value range from near-black to near-white. Fix it now, not during flesh layers.

The Pinkle
Step 3 · Airbrush — The Pinkle

Magenta All Over

Thin Magenta 011 at 1:4 paint:water and apply across all skin in a very thin transparent pass. This tints the zenithal warm and rosy without obscuring the value structure beneath. The black recesses should still show through. Two passes maximum. This layer mimics sub-surface scattering and is what makes the Pinkle Method look luminous rather than chalky.

Magenta 011
Pro Acryl
~20% value — thin transparent pinkle layer
⚠ NOTE: This layer must stay transparent. Too thick and the result goes chalky pink instead of luminous. If you overshoot, let it dry and re-apply a very thin white from above before continuing.
Step 4 · Airbrush

FW Crimson Ink into Recesses

Thin FW Crimson Ink at 1:2 and target deep recesses and flexion points — eye sockets, under the chin, knuckle creases, elbow folds. This deepens shadows with warm organic undertones and pushes value range before flesh goes on. PSI 10–12, 0.2mm nozzle, short bursts. One pass, step back, assess before deciding on a second.

Zenithal Sequence
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime — Full Coverage

P-002 Black Primer undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Apply from all angles — front, back, sides, below. Every surface must be covered. No bare resin. The black is the shadow anchor for every surface on the figure. Missing spots will create value inconsistencies that no amount of later painting can fix.

⚠ NOTE: Never thin primer. Thinning degrades its bonding and purpose. Use undiluted at 30+ PSI with a 0.5mm nozzle only.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Top Down

P-003 White Primer undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Heavy from directly above, fading to nothing by the mid-sides. Never from below. Top planes — head, shoulders, nose — should be near-white. Recesses stay black. The gradient between them is your value map for every subsequent material.

✦ GREYSCALE CHECK: Before adding any color, photograph the primed figure and desaturate. Should read clearly: near-black recesses, near-white top planes, gradient between. If it looks flat, the zenithal isn't strong enough. Fix it now. Color cannot save a bad value structure.

Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spattering

Dried paint on the needle tip causes spitting and spatters. Fix: use a dry brush lightly dipped in airbrush cleaner or thinner to gently wipe the needle tip between passes. Short, gentle strokes. Do not dip the needle in water. Do not let the ferrule enter the airbrush cap. Add 5–10% flow improver to mixes to slow tip dry.

Blowback / Cup Bubbling

Cause: needle not fully forward, paint too thick, or PSI too high for the nozzle. Fix: check the needle seal, thin the mix, and reduce PSI by 3–5. Blowback with Speedpaint 2.0 means the mix is too thin — it activates the self-leveling agent and causes foaming in the cup.

Magenta Pinkle Too Opaque

If the magenta layer obscures the zenithal value structure, you've applied it too thickly. Let dry fully, then re-establish with a very light pass of P-003 White from directly above (zenithal angle only) before continuing with flesh layers. Do not try to paint over a muddy pinkle — it gets worse.

Paint Too Thin / Runs on Large Surfaces

On large surfaces like the cloak, over-thinned paint runs down into folds before drying, creating tide marks. Fix: add more paint to the mix, reduce distance to 8–10cm, and use shorter horizontal passes. Let each pass dry before the next — do not chase a wet gradient on a large surface.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: layering, edge loading, drybrushing, glazing, wet blending (Expert Acrylics), gem recipe, and fine detail for eyes and thigh chain.

Technique · Layering

Thin Layering for Fabric and Skin

Every brush layer in this guide should be thin — 1:1 to 1:2 paint:water minimum. Thin layers are transparent enough to let the airbrushed value structure show through while confirming the color. Stack 2–3 thin layers rather than one opaque coat. Each layer dries in 60–90 seconds at room temperature; patience between layers prevents muddying colors together.

✦ TIP: Test thinness on the back of your hand before applying. The paint should flow off the brush in a thin stream, not sit in a blob. If it runs like water, it's too thin.
Technique · Edge Loading

Fold Edge Highlights on Fabric

For the pink dress and purple cloak, edge-load a flat brush by touching only one edge of the brush's bristle face to the paint. Then drag the loaded edge along a fold ridge in one clean stroke following the fabric direction. The paint deposits on the edge and fades naturally. Reload for each pass — partial brushloads drag unevenly. Used in every fabric highlight step in this guide.

Technique · Drybrushing for Stone

Texture-Revealing Drybrush

Load a flat or fan brush, then wipe it on a paper towel until it leaves almost no mark on the towel surface. Then drag across the stone texture with light horizontal strokes. The raised texture will catch the paint; the recesses will not. Over-loaded brushes smear and obscure detail — when in doubt, wipe more. Drybrush should reveal, not coat.

✦ TIP: The "correct" level of brush dryness is drier than you think. Test on the back of your hand — you should barely be able to see the stroke before applying to the model.
Technique · Glazing for Gems and Skin

Transparent Color Glazing

A glaze is paint thinned to near-watercolor consistency (1:5 to 1:10 paint:water) and applied over a sealed surface to shift the color temperature without obscuring details. Used in this guide for: FW Crimson Ink on skin, Transparent Blue 046 on gems, Brown Wash 201 on leather and stone. Apply, let sit briefly, then wick any pooling in unwanted areas with a clean damp brush. Always seal the surface below a glaze — unsealed layers will lift.

Technique · Fine Detail — Eyes and Small Gems

Eyes and Thigh Chain Work

At 1:6 scale, eye detail is visible and critical. Use a size 000 detail brush with a fine, intact tip. Work one eye, step back to check, then the other — never both wet simultaneously. For catch-light dots: press the brush tip onto the surface and lift immediately (don't drag). For the thigh chain, magnification helps. Steady your hand against the figure stand or your workbench edge for control. If a dot is wrong, immediately wipe with a clean damp brush and retry.

✦ TIP: Keep a piece of dampened tissue on your palette. Between details, briefly touch the brush tip to it to remove excess paint without fully cleaning. Faster than full washes between tiny steps.
✦ GREYSCALE CHECK: At any stage, desaturate a photo of the figure in your phone's editor. It should read clearly in greyscale — visible light source, form, and depth. If it looks flat, values are too compressed. Fix values before correcting color. Value problems cannot be solved by adding more color.
