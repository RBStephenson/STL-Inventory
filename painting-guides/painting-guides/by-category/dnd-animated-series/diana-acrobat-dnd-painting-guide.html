Diana the Acrobat — athletic champion, D&D's most iconic Black heroine. Her deep warm brown skin is the visual centrepiece of the figure: rich, golden-highlighted, and impossible to rush. The gold TMM regalia (headband, collar, arm bands, belt, portal ring) demands strong value contrast — near-black under the bands, near-white specular on every edge. The fur texture reads through directional drybrushing layered over a zenithal base. Keep the fur darker than the skin throughout — the contrast between dark fur and brighter skin highlights is what makes the read work. Light source: zenithal from directly above, offset slightly to the right.

Skin

Deep warm brown — rich, sun-golden highlights on raised planes, cool-dark in recesses. The render inflates values under studio lighting; target the canonical character, not the render preview.

⚠ COLOR ACCURACY FLAG: Shadow Flesh 042 and Warm Flesh 073 are wrong anchors for Diana's complexion — using them produces a light caramel result regardless of underlayers. Dark Flesh 068 is the mid-tone anchor. Highlights shift warm golden (Advanced Flesh Tone S17), never pink or cream. This is a documented failure case — do not deviate from these anchors.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal. Diana is a heroic display figure, which normally favours Method 2A (Pinkle). However, her deep brown complexion means the magenta underpainting would push raised skin planes too pink — particularly visible at the shoulders, cheekbones, and forearms. Method 2B skips the magenta and uses a warm flesh wash instead, creating rich undertone warmth without any pink cast. The result reads as amber-brown glow in the recesses, clean warm-dark tone across the mid planes.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers Tabletop scale, first-time painters, cartoon style

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → red into recesses → thin flesh layers.

Luminous sub-surface scattering Magenta over-pinks deep complexions — wrong for Diana Fair-to-medium complexions, feminine heroic figures
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink glaze over all skin → dark flesh shadow → anchor tone → warm golden highlights.

Rich undertone without pink cast; correct for deep warm complexions Same zenithal dependency as 2A — nail the zenithal before proceeding Deep and warm brown complexions, athletic heroic display figures

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base coat → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty areas: cheeks, forehead) → seal → blue wash (vein zones: temples, wrists, eyelids) → seal → red wash (capillary zones: nose tip, knuckles, lips). Each wash layer wiped back and isolated. Maximum realism through precise undertone mapping.

Most realistic tonal depth achievable; each undertone zone precisely controlled Three varnish layers and dry times; one wash too thick = muddy result; not suitable for 28mm Complex display figures, historical/portrait realism, painters with time to invest. Advanced: combine with Method 2B underpainting for maximum depth.
Freckling: After skin is sealed, thin Dark Golden Brown 062 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Micro-dots only — target shoulders, forearms, nose bridge. For deep brown skin keep it extremely subtle: the freckling should add organic life without reading as a texture change from arm's length. One pass, step back, stop.

Diana's gold elements: headband (with red gem), collar, upper arm bands (both arms), belt (with red gem), and the portal ring on the base. All use the same 1:6 scale gloss-black-base TMM gold recipe. True Metallic Metal — no NMM required for this figure.

✦ VALUE FIRST — GOLD READS FLAT WITHOUT CONTRAST: Even gold needs near-black in deep recesses, true metallic mid-tone on broad planes, and near-white specular on apex edges. Transparent Resin Shaft — Tinting Workflow
Step 1 · Finish

Matte Seal — CRITICAL FIRST STEP

Apply matte varnish over the entire resin shaft before any paint. Bare transparent resin resists water-based acrylic — paint will bead and peel. The matte seal provides a tooth for the Speedpaint to adhere to. Let cure fully (at least 30 minutes).

⚠ NOTE: Do NOT skip this step. Applying Speedpaint directly to unsealed resin will result in the paint pooling, not adhering, and peeling on flex. The matte seal is non-negotiable.
Step 2 · Filter

Dark Tint — Speedpaint 2.0

Army Painter Speedpaint 2.0 — Strong Tone or Grim Black — thinned 1:3 with Speedpaint Medium (not water). Apply in thin passes with a flat brush. Allow to partially self-level between passes. The goal is a dark semi-transparent tint — deep amber-brown or very dark grey-black — that preserves the translucency of the resin shaft while making it read as a dark magical weapon rather than clear plastic.

Speedpaint 2.0 — Strong Tone / Grim Black
Army Painter Speedpaint 2.0
dark semi-transparent tint
✦ TIP: Always use Speedpaint Medium, not water, to thin Speedpaint 2.0 for filter work. Water disrupts the self-levelling chemistry and can cause streaking. Multiple thin passes are better than one heavy pass — you can build depth gradually and stop when satisfied.
⚠ NOTE: Apply over a fully dried, sealed surface only. Speedpaint over unsealed or partially-cured surfaces will lift underlying layers. Do not overwork while wet — the self-leveling agent activates on brush contact and overworking ruins the blend.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: PSI too high for current needle/cup fill level, or cup not fully covered during cleaning. Effect: paint sprays back into cup or up through needle cap. Fix: reduce PSI 5–10 points. Keep cup covered when not actively spraying. Check needle packing nut is not too tight.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving too slowly, inconsistent trigger pressure, or paint too thick. Effect: striped or patchy zenithal coverage. Fix: keep the airbrush moving at a constant speed. Thin further (add 5–10% thinner). Apply multiple light passes rather than one heavy slow pass.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: over-thinned paint, too much trigger at close range, or PSI too high. Effect: paint pools in recesses, runs down vertical surfaces. Fix: add paint back to the mix in small increments. Increase distance 5cm. Reduce PSI. Test on scrap card first — if it runs on the card, it will run on the figure.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: wet blending (Expert Acrylics), controlled layering, directional drybrushing for fur and stone, liner-brush strand painting for hair, and targeted wash placement.

Technique · Wet Blending (Expert Acrylics)

Blending While Paint Is Open

Expert Acrylics stay workable longer than standard acrylics — typically 3–6 minutes depending on humidity and layer thickness. Apply two adjacent colour bands while both are still wet, then use a clean, barely-damp flat brush to blend at the edge. Short perpendicular strokes across the colour boundary gradually intermix the pigments. The key is speed: get both colours down before either dries. If blending starts tearing the paint layer, it has dried — stop, let it cure, and glaze over the top instead.

✦ TIP: Work in small sections. Full-arm blending in one pass is ambitious. Complete one zone (e.g., one shoulder), let it dry, move to the next. The dried zone beneath provides a clean edge to work from.
Technique · Directional Drybrushing

Texture Through Controlled Dry-Brush Direction

For fur and hair textures, the direction of drybrushing defines the material read. Fur tufts: stroke from root toward tip, outward from the garment edge. Hair waves: stroke from root toward tip following sculpted direction. Stone: multi-directional — random edge catches read as fractured mineral surfaces. Load the brush, wipe aggressively on a paper towel until nearly no paint transfers to the towel surface, then apply. Repeat. Each pass should be barely visible — the texture builds through accumulation of many light passes, not one heavy stroke.

✦ TIP: After each drybrushing phase, hold the figure at arm's length and look at the value range — is it reading the material correctly? Fur that's too light loses depth. Stone that's too dark loses texture. Stop the drybrushing when the result reads clearly at arm's length, not when it looks detailed under a magnifier.
Technique · Targeted Wash Placement

Wash as a Precision Tool, Not a Flood

Many steps in this guide call for targeted wash application into recesses only — not flooding the whole surface. Use a fine brush (size 0 or 00), load with wash, touch the tip to the recess or panel line, and let capillary action do the work. The wash flows toward the narrowest point. If you've applied too much and it's pooling on a flat surface, pick it up immediately with a clean dry brush before it dries. Rework is much harder once a wash dries in the wrong place.

Technique · Gem Painting — Dark Base to Bright Specular

Five-Zone Gem Read

The painted gem illusion depends on five distinct value zones across the surface: near-black at the base (deep interior shadow), dark saturated colour in the lower mid-zone, true colour in the upper mid-zone, lighter colour shifted toward warm in the upper arc, and a single white specular dot at upper-left. Each zone must be visibly distinct from the ones adjacent. The dot at the end is what converts a painted oval into a gemstone — it reads as a pinpoint reflection of the light source.
