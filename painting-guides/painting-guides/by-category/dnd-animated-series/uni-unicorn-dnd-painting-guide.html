Uni — A baby unicorn of pure heart and spectacular mane. The painting challenge here is contradiction: a body that must be built dark-first to read as white, and a mane so saturated it reads as living flame. Light source is zenithal with a slight forward tilt. Paint the mane, eyes, muzzle, and horn first — they establish the color story. Paint the body last so the white highlight placement responds to the full composition. Bobby the Barbarian shares this base; his guide lives separately in the D&D series collection.

Hair — Mane & Tail

Uni's mane and tail are the color drama of the entire figure — a fiery, flowing mass of intense orange that should read as living flame against the white body. Strong value contrast and a deliberate color temperature shift from deep red-brown in the shadows to warm yellow at the specular tips are the keys.

✦ THE MANE IS THE DRAMA. Uni's orange mane should hit 70–80% value on light-facing surfaces. If it reads subtle at arm's length, push every stage one step brighter. The color shift: deep red-brown (shadows) → burnt orange (base) → orange (mid) → warm yellow (specular tips). This maps directly onto fire's actual color gradient from base to tip.

Uni's Body — White Without White

The central challenge of the figure. Uni must read as a luminous white unicorn without being painted pure white at the base-coat stage. The technique: build a strong cool blue-grey shadow structure first, layer warm grey mid-tones through it, then push near-pure white only onto the absolute highest planes. The contrast between cool shadows and warm highlights is what your eye reads as white.

✦ WHY "WHITE WITHOUT WHITE": Paint a figure pure white from the start and it reads flat, chalky, formless — all surfaces are equally bright and your eye cannot distinguish shape. Instead: cool blue-grey in the recesses creates a shadow temperature that makes warmer surfaces read as white by contrast. A Heavy Warm White S18 highlight sitting next to a Dark Grey Blue 014 shadow is perceived as bright white. The shadow does the work.

Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spattering

Dried paint on needle tip causes spits. Cause: paint too thick, low humidity, pausing too long. Fix: add 5–10% flow improver. Between passes, remove dried paint from the needle tip with a dry brush lightly dipped in airbrush cleaner — gentle strokes only. Do not dip the needle in water. Do not let the ferrule enter the airbrush cap.

Blowback / Bubbling

Air backflows into the cup while triggering. Cause: trigger activated before needle pulls back, or excess PSI with needle nearly closed. Fix: start air first, then slowly pull back on the needle. Reduce PSI when working at very low needle opening (filter glazing, tint passes).

White Body Reads Grey Overall

After all highlight layers, Uni still reads as grey rather than white. Cause: shadow values are too light, collapsing the contrast between shadow and highlight. Fix: reinforce Dark Grey Blue 014 in recesses before adding more white. Adding more white on top of a flat-value base does not solve the problem.

Paint Too Thin / Runs on Body

White or grey layers run before drying. Cause: over-thinned, or too many passes without dry time. Fix: increase paint fraction. Allow each pass to flash-dry (30–60 seconds) before adding the next. Runs in white paint on the body coat are nearly impossible to hide after drying — work slowly.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: glazing and filter application over sealed surfaces, drybrushing for stone and base, fine liner work for mane strands and recess lines, and wet blending for the Expert Acrylics mane workflow.

Technique · Glazing & Filtering

Glazing — Mane Depth, Body Temperature, Muzzle Warmth

A glaze is paint thinned 1:6 or more — semi-transparent, used to shift color temperature or deepen tonal zones without hiding what's below. Always apply over fully dry and ideally sealed (satin or matte varnish) surface. Flat brush, minimal load, one direction stroke — do not go back over a wet glaze as it will lift. The body temperature filter (Transparent Blue 046 into shadows), the mane red-shift glaze (FW Crimson Ink into curl valleys), and the muzzle nostril depth are all glaze technique applications.

✦ TIP: Apply one thin pass, step back, assess at arm's length, and wait until fully dry before deciding to add a second. A glaze that looks subtle when wet often reads stronger once dry. You can always add; you cannot easily remove.
Technique · Fine Liner — Mane Strands & Recess Lines

Liner Work

Load a size 0 or 000 liner brush fully with thinned paint (1:2 to 1:3). Roll the tip to a point on the palette before each stroke — do this after every 3–4 strokes to prevent splaying. The brush holds paint in the belly and releases gradually through the tip. For mane strands: follow the hair flow direction, stroke in one direction, do not scrub. For recess lines on the horn grooves and leg joints: use capillary pull — touch the tip to the recess edge and let paint flow in under surface tension rather than painting directly.

Technique · Drybrushing — Stone & Base

Drybrushing

Load the brush fully with paint, then wipe repeatedly on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers. The residue deposits only on the highest raised surfaces when dragged lightly. Heavy drybrush (more paint remaining) for broad texture build-up; light drybrush (almost nothing) for final specular tip catches. An old, splayed brush works better for drybrushing than a good one — the frayed fibres catch texture more naturally.

Technique · Wet Blending — Expert Acrylics Mane

Wet Blending

Apply the shadow colour first. While still wet (Expert Acrylics stay open 2–4 minutes on a wet palette), load a clean brush with the mid-tone and pull it across the surface, letting it feather into the wet shadow at the transition edge. One or two passes with a barely-damp clean brush to soften the blend — then leave it. Do not chase the edge repeatedly or you will lift both layers. Work one large curl section at a time; do not attempt the entire mane at once.

Technique · Expert Acrylics — Shadow Anchor

Payne's Grey — The Default Working "Black"

When using Expert Acrylics for any brush-applied shadow on Uni — hooves, muzzle recesses, leg joint lining, or the mane shadow base — reach for Payne's Grey (MEA-013) rather than Carbon Black as the default darkening agent. Payne's Grey is a cool blue-grey with built-in temperature, which integrates naturally into the cool-shadow structure already established by Dark Grey Blue 014 on the body. Carbon Black and Coal Black have zero temperature and can deaden adjacent color when used for shading.

Pure black uses on Uni: pupil dots only (Coal Black 002), and the hoof base undercoat before the warm-brown mid-tone is applied. Everywhere else, shadow = Payne's Grey or a dark cool-grey mix.
