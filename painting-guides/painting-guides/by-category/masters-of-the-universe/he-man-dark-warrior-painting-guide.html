He-Man — CA3D Studios' interpretation strips away the candy-colored toy aesthetic and replaces it with a battle-hardened warrior: deeply bronzed sun-weathered skin, battered dark steel armor, rich leather and earthy pelts, and the iconic Power Sword as the centrepiece. Values first, always. The muscular anatomy must read in greyscale — if the light and shadow on the pectorals, deltoids, and forearms don't create convincing form, no amount of correct color will save it. Light source is zenithal from above; the burgundy fur mantle catches top light while the lower belt and loin fur drop into warm shadow. Priority materials in order: skin → burgundy mantle → steel armor → Power Sword.

Method Selection
Recommended: Method 2B — Red Zenithal. He-Man in this CA3D version is all rugged masculine energy — sun-baked, battle-ready, world-worn. The red zenithal creates natural warmth and a sun-flush that reads immediately as exertion and outdoor living. The mid-tone anchor is Olive Flesh 041 (warm olive/Mediterranean, ~42% value) — correct for this deeply bronzed complexion. Highlights shift warm peachy-amber, not pink or cool-cream.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers 28mm tabletop, first-time painters, speed priority

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → Magenta 011 all over (thinned 1:4) → Transparent Red 047 (undiluted) into recesses → thin flesh layers over top. Do NOT use Flesh Wash (MPA-202) for the pinkle step.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers needed Can over-pink warm or dark complexions; harder to correct Fair/feminine or luminous hero figures, display 1:6 / 75mm
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink 513 thinned 1:2 all over skin → thin flesh layers. Do NOT use PA Transparent Red thinned (shifts magenta). Do NOT use Flesh Wash MPA-202 (too opaque).

Natural sun-warmth and exertion flush; works beautifully on bronzed complexions Same zenithal dependency as 2A — poor zenithal means muddy result Rugged male, military, warrior, sun-weathered figures

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (vein areas) → seal → red wash (capillary zones). Each layer must be sealed. Use Speedpaint Medium (not water) for Speedpaint 2.0 filter variant.

Most realistic tonal mapping — three distinct undertone zones Slowest method — 3 varnish layers and dry times; one wash too thick = muddy Historical, portrait, display max-realism. Combine with Method 2B underpainting for best of both.
Freckling / Speckling: After skin is finished and sealed, thin Burnt Sienna 059 or Petroleum Brown S14 to near-watercolor consistency. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots on exposed surfaces, not streaks. Target shoulders, forearms, upper chest, nose and cheekbones. One pass → step back → assess. Practice on card first — easy to overdo. He-Man's skin should feel weathered, not pristine.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm. Full coverage. No bare resin visible. This is your permanent deep shadow — it lives in every recess and undercut for the rest of the paint job.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~3% value — zero point
✦ TIP: Never thin primer. Apply undiluted. Thinning breaks down its adhesion and turns it into paint with no tooth.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm or 0.3mm nozzle, 30 PSI, 20–25cm. Heavy from directly above (90°), fading to nothing by the equator. The upper surfaces of pectorals, shoulders, tops of forearms, crown of head should be near-white. Under the chin, armpits, underside of arms stay black. This zenithal IS the muscle lighting — get it right before adding any colour.

✦ GREYSCALE CHECK: Photograph the primed figure and desaturate. The muscles must read clearly as lit forms. If it looks flat in greyscale, it will look flat in colour. Fix the zenithal now — not after three layers of skin.
Red Zenithal Underpainting
Step 3 · Airbrush

FW Crimson Ink — Warm Skin Underpainting

FW Crimson Ink 513 thinned 1:2 with distilled water. 0.3mm nozzle, 15 PSI, 10–15cm. Apply all over skin surfaces — one overall pass, even coverage. The crimson should be transparent enough that the zenithal light/dark still reads through it clearly. You are not painting the skin red — you're warming the underlying tonal structure so flesh layers will glow rather than sit flat.

Metals

Three metallic families: cold dark steel (chest plate, wrist/shin armour panels), aged bronze/copper (hardware throughout), and warm gold (ornate chest cross, sword guard details). All use the 1:6 Gloss Black Base Method — you are painting the light that hits metal, not covering the figure in metallic paint.

True Metallic Metal — 1:6 Gloss Black Base Method
✦ VALUE FIRST — METALS: Deep recesses: near-black. Shadow planes: dark metallic. Light planes: bright mid-metallic. Apex edges: near-chrome-white. The gloss black base IS the permanent shadow — any metallic paint applied over it on shadow-facing planes destroys the contrast. Restraint reads as polished metal. Over-coverage reads as toy paint.
Step 1 — Gloss Black Base (All Metal Surfaces)
Step 1 · Brush / Airbrush

Gloss Black Base

Satin Black S39 + a drop of gloss medium (or use a dedicated gloss black lacquer rattle-can). Apply over ALL metal surfaces — armor plates, hardware, sword. This must dry glossy. The gloss surface dramatically increases the reflectivity of VMC applied over it. A matte surface will kill the metallic depth.

Finish

Selective Varnish Pass

Gloss varnish on gem and eye specular only. Satin on leather. Matte on stone base and organic surfaces. Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: needle pulled back before trigger released, or cup overfilled. Effect: paint bubbles back into cup. Fix: always release trigger before pulling needle back. Keep cup no more than 2/3 full. Clean needle tip with a clean dry brush dipped in thinner.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving too slowly, inconsistent trigger pull, or holding airbrush at an angle. Effect: striped or patchy coat. Fix: keep consistent speed and distance. Overlap each pass by 50%. For large skin areas, use smooth arcing motions rather than straight lines.

VMC Spidering

Cause: VMC over-reduced (too much water) or applied over a non-gloss surface. Effect: metallic pigment separates into a spiderweb pattern. Fix: test 1:1 ratio on palette card first. VMC over gloss black resists spidering; over matte surfaces it's prone to it.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: glazing and layering, dry brushing (fur and stone), edge highlighting (leather and metals), liner work (hair strands and beard), fine detail (eyes, gem, sword engraving).

Technique · Glazing

Thin Layering and Glazing

A glaze is paint thinned to near-transparency — 1:4 to 1:8 — applied over a dried layer to shift its colour or value without obscuring it. The key is multiple thin passes rather than one semi-opaque coat. Each pass adds depth; going too thick in one pass creates a streaky tide-mark that's difficult to fix. Load brush fully, then remove most paint on a paper towel before touching the figure — the brush should feel almost dry but still flow freely.

✦ TIP: Test every glaze on the back of your hand first. If it leaves a visible stain after one stroke, it's thinned correctly. If you can see the brush stroke shape, thin it more.
Technique · Drybrushing

Drybrush for Furs, Stone, and Bone

Load a flat brush (old stiff brushes work best), then wipe on paper towel until almost nothing transfers. Then sweep lightly across the textured surface — the bristles skip over recesses and deposit paint only on raised points. For fur: follow the fur strand direction. For stone: random angular sweeps. The key is "less than you think" — the most common drybrushing mistake is too much paint on the brush. If you can see brushstrokes, remove more paint.

✦ TIP: Drybrush in 3–4 progressively lighter passes with increasing value. The first pass builds the broad mid-tone; the last pass is almost nothing, just catching the absolute tips. This layered approach reads far more three-dimensional than a single heavy drybrush.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Edge Highlighting for Leather and Armour

Edge highlighting uses a thinned paint on a fine brush — drag it along a raised edge or corner, depositing a thin bright line. Load the brush, wipe to a fine point, then drag slowly along the edge with consistent pressure. The line should be thinner than it looks at normal working distance — zoom out and check. For leather: the edge highlight sits on the absolute corner of leather folds, not on the face. For armour: the edge line is where two armour faces meet.

Technique · Liner Brush — Hair Strands

Liner Brush Work for Hair

A liner or rigger brush holds a lot of thinned paint in a fine pointed tip. Thin paint to 1:2 to 1:3 — it needs to flow freely off the tip. Pull the brush from near-root toward tip in a single confident stroke following the sculpted hair direction. Hesitating mid-stroke creates a wobble. Paint both hair and beard using these strand-direction strokes. Rotate the figure to always pull toward yourself — never try to push a liner brush away from you.
