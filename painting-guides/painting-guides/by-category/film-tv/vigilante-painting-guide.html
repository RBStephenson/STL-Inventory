Vigilante — Adrian Chase channeled through ZEZ Studios' "New Era" trooper: a battle-worn tactical operator in matte black armor cut with electric cyan panels and crisp white chevrons. This is a hybrid build — the render's silhouette and cyan accents stay intact, but the helmet is pushed toward show-accurate Vigilante: a white crest over a cyan visor band and a hot red eye strip. Light source is zenithal from above with a faint warm bounce from the rubble below. Values first: the armor is mostly low-value, so contrast lives in the white chevrons, the cyan panels, and the chrome blade. Keep the gore restrained — a little blood on the blade edge and a few rubble spatters tells the story without tipping into camp.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Air pushing back through the paint cup — usually a loose nozzle or a clogged tip. Bubbling froths the paint and spatters on the next trigger pull. Check the nozzle is seated, clear the tip, and don't cap the air outlet with a finger to "test."

Cyan Going Dull Over Black

Turquoise sprayed straight over black armor reads muddy and dark. Lay a light ground (Bright Neutral Grey or white) under the cyan first, then build the Turquoise in thin passes — the light base is what makes the color sing.

VMC Metallic Looks Grainy

Over-thinned or sprayed too far away, metallic pigment dries before it hits and lands rough. Move in to 8–10cm, thin closer to 1:1, and keep passes light and wet-looking. A gloss black ground also smooths the read.

Spatter Too Coarse

Blood spatter via airbrush blobs instead of fine dots when PSI is too high or paint too thick. Drop to 8–12 PSI, thin the X27/red well, and pull back so droplets are fine. Always test on scrap and mask the white chevrons first.

Brush Skills

The brush carries the edge highlights, the white chevrons, the chipping, and the blood — the details that make this figure read as battle-worn rather than freshly molded.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Crisp Plate Edges

The single most important brush technique on this figure. Load the brush, wipe it to near-dry on a towel, and drag the side of the tip along each armor edge so only the raised lip takes paint. Work in thin successive passes — Neutral Grey 075, then a finer Bright Neutral Grey 045 on the sharpest corners. A wet brush floods the bevel; a near-dry one gives the clean line that defines every plate.

✦ TIP: Brace your painting hand against your other hand or the bench edge. Edge lines live or die on a steady, supported stroke.
Technique · Liner Stripes

White Chevrons & Stripes

Use a quality liner brush with a fine point and slightly thinned paint (it should flow but hold a line). Pull each chevron stroke in one confident motion rather than scrubbing — start at one end and lift off at the other. Build coverage with the grey→warm-white→specular hierarchy; never start at pure white.

✦ TIP: If an edge wobbles, let it dry fully, then re-cut the boundary with the black armor color. Correcting from the dark side is far easier than chasing a ragged white edge while wet.
Technique · Sponge Chipping

Scattered Paint Chips

Tear a nub of blister-pack foam, pick up a little paint, blot almost all of it off, then stipple lightly. The first dab is always too heavy — it's the second and third that leave the fine, irregular chips. Layer a smaller, darker chip inside the lighter ones for the illusion of depth into the armor.

Technique · Candy Glaze (FW Crimson Ink)

Glowing Red Without the Magenta Trap

For a richer glaze on the visor band or fresh-blood tint, reach for FW Crimson Ink rather than thinned PA Transparent Red, which shifts toward magenta when diluted. Glaze Crimson Ink in thin layers over a sealed red base to deepen and unify it while keeping it transparent and luminous.

⚠ NOTE: Never thin PA Transparent Red for a glaze on this figure — it turns magenta. FW Crimson Ink is the substitute for any thinned/glazed transparent red.
