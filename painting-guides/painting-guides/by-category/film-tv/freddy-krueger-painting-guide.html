Freddy Krueger — Horror's dream demon offers some of the most exciting surface variety in any 1:6 figure: charred and massively textured burned skin; a dirty red-and-olive wool sweater; cracked aged leather; razor-steel blades; and an industrial drain-grate base dripping with blood. Value contrast is extreme — the skin stays dark overall (compressed 5–74% range) because burned tissue does not reflect like smooth skin. Primary light source is zenithal from above, with an optional OSL pass from the base glowing upward in red-orange. Prioritize texture drama and value range over color precision — at arm's length, horror reads through contrast and surface variety.

Skin

Severely burned and scarred — dark, rough, massively textured scar tissue with mottled red-browns, deep recesses, and a compressed value range. The most complex and rewarding surface on this figure.

⚠ VALUE NOTE: The specular sits at ~74% — far below a healthy figure's 90%+ peak. Burned tissue is rough and matte; it diffuses light rather than specularly reflecting it. Push dark shadows hard (near-black in deep recesses) but keep the highlight ceiling deliberately lower than you normally would. If the specular looks too bright, the skin reads as healthy — not burned.
Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal, Adapted for Burned Skin. The red zenithal creates natural underlying warmth suggesting raw flesh beneath the charred surface. The key adaptation for this figure: highlights are built primarily through drybrushing rather than layered glazes, because the extreme 3D-print texture of the sculpt does all the work when a dry brush drags across it. Washes define deep recesses; drybrush builds highlights — faster and more satisfying than attempting smooth glazed layers over rough texture.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Glazed layers alone struggle to capture extreme texture — drybrush adaptation required for best results First-time painters, speed priority, tabletop scale
★ Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → Transparent Red 047 all over → dark charred base coat → drybrush value build → deep recess washes.

Natural raw-flesh warmth from beneath; sculpt texture does the work under a dry brush Zenithal quality is critical — poor coverage will show through every layer above it All rugged, burned, textured, and weathered figures at 1:6 scale

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin seal → yellow fatty zones → seal → blue vein zones → seal → red capillary zones.

Maximum realism; distinct undertone zone mapping Three varnish layers; Freddy's rough texture fights smooth tinting; overkill for a horror figure Portrait, historical, max-realism — not ideal for severely burned skin
No Traditional Freckling — Wound Spatter Instead: Freddy's skin is too heavily damaged for standard freckle-dot texture. The 3D sculpt provides its own micro-texture. After skin is matte-sealed, thin Bold Pyrrole Red 003 to near-watercolor (1:10). Drop PSI to 8, hold airbrush 3–5cm from the deepest wound zones — nasal cavity perimeter, around the eye sockets, and exposed skin creases. Short trigger bursts only. This reads as weeping tissue rather than freckle speckling. Practice on card first.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer, undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Full coverage from all angles — no bare resin visible. This black base remains in the very deepest texture recesses and anchors all shadow values. Freddy's burned skin has near-black pits between scar mounds — the primer does that work for free.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~3% value — absolute shadow anchor; undiluted
⚠ NOTE: Do NOT thin primer. Use undiluted at 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle. Thinning destroys primer adhesion and surface-prep properties.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Top Down

P-003 White Primer, undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Heavy from directly above. White coats the tops of the skull, raised scar ridges, brow, nose bridge, chin, and shoulder tops. Sides of the face and neck receive 30–50% coverage. The brim underside, jaw undersides, and deepest pits remain fully black. Quality of this zenithal determines everything that follows.

P-003 White Primer
Pro Acryl
~95% value — top-plane light; undiluted
✦ TIP: Photograph and desaturate after this step. Confirm a strong gradient from near-black in recesses to near-white on top planes. If it looks flat or uniformly grey, add more white zenithal before proceeding. Fix value in greyscale now — color cannot save a flat zenithal.

Texture Build — Drybrush Sequence
Step 5 · Brush — Drybrush

First Drybrush — Burned Flesh Base

Burnt Sienna 059, near-undiluted on a wide flat brush, loaded then wiped almost completely dry on a paper towel. Drag lightly across the entire skin surface — the raised scar texture peaks catch color immediately. This is the first layer that reads as "damaged flesh" rather than shadow. Work from top down; more coverage on zenithal-lit zones. Aim for ~40% of the skin surface catching this pass.

Burnt Sienna 059
Pro Acryl
~38% value — damaged flesh texture base; near-undiluted drybrush
✦ TIP: If you can hear bristles making a dry scraping sound against the surface, the paint load is correct. If you hear nothing, add a touch more paint. If you see pooling lines, wipe more off. Load → wipe → test on paper → figure.
Step 6 · Brush — Drybrush

Second Drybrush — Raw Flesh Mid-Tone

Dark Warm Flesh S08, near-undiluted, same technique. Less coverage than Step 5 — concentrate on the most raised texture peaks: brow ridges, nose, chin, cheekbone mounds, knuckles, shoulder tops. Reads as raw living flesh visible beneath the burned crust. Approximately 20–25% of the skin surface catches this layer.

Dark Warm Flesh S08
Pro Acryl
~55% value — raw flesh mid-tone; raised peaks only

Third Drybrush — Specular Peak Highlights

Warm Flesh 073, near-undiluted, even less paint on the brush. Absolute highest texture peaks only: top of the nose ridge, highest cheekbone bumps, crown of the skull, apex of major scar mounds on the forehead. Roughly 5–8% of the skin surface total. The contrast between this and the dark base is what makes the texture read from across the room.

Warm Flesh 073
Pro Acryl

Step 8 · Wash

Deep Recess Wash

Brown Wash 201, undiluted, applied with a fine brush directly into recesses — deepest furrows between scar mounds, eye sockets, nasal cavity, mouth corners, and under the chin. Do not flood the entire face. Work in small sections; let capillary action pull the wash in. Place and leave — do not brush back and forth. Wipe any pooling on raised surfaces immediately with a clean damp brush.

Brown Wash 201
Pro Acryl
recess shadow — deepens contrast between drybrush peaks and furrow floors

Raw Wound Accent Glaze

Thin Transparent Red 047 (1:6) and glaze selectively into zones suggesting living tissue — nasal cavity perimeter, inner corners around the exposed eye area, deep skin creases, any visible wound edges. Build in 2–3 passes; seal with matte varnish between passes to avoid lifting. Reads as fresh capillary tissue distinct from the overall charred-brown surface.

Step 10 · Finish

Skin Varnish — Matte

Matte varnish over all skin. Burned scar tissue is matte and dry — no satin, no gloss on the skin. Contrast between matte skin, satin leather glove, and gloss blades is essential for material differentiation at display distance. After matte seal, apply optional wound spatter (see Freckling Note above) if desired.

Eyes & Hat

Sunken, jaundiced eyes peering from deeply shadowed sockets; a battered dark-gray felt fedora with wide black grosgrain band — heavily abused and stained by decades of dream-stalking.

Eyes
Step 1 · Brush

Deep Socket Shadow

Before touching the eyeball, reinforce the socket with Coal Black 002 thinned 1:2 glazed around the entire socket perimeter. Freddy's eyes are deeply recessed — the socket shadow frame is as important as the eyeball itself. This dark surround makes the eyes read as sunken and menacing.

Sclera — Jaundiced White

Do NOT use bright white. Mix Warm Grey 074 with Yellow Ochre 038 (4:1) thinned 1:1. Apply to the whites of the eyes. This yellowed, unhealthy sclera is far more appropriate for a dream demon who has been burned and dead for decades. Pure white sclera looks too fresh and vital.

Iris — Amber Brown

In the original films, Freddy's eyes appear brown with a slightly yellowish-amber quality in close-up shots. Golden Brown 017 thinned 1:1, applied to the iris area. Two coats for full opacity. Leave a dark perimeter at the iris edge — this separation implies depth even at 1:6 scale.

Pupil + Iris Rim

Eye Highlight Dot

A single tiny dot of Heavy Warm White S18 at the upper-left of each iris. Load a fine brush, wipe to near-dry on paper towel, then place a micro-dot. Keep it restrained — a small glimmer, not a bright spark. Freddy's jaundiced eyes should not twinkle.

Sweater

The iconic red-and-olive-green striped wool sweater — dirty, worn, with tattered edges. The color combination was reportedly chosen from a 1982 Scientific American article on the most visually discomforting color pairing: red and green at nearly equal value levels but maximally opposed in hue.

✦ COLOR ACCURACY: The original sweater's red is a medium-bright red — not burgundy, not orange-red, not Christmas red. Bold Pyrrole Red 003 is the correct base. The olive green is a muted, slightly warm olive — not bright yellow-green, not army drab. Camo Green 020 with Yellow Ochre 038 hits the target. Both colors should sit at nearly the same value (~45–55%) while being maximally opposed in hue — that is exactly the unsettling visual combination. Both should read slightly dirty and de-saturated from years of wear.

OSL Technique — Red Upward Glow
Technique · Airbrush

OSL — Light from Below

Object Source Lighting simulates a visible light source casting light onto the figure. For this base, the light source is inside the glowing red ring, pointing upward. To paint OSL correctly: (1) Seal all figure surfaces first. (2) Hold the airbrush below and aim upward at a steep angle. (3) Build very gradually — multiple passes at extreme thinning (1:4 to 1:6). (4) The OSL should be strongest near the source (boots, base of pants) and fade to almost invisible at mid-calf and above. (5) The sweater hem, glove palm, and hat brim underside receive only the faintest outer glow — almost a warm tint rather than a visible color.

✦ TIP: OSL is always easier to add than remove. Start with 3–4 passes at 1:6 thin and step back to evaluate before increasing intensity. The effect is correct when it reads as "something is glowing below" at arm's length without looking like painted-on color up close.
⚠ NOTE: The OSL should be monochromatic red — do not add orange or yellow. Real red light sources cast red-tinted light, not orange. Bold Pyrrole Red 003 + Transparent Red 047 is the correct palette for this effect.
Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spattering

Dried paint on needle tip causes spitting and spatters. Cause: paint too thick, humidity too low, or pausing too long between passes. Fix: add 5–10% flow improver. Between passes, remove dried paint from the needle tip using a dry brush lightly dipped in airbrush cleaner — gentle strokes. Do not let the ferrule enter the airbrush cap and do not dip the needle tip in water.

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: paint dried in the nozzle tip creating a partial blockage; trigger pulled without enough air first. Fix: always open air before opening the needle. If bubbling occurs, flush the cup with cleaner, run cleaner through at high PSI, then re-thin the paint. Blowback often means PSI is too low or paint is too thick.

OSL Looks Like Paint, Not Light

Cause: OSL color applied too thickly, or the angle was too perpendicular (painting the surface rather than grazing it). Fix: seal with matte varnish, then begin again with a much thinner pass (1:8) from a lower angle. OSL should graze across the surface from below — not hit it head-on. The glow is most convincing when it is barely visible on its own and only reads in context.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: insufficient airbrush movement, trigger inconsistency, or paint too thick. Fix: thin the mix slightly, increase movement speed, and overlap each pass by 50%. Keep the airbrush moving before and after triggering. For smooth gradients (like the OSL fade), use a figure-8 motion sweeping the airbrush upward rather than a straight-line pass.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: drybrushing (primary for skin, hat, sweater, grate), glazing (wound accents, OSL outer glow, blood effects), wet blending (Expert Acrylics sub-tabs), and pin washing (metal recesses).

Technique · Drybrushing

Drybrushing — Texture Reveal

Load a wide flat brush with near-undiluted paint, then wipe on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers to the towel. Drag the nearly-dry brush across the textured surface — only the raised peaks catch color. This is the primary highlight technique for Freddy's skin, hat, sweater, and base grate. Heavy-body Expert Acrylics hold even better than dropper-bottle paints for this technique. Technique cues: if you can hear the dry bristle scraping the surface, the load is correct. If you see streaks or pooling, wipe more paint off. Build value in multiple passes — three drybrush passes at increasing value is better than one heavy pass.

✦ TIP: Keep separate brushes for drybrushing — the technique ruins brush shape over time. Old worn flat brushes work better than new ones. Stiff synthetic flats with a bit of spring are ideal.
Technique · Glazing

Glazing — Selective Color and Value Shift

A glaze is paint thinned to near-watercolor (1:4 to 1:10) applied to shift color or value in a specific zone without hiding the texture or layers beneath. In this guide, glazing is used for: wound accent red on skin, blood drip wet effect, OSL outer glow on the figure, and leather highlight. Key rule: one glaze pass is always weaker than you expect. Build to the final result in 2–3 passes, matte-sealing between passes when one glaze builds on another to prevent lifting. Never try to achieve the final result in one heavy glaze — it will pool and look patchy.

✦ TIP: Load the brush, remove excess on a paper towel, then apply with a gentle pulling motion rather than scrubbing. Scrubbing activates earlier layers even after they are dry.
Technique · Pin Wash

Pin Washing — Recess Definition

A pin wash uses capillary action to pull thinned wash into narrow recesses — grate channels, blade fullers, skin furrows, hat fold lines. Load a fine-tipped brush, touch the tip to the recess entrance, and let the wash flow in under capillary tension. Do not scrub back and forth — place the brush tip at the opening and watch the wash travel. Clean any spills on raised surfaces immediately with a slightly damp clean brush before the wash dries. This technique is used for: blade recess shadow, grate dark recesses, hat fold grime, skin furrow depths.

Technique · Wet Blending

Wet Blending — Expert Acrylics

Expert Acrylics stay workable for 10–15 minutes in normal humidity — long enough to blend two colors while both are still wet. Apply the base color, then immediately pick up the shadow or highlight color on a second brush and pull it into the wet base color where you want the transition. A clean damp brush feathers and smooths the edge. Used in this guide for: leather shadows into Burnt Umber, sweater shadows into Pyrrole Red and Chrome Oxide Green. If the base dries before you reach it, re-wet with a damp brush before pulling the second color in.

✦ GREYSCALE CHECK: At any point during painting, desaturate a photo of the figure in your phone's editor. It should read clearly in greyscale — visible light source from above, strong form, and depth. For Freddy specifically: the skin should read as a dark, textured surface with visible peaks and deep pits. If it looks uniformly grey or flat, values are too compressed. Fix values before adjusting color.
