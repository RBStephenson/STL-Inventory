Homelander — America's most powerful (and most dangerous) superhero. The figure demands conviction: a deep, saturated periwinkle-blue suit that reads navy at a distance but rewards close inspection with sculpted texture, warm gold chain trim gleaming against it, and boots and cape in deep crimson leather. Three swappable heads offer genuine choice — but the laser eyes head is the showstopper, and the OSL on it should make the painted version feel like it could level a city block. Values-first throughout: the suit shadow must read near-black in recesses so the blue mid-tone registers as the true color, not a shadow. Light source: zenithal from directly above, with bilateral red OSL for the laser head.

Skin

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal — Homelander is a dominant male figure; his jaw, brow, and cheekbones should read as angular and commanding. The red zenithal underpainting gives him that natural masculine warmth and skin tension without the ethereal glow of Method 2A. If you prefer a more idealized, "lit from within" heroic look for the smiling head, Method 2A is a valid alternative.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, speed priority

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → Transparent Red 047 into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers; heroic glow Poor zenithal = muddy result; harder to correct Fair / feminine / heroic display — valid alternative for this figure's smiling head
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → Flesh Wash 202 all over (replaces magenta) → thin flesh layers built up from zenithal.

Natural masculine warmth and tension; works well for commanding angular features Same zenithal dependency as 2A Rugged / dominant male figures — Homelander's defining read

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (vein areas) → seal → red wash (capillary zones).

Most realistic tonal mapping; three distinct undertone zones Slowest — 3 varnish layers and dry times; one wash too thick = muddy Portrait / display max-realism. Excellent combined with Method 2 underpainting.
No Freckling: Homelander's appearance is fastidiously controlled — smooth, unblemished, almost too perfect. Skip the freckling pass. Any skin texture comes from value transitions, not surface speckling.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm distance. Full coverage — no bare resin showing through. All three heads primed at the same session.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~3% value — full black base
✦ TIP: Prime all three heads at once. Hang them if possible — rotate to catch undercuts on chin, behind ears, under brow shelf. Any missed spots become muddy skin shadows later.
⚠ NOTE: Never thin primer. It breaks down adhesion and defeats the purpose entirely. Use undiluted at 30+ PSI with the 0.5mm nozzle only.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal

P-003 White Primer undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Spray from directly above — heavy white on top planes (crown of head, brow shelf, top of nose, cheekbones, top of hands). Fade to nothing by the undercut zones under the chin and orbital ridge. The black should survive in all recesses and undercuts completely untouched.

✦ TIP: The quality of this zenithal determines the quality of the entire skin result for Method 2B. Before adding any flesh, photograph the primed head and desaturate it in your phone. It should already read as a convincing greyscale portrait with clear light direction. If it looks flat, adjust before proceeding.
Red Zenithal Underpainting (Method 2B)
Step 3 · Airbrush

Flesh Wash Filter — Red Zenithal

Pro Acryl Flesh Wash 202 thinned 1:4 with water. 0.3mm nozzle, PSI 15, 15cm. Apply over the entire skin surface — all of it, not just recesses. The warm wash tints the zenithal while letting the white and black structure show through. Multiple thin passes is better than one heavy one. Build until skin reads as uniformly warm-toned but the zenithal value structure remains clearly visible underneath.

Flesh Wash 202
Pro Acryl
~30% value — warm zenithal tint
✦ TIP: This is the "warmth layer" — it's creating the impression of blood just under the surface. Don't thin it past 1:5 or it goes transparent and loses the effect. The goal is a warm amber-red tint over the whole head, not a wash that pools in recesses.
Base Coat & Shadow
Step 4 · Airbrush

Shadow Base — Deep Recess Anchor

Shadow Flesh 042 thinned 1:3, 0.3mm nozzle, PSI 15. Apply into the deepest recesses — nasolabial folds, under chin, orbital depths, behind ears, between fingers. This is the darkest skin color — not your shadow anchor (that's Payne's Grey for deepest contact zones), but the darkest skin tone that still reads as flesh.

Shadow Flesh 042
Pro Acryl

Step 5 · Airbrush

Mid-Tone Base — True Skin Color

Warm Flesh 073 thinned 1:3, 0.3mm nozzle, PSI 15, 12cm. This is Homelander's actual skin color. Lay it across most of the face and hands — it should cover everything except the deepest shadow zones. Build in 2–3 thin passes rather than one heavy coat. The zenithal value structure must remain visible through it. After this step, the top planes should be reading lighter (zenithal showing through) and recesses darker (shadow flesh showing through).

Warm Flesh 073
Pro Acryl
~55% value — mid-tone anchor
✦ TIP: This is the most important step. At arm's length, Warm Flesh 073 is the color people will perceive as "his skin." Everything else serves to give that color depth. Don't rush it.
Highlights
Step 6 · Airbrush

Primary Highlight — Raised Planes

Focus on the uppermost light-catching planes: brow ridge crest, cheekbone apex, nose bridge top, chin forward face, tops of hands/knuckles. This highlight should read approximately 65–70% value — distinctly lighter than the mid-tone but not yet specular. Keep the spray tight and controlled. Multiple thin passes — this is not a full-face coat, it's targeted illumination.

Step 7 · Brush

Secondary Highlight — Edge Loading

Bright Shadow Flesh S41 thinned 1:2. Tiny amount on a fine round brush, wiped almost dry on the palette edge. Draw along the absolute apex of brow ridge, cheekbone, nose bridge, and chin edge. This should read approximately 80–85% value — getting close to specular territory. If it looks too subtle from arm's length, it's not bright enough. Push it further than feels comfortable.

✦ TIP: Load the brush fully, then wipe it on a piece of paper until almost no paint transfers. That near-dry state gives you precise control for these fine lines. Feather the outer edge of each stroke into the mid-tone with a clean damp brush.
Step 8 · Brush

Specular — Final Catch-Light

Bold Titanium White 001 undiluted, finest liner brush. A single tiny dot or thin dash on the absolute hottest point of each raised plane: tip of nose, center of lower lip, peak of each cheekbone, center of brow ridge between the eyes. This is the only application of pure white in the entire skin recipe. Keep it disciplined — one small precise mark, not painted-on coverage.

Bold Titanium White 001
Pro Acryl
~98% value — specular point only
⚠ NOTE: This is the ONLY application of pure white. Using it as a general highlight produces chalky, dead-looking skin. One dot per zone, maximum.
Step 9 · Glaze / Filter

Unifying Warm Glaze

FW Crimson Ink 513 heavily thinned at 1:10 with water. Apply over cheeks, nose tip, ear rims, and knuckles with a flat wash brush — let it pool very lightly in these zones and wick away from raised surfaces. This adds the subtle capillary warmth that makes fair skin look convincingly alive rather than painted. One pass only — step back and assess before adding more.

FW Crimson Ink 513
FW Acrylic Inks
thinned 1:10 — warmth glaze only
✦ TIP: Don't use Pro Acryl Transparent Red 047 for this step — it shifts magenta when thinned. FW Crimson 513 stays true crimson at any dilution. Test on a scrap card first — at 1:10 it should be barely visible as a tint, not a wash.
Step 10 · Finish

Seal — Satin Varnish

Seal skin with Pro Acryl Gloss Varnish MPAM-003 thinned 1:1 in water, or a dedicated satin varnish. Skin should have a slight sheen — not dead matte, not wet gloss. This also protects the skin before the eye painting and the laser-eyes OSL work.

✦ TIP: Do all three heads at this stage, even if only painting one at a time. The sealed surface is much more forgiving for corrections than bare paint.

Metals

Warm gold throughout — collar chain bands, cuff chain bands, shoulder armor pieces, belt buckle eagle, and boot eagle emblems. All use the 1:6 scale Gloss Black Base method: the black base acts as permanent shadow, metallics are painted only where light would actually strike.

True Metallic Metal (TMM) — 1:6 Scale Gloss Black Base Method
✦ VALUE FIRST: You are not covering metal surfaces in gold paint. You are painting the light that hits gold. Leave the black base fully intact in shadow zones — restraint reads more convincingly than full coverage.
Step 1 · Brush

Gloss Black Base — All Metal Surfaces

Vallejo Glossy Black 70.861, apply over every metal surface — chain links on collar and cuffs, shoulder armor plates, belt buckle assembly, boot eagle emblems. Don't thin it much — just enough to flow smoothly (1.5:1 paint:water). The gloss surface is the key: it creates a mirror-like ground so the metallic layers over it reflect maximally. Let fully cure before proceeding (30+ minutes).

Glossy Black 70.861
Vallejo Model Color
~3% value — gloss black permanent shadow base
✦ TIP: The glossy surface matters here. Don't substitute matte black. The metallic layers rely on the reflective ground beneath them to achieve depth and pop. Matte black base produces a dead, flat metallic result.

Chain Links — Mid Gold Layer

Don't paint the lower half or the interior curve where the link connects. The gloss black should survive everywhere the light doesn't hit. Multiple thin passes build more gold where needed. Target: roughly the upper 60% of each link's surface area.

OSL — Laser Eyes

For the laser eyes head only. The glow is bilateral — both eyes simultaneously — with the strongest OSL cast on the nose bridge between them, spreading across the inner cheeks and brow shelf. The eyes themselves should look white-hot at the core, intensifying to vivid red, then crimson at the outer iris. The face around them reads in red-shifted skin tones. This is the most complex technique in this guide — take it slow, in thin passes, and photograph between passes.

OSL Airbrush Technique
OSL Technique

Bilateral Glow Cast

The laser eyes head has two simultaneous light sources. Hold the airbrush directly in front of the head at eye level — not from above. The spray should hit the nose bridge, inner cheeks, and the underside of the brow from a forward direction. Rotate the head slightly left and right (a few degrees) to ensure the glow cast is symmetric. At ultra-thin dilution (1:8 to 1:10) the glow should build through 4–6 cumulative passes before it reads as clearly visible. Don't rush — each pass is barely visible; they accumulate.

✦ TIP: Mask the hair before OSL work with a small piece of blu-tack or masking tape — a red-shifted helmet of hair is hard to reverse. Keep the glow cast to the face only.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: Trigger pulled too far back before needle moves forward, or paint too thick. Effect: Paint bubble at nozzle splatters back onto figure. Fix: Press trigger down first (airflow on), then ease forward gently. If it persists, thin paint more — 1:4 is minimum for detail work.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: Stopping or starting while trigger is depressed over the figure, or inconsistent distance. Effect: Dark spots or stripes in the coverage. Fix: Always begin and end each pass off the figure. Keep distance consistent. For large flat areas (suit), use sweeping passes that start and finish beyond the edge.

Paint Too Thin — Runs or Pools

Cause: Over-thinned paint (especially washes applied with airbrush). Effect: Paint runs into recesses uncontrollably. Fix: For suit washes, apply by brush — let capillary action do the work. Airbrush glazes at 1:4 or thicker; never thin past 1:6 for colour work.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge loading for piping and leather highlights, liner brush strand painting for hair, OSL glow ring placement, and glazing for skin warmth and leather unification.

Technique · Edge Loading

Edge Loading for Piping & Leather

Load a flat or chisel brush normally, then wipe one face of the brush almost dry on the palette edge. Touch the wiped side to the surface — only the loaded outer edge makes contact with the raised geometry, depositing a controlled line of paint. Used here for the suit piping highlights and leather boot edge highlights. The key is keeping consistent pressure and working with the direction of the seam or edge rather than perpendicular to it.

✦ TIP: Practice edge loading on a piece of card before the figure. The brush should feel almost empty — then test on the card to confirm only the very edge leaves a mark. If the whole brush face prints, wipe more.
Technique · Fine Liner Strands

Hair Strand Painting

Load a fine liner or 000 round brush fully with thinned highlight colour. Wipe to a very fine tip on the palette. Work in the direction of the sculpted hair grain — pull strokes from root toward tip, following the flow. For silver-blonde hair, strokes should be lighter (near-white) and spaced irregularly to look natural rather than machined. Every few strokes, check the overall read from arm's length — individual strands shouldn't read as lines, they should merge into a realistic hair texture.

Technique · OSL Spot Work

Eye Glow Ring Brush Control

The Fluorescent Red → FW Crimson outer ring on the laser eyes requires steady, precise brush control on very small geometry. Rest your brush hand against the figure's base or your desk for physical stability. Use your other hand to hold the head steady against a light-stable mount (or a cork). Work with a magnifier if available. The fluorescent inner and crimson outer areas should have a visible gradient — not a hard line — so the glow reads as emitted light rather than applied paint.

Technique · Thin Glazing

Unifying Glazes

Glazes are paints or inks thinned to near-watercolor transparency (1:6 to 1:12) and applied as a thin coloured film over sealed surfaces. Used here for the skin warmth glaze (FW Crimson 513), the leather unifying glaze (FW Crimson 513), and the root warmth glaze on hair (Brown Wash 201). The key skill is loading the brush, touching the surface at the near edge of the zone, and letting capillary pull draw it flat — not brushing it. For broad zones, a flat wash brush sweeps over and evaporates rather than pools.

✦ TIP: Test every glaze on a spare card first. At 1:8 the tint should be barely visible on white card but clearly present on coloured painted surface. If it reads strongly on white card it's too concentrated — add more water.
