Soldier Boy — Vought's original supe, a Cold War relic who reads as military first and superhero second. The costume's dark green is the dominant read at arm's length, so push its value range harder than feels comfortable: near-black in the deepest folds, near-olive-cream at the crown edges. The red belt and boot accents earn their visual weight by contrast against that dark field — keep them rich, not bright. The aged bronze shield is the centerpiece; restraint in the verdigris goes further than chaos. Light source: zenithal from above and slightly forward, matching Soldier Boy's promotional photography. Values before hues, always.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal. Soldier Boy is a rugged male military figure with a worn, weathered energy. The red zenithal underpainting creates natural warmth and a subtle exertion flush through the thin flesh layers above it — exactly right for a combat-hardened supe who's been frozen for forty years and just thawed out. The magenta of Method 2A would over-feminize the read. Method 1 would require more layers to reach the same depth.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; easiest to correct Can read flat without enough layers at 1:6 First-time painters, speed priority, tabletop figures
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink all over (1:2) → thin flesh layers built over top. Skip the magenta — go straight to red.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; works on fair and medium complexions Zenithal quality is critical — poor zenithal = muddy result Rugged male, military, weathered figures · 1:6 display

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → Magenta 011 all over (1:4) → FW Crimson Ink into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; reads as lit from within Magenta shifts the read toward feminine/heroic — not right for Soldier Boy Fair/feminine, protagonist/hero figures, elegant display pieces

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (vein areas) → seal → red wash (capillary zones).

Most realistic tonal mapping achievable; three distinct undertone zones Slowest method — 3 varnish layers; not suitable for casual builds Historical portrait, display max-realism, painters with time to invest
Beard Stubble & Freckling: After skin is fully painted and sealed with satin varnish, apply dark stubble in the beard zone (chin, jaw, upper lip, below cheekbones) using a worn flat brush stippled with thinned Warm Brown S09 (1:3). Follow with a second stipple pass of thinned Coal Black 002 (1:4) at the densest zones to read as genuine dark stubble. Optional sparing freckling on nose and cheeks with Burnt Sienna 059 thinned to near-watercolor, PSI 8–12, 3–5cm distance.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm. Full coverage, no bare resin. This becomes the permanent deep shadow value that bleeds through every subsequent layer.

Black Primer P-002
Pro Acryl
~3% value — permanent shadow base
✦ TIP: Do NOT thin primer under any circumstances. Thinning breaks down its adhesion and turns it into paint. Apply undiluted at 30+ PSI.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm. Heavy from directly above, fading to nothing by the underside of the chin and neck. Near-white on the crown, forehead plane, nose bridge, cheekbones, and tops of shoulders. Black retained fully in recesses, under brows, under chin, in the sides of the neck. This light/dark read IS the face — get it right before any color touches the skin.

White Primer P-003
Pro Acryl
~92% value — zenithal light
✦ TIP: Step back and desaturate the photo on your phone right now. The greyscale read should show a clear light source — bright top planes, dark bottom planes, shadow side. If it looks flat, your zenithal didn't fade enough. Fix it before adding color.
Value Build — Red Underpainting
Step 3 · Airbrush

Red Zenithal Underpainting

FW Crimson Ink thinned 1:2 (ink:water) — airbrush over the entire skin surface, all over. This is the "red zenithal" that creates the warm living undertone beneath the flesh layers. The ink is semi-transparent so the zenithal still shows through. Apply from all angles — not just top-down — so the ink reaches recesses. Let dry fully before proceeding.

FW Crimson Ink
FW Inks
~20% value — warm crimson underpainting
⚠ NOTE: Do NOT use Pro Acryl Transparent Red 047 for this step. PA Transparent Red shifts toward magenta when thinned, ruining the warm undertone. FW Crimson Ink holds its red hue under dilution.
✦ TIP: The ink will pool slightly in recesses — this is correct behavior. The deep shadow zones want that red warmth. The raised zenithal planes will show paler because the ink sits thinner there over white primer.
Step 4 · Airbrush

Shadow Base Coat

Shadow Flesh 042 thinned 1:3 — applied from zenithal angle only. This is the first flesh tone, establishing the shadow value. Leave the deepest recesses (orbital area, under chin, sides of nose, creases) untouched — the crimson underpainting stays there. Build 2–3 passes, each barely adding opacity.

Shadow Flesh 042
Pro Acryl
~38% value — shadow flesh anchor
✦ TIP: This step should read as approximately 38% value when you squint at it. If it's covering the zenithal entirely after one pass, your mix is too thick. Re-thin and let the gradient breathe.
Step 5 · Airbrush

Mid-Tone — True Skin Color

Warm Flesh 073 thinned 1:3 — zenithal application, slightly narrower zone than Step 4. This is the character's true skin color. It should occupy the largest single value zone on the face. Leave shadow recesses showing Shadow Flesh 042 beneath. 3–4 thin passes to build opacity on the light-catching planes.

Warm Flesh 073
Pro Acryl
~55% value — true mid-tone skin color
✦ TIP: At this point, the face should already read as a face in greyscale — distinct shadows, a middle value, and the promise of more light to come. If it looks washed out or flat, the zenithal wasn't strong enough in Step 2.

Step 10 · Brush

Beard Stubble

Add a second stipple pass with Coal Black 002 thinned 1:4 at the densest zones (chin center, upper lip) to suggest genuine dark stubble. Keep the stipple marks tiny and irregular — large blobs read as dirt, not hair.

Dark warm-brown hair with natural highlights; hazel-green eyes with a strong catchlight. Hair direction follows the sculpt texture — read it before you paint it.

Hair Shadow Base

Warm Brown Zenithal

Dark Golden Brown 062 thinned 1:3 — zenithal from above. This warm brown lands only on the crown and side planes that catch light, leaving Payne's Grey in the recesses between hair clumps. The contrast between cool shadows and warm highlights is what reads as natural hair at 1:6 scale.

Crown Edge Highlight

Warm Brown S09 thinned 1:2 — very sparingly on the absolute outermost edge of the hair at the crown and temples. This is where ambient light catches the hair silhouette. Should read at approximately 50% value — lighter than the strand highlights below but not dramatically so.

Iris — Hazel Green

Jensen Ackles has hazel-green eyes. Apply Faded Green 066 thinned 1:1 as the iris base. While wet, touch a tiny amount of Yellow Green 065 toward the pupil center — the iris is warmer/more saturated at the center and cooler/more muted at the outer edge. Work both eyes simultaneously to keep them symmetrical. At 1:6 scale the iris is tiny — a size 000 brush and a steady hand.

Step 8 · Brush

Pupil, Eyeliner & Catchlight

Coal Black 002 undiluted — a single small dot for each pupil, centered in the iris. Thin Coal Black with a drop of water and line the upper eyelid crease for definition. Once fully dry, apply a tiny dot of Bold Titanium White 001 to the upper-left of each iris as the catchlight. This single white dot is what makes a figure's eyes appear to have life — it must be consistent in position on both eyes.

Coal Black 002

Step 9 · Finish

Eye Gloss

Apply a targeted drop of gloss varnish to each eye using a fine brush. Gloss eyes in a matte or satin face — the contrast in finish type is what reads as the wet surface of the eyeball. Do not let gloss varnish spill onto the eyelid or skin.

✦ TIP: Let the gloss varnish cure for at least 30 minutes before handling the head. Fingerprints in fresh gloss are permanent at this scale.

Metals

Three distinct metal types: gold stars and trim (bright decorative gold), aged bronze shield (weathered patina with verdigris hints), and steel pistol (cold functional steel). All use the 1:6 Gloss Black Base Method — painting the light, not the metal.

✦ VALUE FIRST — EVEN ON METALLICS: Metallic paint reads flat without strong value contrast. Deep recesses near-black; primary metallic in mid-tones; raised edges catch near-white. The principle is identical to any other material — only the paint has a metallic sheen.

FW Crimson Ink — Airbrush Notes
Technique · Airbrush

Working with FW Inks

FW Acrylic Inks are water-based and flow beautifully through a 0.3mm nozzle. They are highly pigmented — thin 1:2 for the skin underpainting pass. Because they are water-based, avoid mixing with acrylic retarder or flow improver in the cup — this can cause separation. Use water only. Clean the airbrush immediately after with airbrush cleaner — FW inks cure hard if left in the cup.

✦ TIP: Tip dry is more common with FW inks than with standard acrylics. Keep a damp cloth nearby and wipe the needle tip every 5–6 minutes during the ink underpainting steps.
⚠ NOTE: Do NOT substitute Pro Acryl Transparent Red 047 for FW Crimson Ink in the skin underpainting step. PA Transparent Red shifts toward magenta when thinned, producing an incorrect and unfixable warm base for the flesh layers.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: paint too thick or PSI too low for viscosity. Effect: blobs and splatters rather than a fine mist. Fix: increase PSI by 2–3, re-thin the mix, and test on card. On skin tones especially, too-thick paint catches on raised planes and blobs rather than floating on.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving the airbrush too slowly or stopping trigger mid-stroke. Effect: visible stripes where paint built up. Fix: always keep moving when the trigger is depressed. Start trigger before each stroke begins and release after it ends. Practice the stroke path on paper first before hitting the figure.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: over-thinning or applying too many passes without dry time. Effect: paint runs down vertical surfaces. Fix: add a small amount of paint to the mix and test consistency — a well-thinned mix flows off a palette knife in a slow unbroken stream. Allow each airbrush pass to dry completely (30–60 seconds) before the next.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting, capillary wash application, stippled stubble, liner-brush strand work, wet blending (Expert Acrylics track), and drybrushing for base textures.

Technique · Brush

Edge Highlighting

Load a size 1 or 0 round brush with thinned paint (1:1 to 1:2). Wipe to near-dry on a paper towel — the brush should feel almost empty. Then drag the very tip along the raised edge of an armor panel, fabric fold crown, or star point. The goal is a thin consistent bright line, not a filled stroke. Consistency of width matters more than brightness. Practice on scrap plastic before committing to the figure.

✦ TIP: The edge line should not be visible as a separate stroke from arm's length — it should read as light catching the surface. If you can see it as a painted line, it's too thick or too bright.
Technique · Wash

Capillary Wash Application

Load a medium round brush generously with wash. Touch the brush tip to the lowest point of a recess — the wash flows in via capillary action without being brushed around the surface. This produces clean, consistent recess pooling without tide lines. Never brush a wash over a flat surface — always flow it in from a low contact point.

⚠ NOTE: Brushing a wash around a surface creates tide marks as the wash dries. If a tide mark forms, clean up the edge while still wet with a barely-damp brush. Prevention is easier than fixing.
Technique · Brush

Stippled Stubble & Texture

Use an old flat brush with the bristles splayed slightly. Load with thinned paint (1:3), wipe to near-dry, then stipple onto the target area with the brush tips — tapping rather than dragging. At 1:6 scale, the individual marks are tiny. The goal is a zone-level value and color shift, not visible individual marks. Build in 2–3 thin layers: each layer adds density without flooding the surface. Assess from arm's length after each layer, not up close.

✦ TIP: Practice stippled stubble on a primed sphere (a ball bearing or similar) before trying it on the face. The transition from "not enough" to "too much" happens in about 10 seconds of stippling — knowing what it feels like at both ends builds instinct faster than fixing mistakes.
Technique · Brush

Drybrushing — Base Textures

Load a stiff flat brush with undiluted paint. Wipe vigorously on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers. Drag the near-dry brush across the textured surface — paint deposits only on the highest raised points of the texture. The result is fast, natural-looking texture contrast. Critical: the brush must be drier than feels comfortable. If it drags wet paint, wipe again. The drier the brush, the more controlled the highlight.

✦ TIP: For the sandbag burlap texture, drybrush in two passes: first with Bright Warm Grey 015 for the broad mid-highlight, then with a near-dry brush of Ivory 023 on only the very topmost threads. The two-pass drybrush reads as woven fabric, not just a dusty surface.
Technique · Brush

Semi-Transparent Metallic Layering (TMM 1:6 Scale)

Thin metallic paint to 1:3 or 1:4. Apply to light-facing surfaces only — do NOT cover the entire metal surface. The thinned metallic is semi-transparent, so the dark base below bleeds through on surfaces that angle away from the light source. You are painting the light that reflects from the metal, not the metal itself. Multiple thin passes build opacity on the most light-facing planes while maintaining transparency on transitional planes.

✦ TIP: After applying the primary metallic, step back and look at the piece from arm's length. At display distance, the metal should already look convincingly reflective from the contrast alone. If it looks like a metallic blob rather than a reflective surface, the value contrast isn't wide enough — deepen the shadows and lighten the specular.
