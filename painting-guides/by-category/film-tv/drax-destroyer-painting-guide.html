Drax the Destroyer — MCU version (Dave Bautista). A warrior defined by his slate-blue-grey skin and the raised scarification tattoos that map the history of his family. Drax reads as a cold, powerful surface with pockets of deep biological warmth — the key challenge is keeping the grey cool and desaturated while still having life and depth. Values follow a wide range: near-black in deep muscle shadows, slate-grey in mid-form, and cool near-white on topmost planes. Light source is zenithal from above, casting dramatic shadow under the pec muscles, jaw, and belt line. The dragon base adds a second challenge: warm/cool contrast between the creature and Drax's cooler skin. References: MCU film stills (2014–2023) and 3dWicked MCU sculpt render. Skin confirmed as "the perfect shade of slate-blue-grey" (James Gunn); tattoos are raised keloid prosthetics turned cherry red.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal — Drax is a rugged, battle-hardened male warrior. Method 2B's warm red underpainting in the recesses creates the biological depth that keeps grey skin from reading as painted stone. Key adaptation for grey skin: apply the Transparent Red step extremely restrained — only into the deepest recesses (armpit folds, deep abdominal creases, shadow under the jaw). The cool grey layers on top will cover most of it, leaving just a hint of warmth in the deepest shadows that reads as life rather than colour.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → grey undercoat → mid-grey base → highlight layers. No underpainting. Works cleanly for grey/alien skin.

Most predictable; excellent control over the cool grey tone Shadows may lack biological warmth; can read flat First-time painter, speed priority, or pure cold grey desired

Method 2B — Red Zenithal ✓ Recommended

Black prime → white zenithal → very restrained Transparent Red in deepest recesses only → grey base coat layers built over top.

Warm shadow depth; skin reads biological not stone; suits rugged warrior Red zenithal must be extremely subtle — too much and grey layers won't cover it Rugged male, warrior, alien with cool organic skin — Drax

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Grey mid-tone base → satin seal → selective yellow (fatty zones) → blue (vein/tendon zones) → red (capillary zones) wash layers, sealed between each.

Maximum realism; the blue wash step reinforces Drax's cool-grey quality; most depth Slowest — 3 varnish layers + dry times; subtle at 1:6 unless display lighting is controlled Display max-realism; combine with Method 2B underpainting for peak depth
No Freckling — Tattoo Integration Instead: Drax has no freckles or skin texture variation. After completing skin base layers, the raised scarification tattoos are painted on top (see the Tattoos tab). The tattoo step must happen after skin is sealed with satin varnish. The Transparent Red tattoo glaze will naturally integrate with the skin surface at the edges of each scar line.

Weapons

Drax carries two weapons: his dual blades (alien-tech knives with a dark utilitarian steel finish) and a large sci-fi bazooka rested over his right shoulder. Both use value-first TMM — near-black in recesses, primary metallic on body faces, near-white specular on the sharpest edges. The bazooka reads heavier and more industrial than the blades; it gets a darker overall value and more aggressive weathering to sell the scale difference between a hand-knife and a shoulder-mounted launcher.

Bazooka — Panel Lines & Weathering
Step 6 · Wash / Enamel

Panel Line Pin Wash — Tamiya Black or Black Wash 200

Two options depending on your preference. Acrylic: Black Wash 200, fine brush, capillary pin wash into all panel lines and vent slots — same technique as the blades. Enamel (recommended for detail depth): Tamiya Panel Line Accent Color Black over a sealed surface. Seal the bazooka with matte varnish first, then apply the Tamiya enamel — it wicks cleanly into lines and can be selectively removed with enamel thinner on a cotton swab. The enamel gives a sharper, more precise result on the heavier panel geometry of a large weapon. Clean up before moving to weathering.

Step 7 · Weathering

Battle Grime — Villainy Ink Goon's Grime

Villainy Ink Goon's Grime (VI-001) — enamel wash, dark brown with a slight yellow-green tinge. Apply after the panel line pass dries. Thin slightly with enamel thinner and stipple into recessed zones: around the barrel base, in vent openings, along the underside of the body housing, and wherever the weapon would collect use-wear and grime from Drax's shoulder. Remove excess from flat surfaces with a clean enamel-thinner swab. This is the detail that separates a display piece from a toy — a shouldered battle weapon should look like it has been fired.

Step 8 · Finish

Selective Varnish

Matte varnish over the entire bazooka as a final seal. Then spot-gloss only the flat top faces of the barrel and any smooth body panels — not the grip, not the underside, not the weathered zones. The contrast between a matte grip/underside and a lightly glossy barrel top is the same read-the-material-at-distance principle as the blades. Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spattering

Paint dries on needle tip causing spits and spatters. Cause: paint too thick, low humidity, or pausing too long between passes. Fix: add 5–10% flow improver to the mix. Between passes, remove dried paint from the needle tip using a dry brush lightly dipped in airbrush cleaner — gentle strokes. Do not let the ferrule enter the airbrush cap.

Blowback / Bubbling

Air back-flows into the colour cup, causing paint to bubble. Cause: trigger pressed (air open) while needle is fully closed or needle seal worn. Fix: verify needle is forward before opening air. If seal is worn, clean the back-end and apply a small amount of airbrush seal grease.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Horizontal bands of paint rather than smooth gradient. Cause: moving too slowly or inconsistent trigger pull. Fix: maintain a constant, even walking speed across the surface. Practice consistent trigger pull on a piece of paper before spraying the figure. Smooth, even motion at constant distance.

Grey Looks Flat / No Value

Grey skin reads as a single flat tone with no shadow or highlight. Cause: mid-tone applied too broadly, covering the zenithal structure. Fix: do the greyscale check BEFORE applying any colour. If the zenithal is solid, paint more sparingly — the mid-tone should cover 60% of the surface maximum, not 100%.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: layering (skin and tattoos), edge highlighting (blades, scale tips), controlled pin washing, thin-layer glazing, and drybrush (rock base). All technique descriptions are specific to this figure.

Technique · Layering

Thin-Layer Build — Skin and Tattoos

Apply multiple thin coats rather than one opaque coat. Each layer should be thin enough to show the layer below — build coverage gradually. For skin, each layer covers slightly less area than the previous (working up toward the highlight). For tattoos, each layer builds from shadow outward to the raised ridge. Thin your paint until it flows easily from the brush without beading. If it drags or leaves brush marks, it's too dry — add a small amount of water.

✦ TIP: The secret to smooth layering on resin at 1:6 scale is having the correct water content in the brush — not just in the paint. Load the brush, then wipe it on a damp palette or tissue until it barely drags. The right amount of moisture in the brush prevents the paint from setting too fast before you can blend.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Metallic Edge — Blades and Scale Tips

Load a flat or liner brush with paint, wipe the brush almost dry on a palette. Hold the figure so the edge you want to highlight is horizontal. Drag the nearly-dry brush tip lightly along the edge. The residue paint catches only on the raised edge and not the surrounding surface. For blades: thin lines only — one stroke per edge, resist the urge to go back over it. For scale tips: same principle applied to the most prominent forward-facing scale ridges.

✦ TIP: The most common edge highlighting mistake is loading too much paint and getting a wide blob rather than a crisp thin line. When in doubt, wipe more off the brush. You can always add another pass, but too much at once is harder to fix.
Technique · Pin Washing

Targeted Recess Wash — Tattoos, Blades, Stone

Pin washing uses capillary action instead of brushwork to get wash into recesses. Load a 000 or 00 brush with wash paint. Touch only the very tip of the brush to the opening of a recess or panel line. Surface tension pulls the wash into the recess automatically. Lift the brush immediately — don't draw it along the surface. The wash flows in and settles under gravity. If it flows somewhere you don't want it, immediately blot with a barely-damp clean brush before it dries.

⚠ NOTE: Pin washing must be done over a sealed surface (satin or matte varnish) to prevent the wash from lifting or staining surrounding soft acrylic layers. Always seal before washing.
Technique · Drybrush

Rock Base Texture Work

Drybrush is the primary technique for rock base highlights. Load a flat or fan brush with paint, then wipe across a paper towel until virtually no paint transfers — the brush should leave only a faint smear on the towel. Stroke across the rock surface in a sweeping motion. The residue paint catches only on the topmost texture peaks. Build progressively lighter and smaller passes for each highlight tier. Expert Acrylics heavy-body paints are outstanding for drybrush — the high pigment density and thick body hold extremely well on a near-dry brush.

✦ TIP: Always hold the brush at approximately 45° to the surface and stroke in one direction per pass. Going back and forth in random directions on rough stone texture actually works fine for rock, but for organic surfaces like scales, stroke in the direction of the form (spine to tip).
✦ GREYSCALE CHECK: At any point during painting, desaturate a photo of the figure in your phone's editor. It should read clearly in greyscale — visible light source, form, and depth. If it looks flat, values are too compressed. Fix values before correcting colour. This check works for every material in this guide: skin, tattoos, stone, scales, and blades all have their own distinct value structure that should be readable in greyscale.
