Superman (Christopher Reeve, 1978) — The definitive cinematic Superman. Fair warm complexion, luminous and heroic — light must come from within this figure, not from the room. Paint the entire figure with one imagined key light: overhead, front-center, like a studio spotlight or Kryptonian sunlight. Every surface earns its highlight through painted value structure; never rely on room lighting to do the work. The suit is medium royal cobalt blue (the actual fabric had a slight teal cast but photographed clean blue); the cape and trunks a vivid primary red; the S-shield and belt warm golden-yellow. Costume design by Yvonne Blake & Richard Bruno. Reference: Superman: The Movie (1978), dir. Richard Donner.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2A — The Pinkle Method — Superman is a luminous heroic protagonist with a fair warm complexion. The Pinkle method's magenta underpainting creates sub-surface scattering that reads as "lit from within" — exactly right for the Man of Steel. Reeve's skin under the film's studio lighting had a warm, almost glowing quality. Method 2B (Red Zenithal) is a valid faster alternative for a slightly more weathered look.
Recommended

Method 2A — The Pinkle Method

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over (thin) → Transparent Red into recesses → thin flesh layers built over the top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; warm shadows come naturally Poor zenithal = muddy result; harder to correct mid-process Heroic / protagonist figures, fair-skinned display pieces, 1:6 scale

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Same as 2A but skip magenta — apply Transparent Red or Flesh Wash directly over the white zenithal, then thin flesh layers.

Faster; natural warmth; works well on male figures Less sub-surface glow than 2A Rugged male, weathered figures — valid alternative here

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → dark undercoat → base tone → mid-tone layers → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; easiest to correct Can read flat at display scale without many layers 28mm, first-time painters, speed priority
Freckling / Speckling: After skin is sealed, thin Burnt Sienna 059 or Flesh Wash 202 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots, not lines. Target nose bridge, cheekbones, upper shoulders, backs of hands. One pass → step back → assess. Keep it subtle on a heroic figure. Practice on card first.

Step 3 · Airbrush

Magenta All Over — Sub-Surface Scatter

Magenta 011 — thin at 1:4 to 1:5, transparent coat over all skin. You're tinting the zenithal, not obscuring it. White should still read through clearly. This layer simulates sub-surface scattering — the warm glow beneath living skin. The face will look surreal. That's correct. Do not add more — trust the process.

Magenta 011
Pro Acryl
~50% value — transparent pinkle layer
⚠ NOTE: If magenta kills your zenithal, it's too thick. 1:5 or 1:6 is fine. The value structure underneath must remain visible through the magenta layer.
Step 4 · Airbrush

Transparent Red — Recesses

Transparent Red 047 — undiluted or lightly thinned at 1:2 maximum, targeted into deep recesses: eye sockets, beside the nose, between lips, under the chin, ear canals, knuckle creases, inner elbow folds. Do NOT thin aggressively — Transparent Red 047 shifts to magenta when heavily thinned. For any glazed/thinned red application elsewhere, use FW Crimson Ink instead.

Dark warm brown hair with the iconic Superman curl. Piercing blue eyes — vivid and clear, echoing the suit's color family.

Airbrush Skills

Techniques used in this guide — all referenced to real steps above. Greyscale check applies to the full figure before any color is applied.

Core Techniques Used in This Guide
Technique · Airbrush

Black Primer Coverage

Nozzle: 0.5mm. PSI: 30–35. Distance: 20–25cm. Ratio: Undiluted. Multiple overlapping passes at medium speed. Move the airbrush before pulling trigger, release trigger before stopping movement. No pooling in recesses.

✦ TIP: Rotate the figure and spray from every angle to avoid holidays (missed coverage). Primer must cover 100% — any bare resin will refuse paint later.
Technique · Airbrush

Zenithal Priming (White Over Black)

Nozzle: 0.5mm. PSI: 28–32. Distance: 20–25cm. Ratio: Undiluted. Spray from directly above + slightly forward. Rotate on the vertical axis only — never tilt. The goal is a gradient from bright white on absolute top planes to unbroken black in recesses and undersides. Push it harder than feels right. Photograph and desaturate to verify the value story before continuing.

⚠ NOTE: The zenithal IS your light source map. A weak zenithal gives flat results no matter how good the color layers are. If the value gradient is too compressed after zenithal, re-black the figure and start again — it's faster than trying to fix it through color layers.
Technique · Airbrush

Transparent Misting — Skin & Pinkle Layers

Nozzle: 0.3mm. PSI: 18–22. Distance: 15–20cm. Ratio: 1:4 to 1:5. Magenta and skin layers need to be nearly transparent — they tint the zenithal rather than obscure it. Low PSI for fine atomization. Short bursts. Assess coverage frequently. Can always add; cannot remove.

✦ TIP: After each misting pass, step back 50cm and assess. The zenithal value structure should still be fully legible through the color layer. If you can't read the value structure, the layer is too thick.
Technique · Airbrush

Directional Highlight — Color Layers

Nozzle: 0.3mm. PSI: 20–25. Distance: 12–18cm. Ratio: 1:2 to 1:3. Target the same planes as the white zenithal — chest, shoulders, raised fold peaks, top of head. Use a tight spray pattern. Blend out toward the edges. Multiple light passes beat one heavy pass.

Technique · Airbrush

Fine Detail & Specular — Skin Highlights

Nozzle: 0.2mm. PSI: 15–18. Distance: 8–12cm. Ratio: 1:4 to 1:5. Final skin specular layer. Extremely fine control needed. Keep the trigger barely open. Hits just the topmost raised plane — nose tip, brow ridge, cheekbone peak. One or two careful passes maximum. Mask surrounding areas with tape if precision is difficult.

Technique · Airbrush

The Greyscale Check

After completing all zenithal priming — before any color — photograph the figure and desaturate the image in your phone's editor. The figure should read as a coherent value structure: bright top, mid-tone forward planes, dark undersides and recesses. A visible, clear light source direction. If the image reads flat (all values similar), push the zenithal harder. Do this check again after base coats are applied. Values should still read clearly through the color. Fix values before fixing color.

✦ TIP: The greyscale check is not optional on a display-scale figure. It costs 30 seconds and will save you hours of chasing flat results later.
Technique · Airbrush

Skin Freckling

Nozzle: 0.3mm (almost fully closed). PSI: 8–12. Distance: 3–5cm. Ratio: Near-watercolor thin. After skin is fully sealed. Short fractional trigger bursts — you're atomizing single micro-dots, not spraying. Target nose, cheeks, upper shoulders, backs of hands. One pass → step back → assess. This is the easiest technique to overdo. Practice on scrap card first.

⚠ NOTE: The gap between "good" and "too much" freckling is about 2 seconds of trigger time. Go slow. On a heroic figure like Superman, keep freckling very subtle.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide. All are referenced to specific steps in the tabs above. These notes are the "how to hold the brush" layer — the mechanical skills that make the color recipes work.

Techniques Used in This Guide
Technique · Brush

Edge Highlighting

Load a flat or filbert brush lightly with near-undiluted paint. Wipe most of the paint off on the palette edge. Draw the very corner or edge of the brush along a raised detail edge — fold ridge, boot cuff, shield border. Consistent pressure. Let the paint deposit itself on the highest edge only. Thinner line = harder, more defined material (leather, hard armor). Wider, softer line = softer material (fabric, skin).

✦ TIP: The "less paint than you think" principle always applies to edge highlighting. Reload too early and the line becomes a blob. Better to run the brush once and barely deposit paint than to reload mid-edge.
Technique · Brush

Fine Liner Work — Outlines & "S" Shield

Load a fine liner or 00 round brush to the very tip only. Thin paint to a flowing consistency (not dripping, not stiff). Rest your hand or the figure on a stable surface. Draw from the shoulder, not the wrist — longer, more controlled strokes. Pull liner strokes toward you. For the "S" shield, do the black outlines first; let dry completely before filling colors.

✦ TIP: A well-loaded liner brush has enough paint to complete a 3–4cm stroke without reloading. If you're reloading every 5mm, the paint is too dry or too thick.
Technique · Brush

Dry Brushing — Gold Belt & Metallic Highlight

Load a stiff flat or fan brush with metallic paint. Wipe on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers. Drag the brush lightly across raised surfaces — belt edges, shield rim. Metallics dry-brush extremely well because of their particle nature. Result: crisp, light-catching deposits on the highest texture points only.

⚠ NOTE: Dry-brushing over matte base coats leaves a chalky residue if the brush isn't wiped enough. Test on the back of the figure or a scrap before applying to the belt.
Technique · Brush

Capillary Wash — Skin Recesses

For Transparent Red 047 into skin recesses — thin the paint to watercolor consistency, load the brush fully, then touch the very tip to a deep recess (inner eye corner, beside nose, ear canal). Surface tension pulls the paint into the recess automatically. Wipe back any excess from flat surfaces immediately with a clean slightly-damp brush before it dries.

✦ TIP: The brush tip must actually touch the recess edge — hold over and it won't flow. The model must be reasonably level so gravity helps capillary action.
Technique · Brush

Wet Blending (Expert Acrylics Sub-Tabs)

Applies to cape, suit, and trunks Expert Acrylics methods. Work one panel/section at a time. Apply shadow color, then while still wet apply mid-tone directly adjacent. Use a clean damp (not wet) filbert brush to feather the join — light horizontal strokes across the boundary. Work quickly — Expert Acrylics have a longer open time than Pro Acryl dropper, but they still dry. Use a Slow-Dri medium if you need more time.

✦ TIP: Wet blending on a vertical surface is harder than horizontal — thin paint more aggressively and use smaller sections. For large fabric elements like the cape, work one fold at a time, not the whole surface.
Technique · Brush

Specular Dot Placement

Final highlight/specular dots are placed on the topmost, most-light-struck point of a surface. This is a single tiny mark — not a stroke. Load a 000 round or liner to the very tip. Touch once to the surface and lift. The paint should leave a rounded dot, not a drag mark. If it leaves a streak, the brush is overloaded or the paint too thin.

✦ TIP: On a 1:6 figure, specular dots should be 0.3–1.0mm in diameter. Larger than that and it reads as highlight, not specular. When in doubt, go smaller.

Thinning Reference

All airbrush and brush ratios used in this guide. Ratios are paint:thinner unless stated otherwise.

Nozzle Size = Paint Fraction Rule
The nozzle diameter as a decimal is the paint fraction — the remainder is thinner.
0.5mm → ~50% paint · 50% thinner → approx 1:1 0.3mm → ~30% paint · 70% thinner → approx 1:2.3 0.2mm → ~20% paint · 80% thinner → approx 1:4 This is a starting point. Paint pigment density, brand viscosity, and PSI all affect the ideal ratio. Always test on a scrap card first. A well-thinned mix flows off a palette knife in a slow, unbroken stream — not blobs, not water. Spits or spatters → add thinner. Loses opacity too fast → add paint.

Common Problems & Fixes

Airbrush spatters & spits

Paint too thick for the nozzle, or PSI too low for the ratio. Add thinner in small increments (5% at a time). Check for dried paint at nozzle tip — flush with thinner between colors.

Color looks chalky / washed out

Paint too thin — losing opacity and leaving only the white carrier. Either increase paint ratio or switch to a more pigment-dense paint for base coats. Happens most often with yellow and red over light primers.

Yellow over dark primer — no coverage

Yellow is inherently semi-transparent. Undercoat with Warm Yellow 072 or Golden Yellow 006 at 2:1 ratio — 3 passes — before adding Bismuth Yellow on top. Never try to cover dark primer with yellow in one coat.

Transparent Red shifting to magenta

Transparent Red 047 shifts magenta when heavily thinned. Use undiluted or maximum 1:2 for recess application. For any thinned red glaze, substitute FW Crimson Ink instead.

Edge highlights too wide / blobby

Too much paint on the brush. Wipe off on palette edge until the brush feels almost dry, then apply. The first pass should barely deposit paint — build up with multiple passes rather than one loaded stroke.

Wet blend dried before finishing

Work smaller sections with Expert Acrylics — one fold or panel at a time. Add a drop of Slow-Dri medium to extend open time. Keep a mister bottle nearby; a very light mist of water over the work surface slows drying.
