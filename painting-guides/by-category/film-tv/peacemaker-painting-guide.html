Peacemaker — John Cena's Christopher Smith is a walking contradiction: chrome-domed, flag-caped, blood-drenched conviction. The figure's value story is anchors in brutal contrast — a mirror-polished helmet surrounded by everything that's been dirtied, torn, and bled on. Zenithal light source from above and slightly forward, with no OSL; this is daylight on a battlefield. Priority materials in order: chrome helmet (the showstopper), battle-damaged red shirt, weathered khaki pants, and Eagly's feather detail.

Method Selection
Method 2B — Red Zenithal Recommended. Peacemaker is rugged, masculine, and battle-worn. The red zenithal creates the natural warmth and exertion flush of someone who's been in a prolonged, brutal fight — without the Pinkle's luminous idealization. The warm undertone reads as physical intensity rather than ethereal glow.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers; misses the battle-flushed warmth Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, anime/cartoon figures

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → Transparent Red into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers Creates an idealized, luminous look that doesn't suit Peacemaker's gritty energy Fair / feminine, protagonist / hero display figures

Method 2B — Red Zenithal Recommended

Black prime → white zenithal → warm red wash all over → thin flesh layers on top. Skips the magenta entirely.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; works perfectly on fair-medium complexions Same zenithal dependency as 2A — poor zenithal means uneven warmth Rugged male, military, survivor, battle-worn figures
Freckling / Speckling: Optional but effective on the shoulders and forearms. Thin Burnt Sienna 059 to near-watercolor, PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Short bursts only — micro-dots, not spray. One pass → step back → assess. His exposed skin reads weathered, not immaculate. Blood spatters are handled separately in Step 10.

Helmet

The chrome dome is the visual signature of the figure — polished to near-mirror reflectivity, dented and scratched from battle but never compromised. 1:6 scale Gloss Black Base Method. The teal graffiti marks on the crown are painted last.

✦ VALUE FIRST — CHROME PRINCIPLE: At 1:6 scale, polished chrome doesn't gradually fade from light to dark — it has hard alternating bands of near-white and near-black as surface planes change angle relative to the light source. You are NOT painting silver paint on the helmet. You are painting the light that hits chrome, leaving black base to be the reflection of shadow areas. Restraint in coverage reads more convincingly than full silver coverage.
True Metallic Metal — Gloss Black Base Method
Step 1 · Airbrush

Gloss Black Base

Satin Black S39 as the gloss-black base — apply 2–3 thin coats at 1:2 thinning, 0.3mm nozzle, PSI 15. Full coverage on the entire chrome dome. Let fully cure (30+ minutes minimum). This black is permanent — it will show through the silver as the shadow reflection zones on the chrome surface. The glossier the finish, the more convincing the chrome will be.

Satin Black S39
Pro Acryl
~5% value — permanent shadow; the reflection of dark areas on chrome
✦ TIP: Add 1–2 drops of Gloss Varnish MPAM-003 to the Satin Black mix to push it fully gloss. A more reflective black base means a more convincing chrome result over the top.
Step 2 · Airbrush

Map the Light Planes

Before airbrushing silver, study the helmet carefully under your work light. The dome is a sphere — the zenithal light source means the top hemisphere faces the light; the equator and lower half face away. Additionally, any dents or surface features create micro-variations in plane angle. These light-facing planes get silver; shadow-facing planes keep the black base. Think in bands.

✦ TIP: Hold the helmet under your work light and rotate it slowly. You'll see actual light catching different zones — those are exactly where your silver will go. Trust what you see, not what you imagine a helmet "should" look like.
Step 3 · Airbrush

Mid Silver — Light-Facing Planes (Two-Pass Build)

VMC is pre-thinned — use 1:1 with distilled water, not 1:3. 0.3mm nozzle, PSI 12–15, 10cm. Pass A — Duraluminium: Apply VMC 77.702 Duraluminium from the zenithal angle to all light-facing planes. This slightly darker, greyer silver establishes the mid-value body of the chrome. Multiple thin passes. The gloss black bleeds through shadow-facing planes automatically — do NOT paint the equator band or underside. Pass B — Aluminium: Without cleaning the cup, load VMC 77.701 Aluminium and hit only the brightest center of the light-facing zone — the very top of the dome, directly facing the light. This builds a tonal gradient from greyer mid-tone at the edges of the lit zone to a brighter neutral silver at the apex. Let each pass dry before adding the next.

Duraluminium 77.702
Vallejo Metal Color
~50% value — shadow-edge of lit plane; greyer mid chrome

✦ TIP: VMC metallic pigment settles fast. Agitate the cup frequently during the session — every 2–3 minutes if you're taking your time between passes. A dried-out cup means speckled, inconsistent coverage.
Step 4 · Brush

Chrome Edge Highlight — Apex Lines

VMC 77.707 Chrome near-undiluted (1–2 drops of water maximum to restore flow). Load a size 0 flat brush, wipe to near-dry, then drag along the very apex of the dome's equator ridge and any raised surface features — rivets, seams, dent edges. Chrome is the highest-reflectivity paint in the VMC line; it does not need white mixed in. These are fine lines, not broad areas. At arm's length they read as near-mirror glints against the darker Aluminium body beneath.

Chrome 77.707
Vallejo Metal Color

✦ TIP: VMC Chrome by brush is extremely smooth and self-levels well — it doesn't need the same "near-dry brush" discipline as a standard acrylic. But keep the application to genuine apex lines only. If you find yourself painting a broad area, you've gone too wide.
Step 5 · Brush

Specular Dot Highlights — Pure Point Glints

VMC 77.707 Chrome near-undiluted, size 000 detail brush. Single dot applications to the sharpest corners and most directly lit apex points — where a real chrome surface would produce a near-mirror reflection of the light source itself. These are not strokes; they are tiny deposits. The distinction between Step 4 (edge lines) and Step 5 (dots) is important: edge lines trace the ridge; dots mark the single hottest point on that ridge. 3–5 dots maximum on the entire helmet.

✦ TIP: The same Chrome paint does both Step 4 and Step 5 — the difference is application method and area, not paint. You don't need to reload between steps.
Step 6 · Wash

Recess Darkening — Battle Damage & Seams

Black Wash 200 targeted into any mechanical seam lines, deep scratches, or dent creases using a 000 brush. Capillary action does the work — touch the tip into the recess and let it pull the wash in. Immediately wipe any excess from the flat chrome surface with a clean nearly-dry brush before it dries. The gloss black base already handles broad shadow zones; this step is only for sharp-edged linear details.

Step 7 · Brush

Teal Graffiti Marks — Crown Detail

The reference shows teal-green paint marks on the dome crown — the look of painted-on pigment applied after the chrome. Bright Green Blue 009 + Bright Jade 067 mixed 2:1, thin 1:1. Apply with a size 0 round in loose irregular strokes following the reference. The edges should be slightly rough, not crisp — this is painted-on marking paint, not manufactured detail. Let a tiny amount bleed slightly for authenticity.

Step 8 · Finish

Final Varnish — Gloss Chrome + Matte Marks

Apply Gloss Varnish MPAM-003 to the entire chrome surface — this amplifies the metallic reflectivity and seals the silver layers. Then apply Matte Varnish MPAR-V01 specifically and carefully over just the teal graffiti marks, creating a contrast in finish between the shiny chrome dome and the flat painted-on marks. This finish contrast does heavy work for material differentiation.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling at Nozzle

Cause: Needle not fully seated, damaged nozzle cap, or paint in the air cap. Effect: Air pushes back through the cup creating bubbles or backspray. Fix: Check needle chuck tightness. Remove and clean the air cap. Check for nozzle cap cracks — replace if damaged.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: Inconsistent trigger pressure or speed, paint drying mid-spray (tip dry), or PSI too high for the distance. Effect: Stripes or patches of uneven opacity. Fix: Maintain steady movement speed. Check for tip dry. Drop PSI 2–3 and increase distance slightly. Pre-wet the surface with flow improver if working in a warm dry environment.

Paint Too Thin / Runs or Beads

Cause: Over-thinning, previous coat not dry, or applying from too close. Effect: Paint runs, pools in recesses, or beads instead of adhering. Fix: Allow previous coat to dry 10+ minutes. Increase paint fraction in the mix. Move further from the surface. Apply lighter passes rather than flooding a single pass.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting, drybrushing, stippling (mud and blood), wet blending (Expert Acrylics), blood effects application, and targeted wash control.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Edge Highlighting — Flat Brush Drag

Load a size 0 flat brush with paint thinned to approximately 1:2. Wipe most of the paint off on your palette until the brush is almost dry. Lay the flat edge of the brush along a raised edge and drag along it in one smooth motion. The near-dry brush deposits a fine bright line exactly on the edge without bleeding onto adjacent surfaces. Used on boot edges, armor plate rims, fabric fold peaks, blade cutting edges, and helmet seams. The key is removing almost all the paint before application — this technique depends entirely on a near-dry brush.

✦ TIP: Test each stroke on your palette before applying to the figure. The stroke should feel barely sticky — if you can see a solid line on the palette, you still have too much paint on the brush.
Technique · Drybrushing

Drybrushing — Feather Texture & Edge Dust

Used on Eagly's feathers and the base rubble. Load an old flat brush with undiluted paint, then wipe repeatedly on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers. Brush rapidly across the surface in short strokes — the nearly-dry bristles deposit paint only on the highest surface textures (feather barb tips, rubble edges). If you see solid coverage, your brush is still too wet. True drybrushing looks like it did almost nothing — until you step back.

✦ TIP: Keep a dedicated drybrush — the bristles spread with use, creating a perfect fan for feather and rubble work. Never use your good detail brushes for drybrushing; it destroys their shape irreversibly.
Technique · Stippling

Stippling — Mud, Blood, and Weathering Texture

Used for mud on the pants and blood effects throughout. Load a stiff-bristled brush (old size 2 flat, or a dedicated stencil brush), dab off on palette until near-dry, then tap the brush tip straight down onto the surface rather than dragging. Stippling creates organic irregular texture that reads as spray, spatter, or coarse material — it cannot be mimicked with layering or drybrushing. The key difference from drybrushing: vertical pouncing motion (stipple) vs. horizontal dragging motion (drybrush).

Technique · Blood Effects

Dry Blood + True Blood — Layered Application

Always apply in sequence: Dry Blood WP3164 first (dried/old blood, matte and dark), then True Blood WP3165 last (fresh/wet blood, self-leveling gloss). Seal the surface between them if needed. Dry Blood is stippled; True Blood is allowed to pool naturally — don't brush it out, just apply and let gravity and the self-leveling agent do the work. True Blood takes 24 hours to cure fully. Never seal over True Blood — it kills the gloss intentionally.

⚠ NOTE: True Blood fingerprints stay visible permanently once cured. Handle the figure only by the base and non-blood-effected surfaces after application.
Technique · Targeted Wash Control

Controlled Wash Application — Two-Brush Method

For precision wash placement (helmet seams, boot lace eyelets, gun panel lines), use two brushes simultaneously: one 000 detail brush loaded with wash, and one clean slightly-damp flat brush held in the other hand. Touch the loaded brush to the recess and let capillary action pull the wash in. Immediately use the clean brush to wick away any excess that bleeds onto adjacent surfaces. The clean brush must be only barely damp — too wet and it will drag the wash around. This method gives you wash in exactly the right place with clean edges.
