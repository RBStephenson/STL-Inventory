RoboCop — Film-accurate Detroit steel means cold, blue-grey metallic with subtle aqua-to-purple interference — not the toy-silver you see on action figures. Turbo Dork Ice To Never over a dark silver base reproduces that on-screen color ghost. The cinematic lighting rig is baked in: a hard red/magenta bounce from the left, cold blue fill from the right — do not rely on ambient room lighting; build both into the paint. Murphy's exposed skin is barely human, and the punk below is all impact, blood, and bright green hair. Values first, always — the armor reads flat at any distance if the recesses don't go near-black.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal — Murphy is rugged, masculine, and barely-human. The red zenithal underpainting reads as suppressed warmth — the ghost of the living man underneath the machine. Keep highlights restrained; this isn't a heroic complexion, it's a surviving one.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; easy to correct Can read flat at this scale without tight value range First-time painters; speed priority; any skin tone
Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → Flesh Wash 202 all over (or thinned FW Crimson Ink 1:3 as red glaze) → thin flesh layers over top. Skip magenta.

Underlying warmth and exertion flush; reads masculine and world-worn Needs good zenithal — poor zenithal = muddy result Rugged male, military, survivor, weathered — Murphy exactly

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → FW Crimson Ink recesses → thin flesh layers.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers Too bright and heroic for Murphy's pallor; magenta would over-pink Fair/feminine, protagonist/hero figures, luminous complexions
No Freckling: Murphy's exposed skin is too small at 1:6 and the pallid cyborg tone should read smooth and slightly waxy. Skip freckling entirely — add only a subtle gloss varnish over the lips and a very thin cool wash under the jawline to suggest the machine beneath.

Armor — Film-Accurate Blue-Grey Steel

The 1987 suit is NOT silver — it's a dark blue-grey with aqua, purple, and gold interference that shifts depending on angle and lighting. Turbo Dork Ice To Never reproduces this over a dark metallic zenithal. Cinematic OSL from two sources: red/magenta from the left, cold blue from the right.

Foundation — Dark Metallic Base
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm, 30+ PSI. Full coverage of all armor surfaces. The black must be solid — Turbo Dork's color shift only activates over dark surfaces, so any grey or light areas beneath will kill the effect.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~3% value — black primer base
⚠ NOTE: Do NOT thin primer. It must go on undiluted at 30+ PSI through a 0.5mm nozzle. Thinning primer breaks its adhesion and coverage properties.
Step 2 · Airbrush

Dark Silver Metallic Zenithal

VMC 77.702 Duraluminium thinned 1:1 with distilled water — zenithal pass from above and slightly forward. Leave deep recesses, panel line undersides, and joint gaps pure black. Upper armor panels should reach approximately 40–50% metallic coverage. This is NOT full coverage — the black underneath is load-bearing for the Turbo Dork step.

Duraluminium 77.702
Vallejo Metal Color
~25% value — dark metallic base
✦ TIP: Use the zenithal to establish your light source direction — slightly forward and above. This should match the natural overhead-plus-forward angle of a display light.
Turbo Dork Color Shift — The Film-Accurate Effect
Step 3 · Airbrush

Turbo Dork Ice To Never — Color Shift Layer

Ice To Never thinned 1:1 with Turbo Dork Thinning Medium. Apply from the same zenithal angle as step 2 — 2 to 3 thin coats, allowing each to dry between passes. This activates over the dark metallic base and produces a blue → greenish-blue → purple shift at different viewing angles, reproducing the film suit's interference effect. Do NOT apply over any areas that are not dark — if silver migrated into recesses, mask them first.

Ice To Never
Turbo Dork Turboshift
Color shift — blue → greenish-blue → purple
✦ TIP: View the piece from multiple angles after each coat. The shift should be visible when you rock the figure. If it looks flat/dull, it's either over a too-light base or needs another coat.
⚠ NOTE: ONLY thin Turbo Dork with Turbo Dork Thinning Medium, not water. Water can break the interference suspension.
Step 3b · Airbrush — Optional Gold Layer

Galaxia — Adding the Gold Interference Note

Optional: one very thin coat of Galaxia thinned 1:1 with TD Medium, zenithal angle only, hitting the very top third of upper armor panels. Galaxia shifts blue → purple → gold and adds the subtle warm gold notes visible in the 1987 suit at certain angles. Do NOT layer this heavily — it should only be visible as a warm glint at extreme angles.

Step 8 · Wash

Panel Line Wash — Armor Seams

Apply Black Wash 200 undiluted into the recessed seams of the armor panels with a fine liner brush. Pull the wash by capillary action — do not brush it around. The wash should flow into the line and stop at the panel surface. Wipe any excess that migrates onto flat surfaces before it dries.

✦ TIP: For the very finest panel lines, Tamiya Panel Line Accent Black 87131 (enamel) gives finer capillary action. Apply only over a sealed surface, remove excess with enamel thinner on a cotton swab.
Cinematic OSL — Build the Light Into the Figure
Step 9 · Filter

Red/Magenta OSL — Left-Side Panels

Seal armor with satin varnish first. Then FW Crimson Ink 513 thinned 1:5 with water — apply as a very thin glaze to the left-facing surfaces of the shoulder, helmet, chest plate, and left arm. This simulates a hard red bounce light from the left (matching the render's red-magenta practical light). 1–2 thin passes; wipe back the edges with a damp flat brush to keep it from pooling.

FW Crimson Ink 513
FW Acrylic Inks
Thinned 1:5 — red OSL filter
⚠ NOTE: Never use PA Transparent Red thinned for this — it shifts to magenta. Use FW Crimson Ink only for glazed red applications.
Step 10 · Filter

Blue OSL — Right-Side and Helmet Top Panels

Transparent Blue 046 thinned 1:5 — apply as a thin glaze to right-facing surfaces and the top of the helmet. This simulates the cold blue fill light from the right. It should subtly push those surfaces cooler and bluer. Again, 1–2 passes maximum — too heavy and it kills the metallic base.

Transparent Blue 046
Pro Acryl
Thinned 1:5 — blue OSL filter
✦ TIP: The OSL is baked in, not reliant on display lighting. Your figure should look cinematic under any light — even overhead room light — because the lighting information lives in the paint.
Step 11 · Finish

Varnish — Satin Overall, Targeted Gloss

Satin varnish over all armor. Then spot-apply gloss varnish with a brush to any particularly prominent edge highlights and specular catch points on the helmet and shoulder plates. The contrast between matte recesses and gloss highlights amplifies the sense of polished metal.

OSL — Building Cinematic Lighting Into the Figure
Technique · OSL Filter

Two-Source Cinematic Lighting

OSL (Object Source Lighting) simulates a specific light source hitting the figure, independent of where the viewer's display light is. For RoboCop, the render uses two practicals: hard red-magenta from the left, cold blue from the right. After establishing the metallic base and sealing it, apply the OSL as transparent tinted filter glazes — very thin, two or three passes maximum. The key rule: only the surfaces geometrically facing the light source receive the tint. Front-facing surfaces get neither or both, depending on which light dominates at that plane. Getting this right means the figure looks cinematic under any ambient light.

✦ TIP: Hold the figure at the angle of each light source while applying. Your brush should be going toward the light-facing planes and nowhere else. It's a targeting exercise.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: pressure too high, distance too close, cup overfilled, or needle pulled back too far at once. Effect: paint pushes back into cup or spits bubbles. Fix: reduce PSI to 12–15, move back to 15cm, reduce cup fill to 1/3 max, use shorter needle pulls with more passes instead of one heavy pull.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: inconsistent trigger pressure, moving the airbrush at uneven speed, starting/stopping the stroke over the model. Effect: visible stripes or hot spots. Fix: start and end each stroke off the model, keep airbrush moving at constant speed. Rotate the model rather than changing airbrush angle for large coverage areas.

Turbo Dork — Color Shift Not Visible

Cause: base wasn't dark enough (grey or pale surface underneath), paint was applied too thick in one coat, or thinned with water instead of TD Medium. Fix: Ensure primer black is truly dark in the application area. Re-prime if needed. Thin only with TD Medium. Apply in 2–3 very thin coats and view from multiple angles after each coat.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting on metallic armor, wet blending on skin, glazing for OSL and blood, drybrushing for base texture, controlled enamel wash.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Hard Edge Highlight on Armor Panels

Load a flat or liner brush with chrome/metallic. Drag the side of the tip along the edge of an armor panel — the goal is a thin, consistent bright line at 90%+ value. Before the stroke: wipe the brush to near-dry on a paper towel after loading. The drag should deposit just enough paint to catch the edge. Common failure mode: brush too loaded → wide blob instead of line. If it's too wide, wipe back immediately with a damp brush.

✦ TIP: These highlights are what makes the armor read as polished steel from display distance. If they look too bright up close, step back — at arm's length they should look exactly right.
Technique · Wet Blending

Wet Blending — Expert Acrylics Workflow

Expert Acrylics stay wet and workable longer than standard acrylics. Apply the shadow color, then immediately apply the mid-tone color adjacent to it. Use a clean, slightly damp flat brush to stroke into the join — the two colors will blend. Work quickly in sections and keep the surface slightly damp (a breath on the figure can buy 30 more seconds). Ideal for skin and leather on a 1:6 face at this scale.

Technique · Targeted Gloss — Blood Effects

Selective Gloss for Blood

Gloss varnish with a fine brush, applied only to blood surfaces. The key is sharp cutoff between gloss blood and matte skin/ground. Use the side of the brush tip to feather the gloss edge exactly at the blood boundary. Gloss on the wrong surface (matte flesh) immediately kills the realism — clean it up with a damp brush before it dries.

Technique · Enamel Wash Control

Villainy Ink / Tamiya Enamel Panel Lines

Apply enamel washes only to surfaces sealed with acrylic varnish (matte or gloss). Let the enamel flow into recesses by capillary action — do not spread it with the brush. Allow 10–15 minutes, then remove excess from raised surfaces by stroking with a flat brush lightly moistened with enamel thinner. The enamel in recesses is protected by the seal; the enamel on raised surfaces comes off cleanly. Cure time 4+ hours before re-sealing.

⚠ NOTE: Never apply enamel over unsealed acrylic paint. It will dissolve and lift the layer beneath.
Technique · Drybrush — Base Texture

Drybrushing for Base Surface Grit

Load brush with paint, then wipe aggressively on paper towel until virtually no paint comes off. Stroke lightly over the textured base surface — paint deposits only on the peaks of the texture. For Expert Acrylics: heavy-body formula means they hold on a near-dry brush exceptionally well. Apply in overlapping short strokes in multiple directions to avoid a grain pattern.
