Spider-Man — Groudeffected's sculpt leans cartoony and expressive, which means we paint it the same way: cel-shade thinking, not smooth realism. Wider value steps, more abrupt transitions, saturated clean color zones. The figure's two dominant colors — red and blue — live at opposite ends of the warm/cool spectrum and need strong internal value structure to read as three-dimensional. The iconic white lenses are the face; they need to pop. Light source is zenithal (directly overhead), making the top of every surface the lightest. Primary finishing challenge is the embossed web pattern — paint your suit values first, varnish, then line the web grooves last so the lines sit crisp on top.

Red Suit

The red panels cover the head, chest, upper arms, and lower legs. The canon color is a vivid primary red — warm, saturated, full of pop. For a cartoony sculpt, we push the value range wider than feels comfortable and keep the shadow/highlight transitions deliberately abrupt.

Cartoony Cel-Shade Approach — This sculpt rewards a more graphic technique than a realistic display figure. Rather than smooth, blended transitions between values, aim for defined zones: a clear shadow zone, a clear mid-tone, and a clear highlight zone. The transitions can be soft or hard — but both zones should be readable as distinct. Think about how Spider-Man looks in a comic panel: strong light on top, bold shadow underneath, no wishy-washy middle. That's the target.

Color Build
Step 3 · Airbrush

Bold Pyrrole Red — Base Coat

Bold Pyrrole Red 003 thinned 1:3 to 1:4 (paint:water). Apply zenithal from above — hit top planes first with the most coverage, trail off on side planes, and leave shadow zones dark. Three to four thin passes over all red areas. The grey zenithal and black prime should show through in recesses and shadow zones; that grey is your shadow undertone and it's exactly right. Do not chase full opacity on the first pass. Let the form breathe.

Graphic Mid-Highlight — Orange Red

Orange Red S31, thinned 1:2. Apply to all raised planes of the red suit: the top of the shoulder, the outer surface of the arm, the front of the chest, the top of the thigh and shin. For the cartoony look, the transition from shadow to this highlight can be relatively abrupt — you don't need to feather it out perfectly. A clean-ish edge actually reads more like a comic book panel and suits this sculpt. Cover the raised planes generously; the dark zones should only be visible in the recesses.

Lenses

The white lenses are Spider-Man's face. They need to do everything — convey expression, catch light, and pop against the red mask. The cartoony sculpt gives you large, expressive lens shapes to work with. The approach: crisp, bright white with simple cool shading. Gloss finish is mandatory — the lenses must read as actual optical material, not painted plastic.

Foundation
Step 1 · Brush

Lens Perimeter Shadow

Coal Black 002 thinned 1:3. Using a fine round brush, paint a thin dark edge around the inside perimeter of each lens opening — not a thick frame, just a 0.5–1mm dark border that lifts the lens away from the surrounding red mask. This creates depth and keeps the white from bleeding optically into the red. Work carefully around the entire lens boundary.

Coal Black 002
Pro Acryl
~5% value — perimeter shadow
Step 2 · Brush

White Lens Fill

Bold Titanium White 001 thinned 1:1 for first coat, then near-undiluted for second coat. Fill the entire lens area with white. Two coats to get solid, opaque coverage. Make sure there are no streaks or visible brush strokes — these will read as surface contamination, not texture. Let each coat dry fully before the next.

Bold Titanium White 001
Pro Acryl
~98% value — solid lens fill
✦ TIP: If you have a hairy or rough brush that leaves texture, switch to a flat synthetic brush for the white fill — flat brushes leave a smoother surface on flat-ish areas like the lens.
Lens Shading
Step 3 · Brush

Cool Lens Gradient — Faded Ultramarine

Faded Ultramarine 013 thinned very heavily — 1:6 to 1:8 (paint:water). This is almost a water-color consistency. Apply around the lower and side edges of each lens, building a subtle cool shadow that implies the lens curving away from the direct light. The upper-center of each lens should remain the brightest white. Leave that area completely untouched at this stage. One or two gentle passes only — let it pool at the lower arc of the lens.

Faded Ultramarine 013
Pro Acryl
extremely thin glaze — cool lens shadow
✦ TIP: Faded Ultramarine 013 is already desaturated, which is exactly what you want here. A vivid blue lens shading looks artificial. The desaturated blue-grey reads as "shadow inside a white reflective material" — accurate and natural.
Step 4 · Brush

Strengthen Center White

Bold Titanium White 001, near-undiluted. Re-apply to the upper-center and central zones of each lens to strengthen the white where the gradient has maybe washed it down. This sharpens the contrast between the shaded perimeter and the bright central zone — which is what creates the illusion of a convex reflective surface.

Bold Titanium White 001
Pro Acryl
~98% value — central brightness restore
Finish
Step 5 · Brush

Specular Catch-Light Dot

Bold Titanium White 001 undiluted. Using the very tip of a 00 or 000 brush, place a single small dot in the upper-center of each lens — roughly where a light source would catch a reflective surface. This is optional but adds significant life to the lenses, giving them an almost pupil-like focal point. Both lenses must match exactly in position.

Step 6 · Finish

Targeted Gloss Varnish — Lenses Only

Apply gloss varnish very precisely to the lens surfaces only. Use a small flat brush and stay within the lens boundary — do NOT get gloss on the surrounding red mask. The gloss transforms the painted surface into something that reads like actual optical glass or plastic. This single step does more for the "character recognition" of the figure than almost anything else in the guide.

⚠ NOTE: The rest of the suit stays satin. The lenses are the ONLY gloss area on the figure. Gloss on the suit panels destroys the fabric illusion.

Web Details

Two distinct jobs here: the embossed web pattern on the suit, and the web-shooter hardware on the wrists. The web lines go on last — after suit painting is complete and varnished. This sequence is critical.

Web Lines — Sequence Note
✦ CRITICAL SEQUENCE: Web lines go AFTER the suit is fully painted and satin-varnished. The varnish gives you a forgiving surface — if a web line strays, you can wipe it off while wet and the varnish protects the paint underneath. Painting web lines into fresh paint risks lifting and mudding. Do not skip the varnish step.
Step 1 · Brush

Web Lines on Red Panels

Coal Black 002 thinned 1:4 (paint:water). Using a fine liner brush (size 10/0 or 20/0), trace along the sculpted grooves at the base of each raised web line — NOT on top of the web line itself. The web lines on this sculpt are raised ridges; the shadow should live in the groove beside each ridge, not across its top. This gives a graphic, cel-outlined effect that suits the cartoony sculpt. Work in sections — a few grooves at a time — to keep control.

Coal Black 002
Pro Acryl
~5% value — web line outline
✦ TIP: Load the liner brush, then wipe the side on your palette to reduce volume to a fine bead. Drag rather than dab. The satin varnish underneath lets the thin paint flow smoothly. If a line goes crooked, wipe immediately with a slightly damp cotton swab.
Step 2 · Brush

Web Lines on Blue Panels

Same Coal Black 002 at 1:4, same technique. The contrast between black web lines and blue may feel stronger than on the red panels — this is correct. On the blue, if the pure black feels too harsh, you can switch to a 1:1 mix of Coal Black 002 + Dark Navy Blue AMP-005 for a slightly softer dark line that's still very dark but has a blue temperature that harmonizes with the panel color beneath.

Base

NYC urban street environment — asphalt, concrete, a street sign, and traffic signal hardware. Dark, gritty, textured. The goal is a base that reads as a believable New York moment without competing with the bright figure above it. Keep values on the base lower and more muted than the suit.

Dark Base — Near-Black Asphalt

Coal Black 002 thinned 1:2 — full coverage of all base surfaces. Asphalt in the city is very dark, especially at night or in overcast light. Go darker than you think. This base establishes that the figure is above an urban ground, not floating on a neutral surface.

Airbrush Skills

This guide uses the airbrush for priming, zenithal foundation, and the red and blue base color builds. The cel-shade highlights are done by brush — so the airbrush carries the value structure, and the brush does the graphic finish work.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: PSI too high for the nozzle distance, or paint too thin. The airstream overwhelms the wet paint layer. Fix: lower PSI by 3–5, increase distance to 15–18cm. If paint is very thin, add a small amount of paint back to the cup and test on a card before continuing.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: Moving too slowly or stopping while the trigger is depressed. Results in heavier deposits at pass starts and ends. Fix: always start the airbrush movement before opening the needle, and close the needle before stopping the movement. Keep a consistent speed across the surface.

Paint Too Thin — Runs

Cause: Ratio off, or too many passes without drying time. Thin paint runs on vertical surfaces and loses all opacity. Fix: thicken the mix slightly (add paint), reduce PSI, increase distance, and do shorter passes. Let each pass tack before the next.

Brush Skills

The brush work in this guide falls into four main techniques: cel-shade edge highlighting, liner brush web lines, targeted gloss finish work, and drybrushing on the base. Each serves a specific purpose and all of them show up in the finished figure.

Technique · Cel-Shade Edge Highlighting

Graphic Highlight for Cartoony Figures

Unlike a realistic figure where highlights are blended smoothly, cel-shade highlighting keeps the transition abrupt. Load a size 1 or 2 round brush with a thinned highlight color (1:2 paint:water). Wipe the brush on the palette to a medium load — not dripping, not dry. Apply the highlight to the raised plane with smooth, deliberate strokes. Let the edge of the highlight zone be visible but not sharp. If the result looks too crisp, use a barely-damp clean brush to soften just the very edge — a single pass of the brush tip is enough. The goal is a defined zone, not an invisible blend. Step back frequently to check at arm's length — the highlight should be clearly readable from 50cm.

✦ TIP: "Too much blending" is a real risk on cartoony sculpts. If you keep blending past the point where the highlight zone is distinct, you end up with a realistic-style gradual transition that fights the sculpt's intentional stylization. Know when to stop.
Technique · Liner Brush — Web Lines

Consistent Fine Lines on Curved Surfaces

A quality liner brush (10/0 or 20/0) loaded with well-thinned paint (1:4) is the tool for web lines. The loading technique matters: fill the brush fully, then wipe the outer bristles on the palette so you have a fine point with paint still in the body of the bristle mass. As you draw the line, the bristle mass feeds paint to the tip at a consistent rate — keeping the line even for longer. On curved suit surfaces, rotate the figure rather than twisting your wrist into an awkward position. Let the figure come to the brush, not the other way around. Keep strokes short: 5–8mm at a time, lift, reposition, continue.

✦ TIP: Liner brushes dry out fast at the tip. Between sections, re-load and wipe again — a dried tip produces a scratchy, inconsistent line. The satin varnish on the suit surface lets you wipe any stray lines off immediately with a damp swab.
Technique · Targeted Gloss

Precision Varnish Application for Lenses

Applying gloss to just the lenses without contaminating the surrounding red suit requires a small flat brush (size 0 or 1 flat) and deliberate edge control. Load the brush with gloss medium, wipe off excess, and apply to the lens center first — then carefully work toward the edge, stopping just at the boundary. Turn the figure to check from multiple angles before the gloss dries. If any gloss gets onto the red suit, wipe immediately with a barely-damp brush before it sets — the satin varnish below will protect the painted surface.

⚠ NOTE: Do NOT apply gloss to the suit. The visual difference between satin suit and gloss lenses is what reads as "optics" — without that contrast, the lenses just look wet instead of lens-like.
Technique · Stipple Drybrushing — Base Texture

Concrete and Asphalt Surface Texture

Load a stiff flat or fan brush with a small amount of grey paint, then wipe it thoroughly on a paper towel until almost no paint comes off on the towel. Pounce the near-dry brush onto the base surface with random, light up-down motions. The remaining pigment catches only on surface peaks and texture details, building up a grainy, particulate texture that reads convincingly as concrete aggregate. Two to three passes of this over the wash layer creates the look of worn city pavement.

✦ TIP: "Almost too dry" is the right state for this technique. If the brush is leaving obvious paint strokes rather than random dots, there's too much paint on the brush. Wipe more.
