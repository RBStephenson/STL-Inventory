Cassie Hack — slasher hunter from Tim Seeley's Hack/Slash comic series. Fair warm skin with the exertion flush of someone who's been in a fight all night; values first means reading rugged and world-worn, not soft and idealized. Zenithal light source from directly above: her pale face catches top-light hard while deep shadows pool under her chin and in the creases of her outfit. Priority materials: skin (Method 2B Red Zenithal), high-contrast dark fabric with pleat differentiation, heavy dried body splatter, fresh axe blood as satin narrative contrast. Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal. Cassie has a fair warm complexion, but she's a fighter — her skin should read as warm and flushed, with exertion visible in the cheeks and knuckles. The red zenithal creates that underlying warmth automatically, giving her the look of someone who's been sprinting through the dark rather than sitting for a portrait. Magenta (Method 2A) would skew too soft and feminine for the character's energy.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; easy to correct mid-session Can read flat without enough layers at 1:6 scale First-time painters, 28mm tabletop, speed priority

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → red ink into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers needed Skews too soft and pink for Cassie's tougher character energy Fair / feminine / heroic protagonist display figures
★ Recommended for Cassie

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink glaze all over → thin flesh layers over top. The red underlayer creates natural exertion flush and survivor warmth.

Natural warmth and flush; zenithal does the heavy lifting on value Same zenithal quality dependency as Method 2A — nail the zenithal first Rugged, weathered, world-worn figures; survivor and fighter skin types

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (vein areas) → seal → red wash (capillary zones). Filter variant uses Speedpaint Medium, not water.

Most realistic tonal mapping; three distinct undertone zones Slowest method — three varnish layers and dry times required Historical, portrait, maximum-realism display pieces
Freckling / Speckling: After skin is sealed, thin Burnt Sienna 059 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots, not lines. Target nose bridge, cheeks, and the backs of hands. One pass → step back → assess. Cassie's aesthetic reads as physically real rather than clean — moderate freckle density suits her well.

Blood Effects

Cassie is a slasher hunter — heavy blood coverage is part of the character. All blood here is dried: dark brown-red, matte, textured where thick. Not fresh. Not glossy. Not bright red.

✦ DRIED VS FRESH — THE KEY DISTINCTION: Fresh blood is bright red and slightly glossy. Dried blood is dark brown-red — almost black in thick deposits, transitioning to dark rust-red in thin layers. Cassie's blood has dried. If anything looks too red or too glossy, it needs another pass of darker color or matte varnish.
Blood Spatter — Airbrush
Step 1 · Airbrush — Effects

Large Splatter Pattern

Dry Blood WP3164 thinned 1:3. PSI 8–12, 0.2mm nozzle, 3–5cm from the surface, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts produce micro-droplets that scatter realistically. Aim for torso, arms, and face. Blood spatters radially from an impact — concentrate on one side of the figure as if from a single strike direction. Start with larger drops (slightly more trigger), then add micro-spatter over the top. Rotate the figure 30–40° between each burst — real fight splatter comes from multiple directions.

Blood Spatter Technique
Technique · Effects

Airbrush Blood Spatter

Set PSI to 8–12 (lower than standard work). Hold airbrush 3–5cm from the figure surface — much closer than normal. Close the needle almost completely. Short, sharp trigger pulls produce droplets; longer pulls produce streaks. Build up in multiple passes from different angles — rotate the figure 30–40° between each burst so the spatter reads as coming from multiple impact events during a fight.

✦ TIP: Practice on a white card held at the same distance first. The gap between convincing splatter and chaotic mess is two seconds of trigger time. One pass, step back, assess.
⚠ NOTE: Mask Cassie's face with adhesive putty if you want to control the face separately from heavy body spatter. Remove carefully after drying, before varnishing.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: needle pulled back too far while trigger is depressed. Effect: large bubble of paint erupts from the nozzle. Fix: maintain a consistent, small needle pull. Close the needle before releasing the trigger at the end of a pass. Don't overfill a side-feed cup.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: inconsistent trigger pressure or movement speed, or paint too thick. Effect: visible striped bands in what should be a smooth gradient. Fix: keep the airbrush moving at a consistent speed, and match trigger pull to movement speed — slow on trigger when slowing your hand. Add thinner in small increments if the paint is too thick.

Paint Runs / Too Thin

Cause: ratio too high, or PSI too high at close distance. Effect: paint runs down the figure, obscuring the zenithal. Fix: add paint to reduce the water content. Increase distance or reduce PSI. On the zenithal, err toward too thin — you can always add more passes, but you can't un-flood a zenithal.

Brush Skills

Edge highlighting, thin layering, capillary wash placement, and blood stippling — the brush techniques used in this guide.

Technique · Brush

Edge Highlighting — Black Surfaces

Load a fine flat or chisel brush with your highlight mix. Wipe the brush until nearly dry — only a small amount of paint remains. Drag the loaded tip along the fold edge or corner. Paint deposits on the raised edge only because pressure is light. Work a small section at a time. On Cassie's fabric, the highlight should be a fine even line — not a blob, not a wash. For torn short edges, use short 2–3mm irregular strokes rather than a continuous drag, replicating torn fabric rather than a clean sewn edge.

✦ TIP: Vary the highlight density along torn edges for a worn, irregular look — no two torn edges in a real garment are identical.
Technique · Wash

Capillary Wash Placement

For all wash steps — boot creases, wood grain, fabric fold lines — use capillary action, not brushwork. Touch the loaded wash brush to the edge of the target recess and let the paint flow in on its own. Do not scrub or push the wash. Wipe any pooling on raised surfaces immediately with a barely-damp clean brush before it dries.

⚠ NOTE: Over-brushing a wash while wet activates the surfactants and creates a tide-mark edge as it dries. Touch and release — don't scrub.
Technique · Effects

Blood Stippling

Use an old brush with splayed, irregular bristles. Load with near-undiluted Murder Scene WP2057 or Dry Blood WP3164. Dab the brush directly down — don't drag. The irregular bristle tips create the granular texture of dried blood rather than a smooth painted surface. Build up in multiple layers: first pass lighter and sparser; second pass denser in the impact zones. Work from dark (Murder Scene) at the center to slightly lighter (Bloodmoon Red WP4006) at the edges.

✦ TIP: If you don't have a ratty brush handy, deliberately splay the bristles of a synthetic brush by pressing the tip firmly against the palette. The resulting irregular splay replicates the stippling texture.
Technique · Brush

Thin Layering — Skin Build

Every skin layer in this guide is applied thinly (1:1 to 1:2 paint:water) and built up in multiple passes. Each layer should be semi-transparent — you should still be able to see the value structure of the zenithal through it. Paint that is too thick covers the zenithal completely and flattens the result. Three thin layers always produces a better result than one thick layer.

✦ TIP: A well-thinned skin layer should look almost too transparent when first applied. As it dries, it reaches correct opacity. If the zenithal disappears after one coat, the paint was too thick.
