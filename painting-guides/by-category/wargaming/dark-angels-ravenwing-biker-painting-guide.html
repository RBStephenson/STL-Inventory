Ravenwing — The Hunters — Black-armored fast attack specialists who pursue the Fallen across the galaxy aboard jet-black Space Marine bikes. Unlike the ash-worn black of the Shattered Legions survivors, the Ravenwing black is 40k clean: sleek, matte midnight, with cool highlights that read as the dark catching ambient light rather than battle damage. The bike is as much the subject as the rider — significant steel and chrome surfaces that contrast directly against the matte black. The red accent on any cloth (robe ends, tabard details, wing motifs) is the same ceremony-red as the Deathwing: wine dark, not parade bright. Zenithal light from above. Priority surfaces: armor black, bike chrome, red accents, chapter heraldry.

Armor

Midnight black ceramite — clean and sleek, not weathered or dusty. The Ravenwing operates in the 41st millennium with access to Chapter armories; their armor is maintained and lacquered. The black needs subtle cool highlights that read as the surface catching ambient light at speed — a dark blue-grey edge, a cooler near-white specular, without any of the warm dusty tones of the Raven Guard or the warm bone overtones of the Deathwing. This is a different 40k black from anything else in the collection.

The Bike

The Space Marine bike is as much a signature element as the rider — and for the Ravenwing it's a primary visual subject. The matte black bike body contrasts against chrome exhaust pipes, steel engine detail, brushed silver intake housings, and the occasional red warning stripe or tyre valve. Getting the bike right means understanding which surfaces are intentionally matte (the main body), which are metallic (mechanical components), and which are rubber (tyres). Different materials, clearly differentiated.

Body & Fairings — Matte Black
Step 1 · Brush

Body Edge Highlights — Cool Grey

The bike body stays black from the prime (no additional base coat needed). Apply Payne's Grey S02 (thinned 1:3) as a subtle edge highlight on the raised edges of the body fairings, front cowl, and mudguard. Then Dark Cool Grey 046 (thinned 1:4) as a very fine second line on the sharpest edges. At display distance the bike body reads as matte black with subtle edge definition — it should not read as a different color. Keep the edge highlights even finer than on the rider armor; the bike is a large background element relative to the riding figure.

Troubleshooting

Chrome Exhaust Streaky / Not Reflective

Cause: either VMC Chrome was not agitated (pigment settled, giving mostly medium), or it was applied over an insufficiently dark base (the black prime may have been grey rather than true black, reducing the reflectivity contrast). Fix: agitate the bottle and cup thoroughly before each pass. Chrome applied over a true matte black prime reads with significantly more reflectivity than over grey or semi-gloss surfaces — the dark undercoat provides maximum contrast for the metallic pigment. If the pipes are already dry and streaky, a second pass post-agitation at 1:1 will layer flat and even the coverage.

Bike Body Edge Highlight Too Visible

Cause: edge highlight on fairing applied too heavily — reading as a colored line rather than a subtle sheen. The bike fairing curves are gentle, not hard armor-plate edges, so the highlight needs to be more of a ghost than a line. Fix: use a very dry brush with minimal paint, almost a glaze consistency, and apply at a low angle. If the line is already dry, a very thin glaze of Black Wash 200 (1:6 — almost pure water with a drop of wash) over the affected fairing area will knock it back without disturbing the surrounding black.

Cool Gray Highlights on Armor Look Wrong vs. DA Green

Cause: the Ravenwing is typically displayed alongside DA Primaris (green) and Deathwing (bone) — all three use different highlight temperatures. The cool grey on the Ravenwing can look cold or stark when first placed next to the warm green and warm bone. This is correct. The visual tension between cool black / warm green / warm bone IS the DA army palette — it reads intentionally at display distance. If you are seeing it while painting up close, step back to arm's length. The cool/warm contrast reads as a design choice, not a mistake.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: cool-tone edge highlighting on black armor, differentiating matte vs. chrome surfaces, subtle fairing edge effects, brush-only bolter steel.

Technique · Cool Black Highlights

Edge Highlighting Ravenwing Armor at 28mm

The three-stage cool hierarchy (Payne's Grey S02 → Dark Cool Grey 046 → Bright Neutral Grey 045) uses very similar values with a consistent cool temperature. At this low value range the transitions are subtle — the highlight reads as depth rather than a colored line, which is exactly correct for black armor. Load a size 0 brush with Payne's Grey S02 thinned 1:2, wipe to a fine point, and stroke along each plate edge in a single motion. The key discipline: resist the urge to brighten further. The specular Bright Neutral Grey 045 dot should be the only highlight visible from arm's length on the armor. Everything below it is transition tone that the eye perceives subconsciously.

✦ TIP: If you've also painted Deathwing or Raven Guard, you will notice this scheme's edge highlights feel "invisibly dark" compared to the warm ivory or warm grey of those schemes. This is intentional and correct. Trust the Bright Neutral Grey 045 specular dot to carry the highlight read. Don't compensate by brightening intermediate steps.
Technique · Material Differentiation

Matte Black vs. Chrome vs. Steel

The Ravenwing model has three surface types that must all read as clearly different materials: matte black armor/fairing, chrome exhaust pipes, and steel engine components. These are differentiated by surface finish, not just color. Matte black: no gloss, subtle cool edge highlights only. Chrome exhaust: airbrushed VMC Chrome = inherently shiny metallic surface. Steel engine: Gunmetal → Aluminium gradient = lighter but less pure reflectivity than chrome. Ensure these distinctions survive the final varnish pass: matte varnish on the black and steel, semi-gloss on the chrome pipes, full gloss on the eye lenses only.

Technique · Fairing Curves

Edge Highlights on Curved Bike Fairings

The bike fairing curves are convex — they need a highlight that follows the highest point of the curve rather than a hard plate edge. Load a flat brush sideways and drag it along the highest curve ridge with almost no pressure — the brush side-loads paint onto the high point. Alternatively, load a round brush very lightly and drag the heel of the brush (the very tip) along the curve peak with a light, almost floating pressure. Either method produces the subtle edge effect needed. For concave fairing areas (air intakes, cutouts), no edge highlight is needed — these read as shadow zones that the surrounding matte black establishes naturally.

Grimdark Weathering

A Ravenwing biker does not pull into a garage for routine maintenance. The bike accumulates road debris, exhaust soot, hydraulic leaks, and things best not identified on the wheel arches. The rider's armour picks up grime in the joints and blood spatter on the forward-facing surfaces. These techniques push the model from tabletop-clean to genuinely field-worn — and on a black-armoured vehicle the contrast between the paint scheme and the weathering layer is surprisingly dramatic. Two paths: Villainy Ink enamel or John Blanche Masterclass acrylic.

⚠ ENAMEL WARNING — READ BEFORE OPENING A VILLAINY INK:
Villainy Inks are oil/enamel-based — they are NOT acrylic. Applied to bare or unsealed acrylic paint they will soften and lift it. You must seal the model with varnish before any Villainy Ink step. Cleanup is with enamel thinner or white spirit only. Dry time: 4–12 hours. Keep a dedicated enamel-only brush and never rinse it in your acrylic water cup.
Choose Your Path
☠ Path A — Enamel

Villainy Ink Path

The enamel path is particularly effective on the bike itself — the vehicle has large flat panels, deep mechanical recesses, and a wheel arch that becomes a focal point for mud and debris. Selective removal from the flat fairing panels while leaving the undercarriage and mechanical recesses loaded with grime creates an immediate narrative contrast between the armoured surface and what lives underneath.

You'll need: MPAR-V01 Matte Varnish, VI-001 Goon's Grime, VI-007 Dystopian Brown, VI-002 Sector Rust, VI-005 Ichor of the Damned, VI-004 Carrion Crimson (optional), enamel thinner, cotton swabs, dedicated enamel brush.

🖌 Path B — Acrylic

John Blanche Masterclass Path

All acrylic, water cleanup, no seal required. WP4008 Grimdark Shadow and WP4009 Filth Wash do the heavy lifting for grime and mud respectively. Less selective control in the recesses, but the result on black armour and a black bike is still very effective — dark on dark grimdark is all about texture and finish differentiation. Skip to the John Blanche Acrylic Path section below.

You'll need: WP4008 Grimdark Shadow, WP4009 Filth Wash, WP4005 Grime Wash.

Trouble Shooting
Enamel lifted the black armour or bike paint
Cause: Applied Villainy Ink to an unsealed surface.
Fix: Let the enamel dry fully (4–12 hours). Touch up with Coal Black 002 and the appropriate highlight colours. Seal thoroughly before any further enamel work. The seal step is not optional.
Undercarriage grime looks too uniform — no depth
Cause: Applied VI-001 or Filth Wash as a single even layer with no variation.
Fix: Once the base grime layer is dry, add a second concentrated pass of the same ink into the absolute deepest corners of the wheel arch and undercarriage. The variation between areas with one layer and areas with two creates the illusion of accumulated debris rather than a wash.
Wheel arch blood spatter looks like a smear, not spatter
Cause: Applied VI-004 Carrion Crimson with a brush stroke rather than stippling.
Fix: Load a stiff old brush or toothbrush with a small amount of VI-004 and use a finger to flick the bristles in a controlled direction. Practice on paper first. The directional element — forward-pointing spatter marks from the wheel rotation direction — is what makes it read as a velocity effect rather than applied paint.
Enamel thinner hazed the chrome exhaust finish
Cause: Enamel thinner contacted the chrome pipes during selective removal on adjacent surfaces.
Fix: Use white spirit only (not acetone). Apply thinner with a cotton swab that has been squeezed dry on a paper towel before touching the model — minimal thinner, maximum control. If hazed, a thin re-coat of MPAM-003 Gloss Varnish over the chrome once everything is cured will restore the reflective finish.
