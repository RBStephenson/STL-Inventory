Raven Guard Shattered Legions Survivor — A warrior who walked out of the ash clouds of Isstvan V carrying nothing but his armor, his bolter, and the weight of ten thousand dead brothers. The aesthetic is restrained and grim: warm black ceramite with no gloss and no ceremony, scratched white shoulder pads, and red optical lenses burning in the dark. Unlike the Iron Hands (whose black carries a cold blue-grey undertone), the Raven Guard's black reads warm and dusty — weathered by volcanic ash rather than glacial steel. The visual difference between the two legions lives entirely in the highlight color temperature. Zenithal light from directly above; no OSL. Priority materials: armor tone, white shoulder panels, lens glow.

Warm black ceramite — distinguished from the Iron Hands' cold blue-grey by warm-toned highlight colors rather than a cool glaze. The black reads dusty and ash-worn, not glacial. No color airbrush passes beyond zenithal: all the tonal work is done with warm grey edge highlights and an ivory specular.

Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm. Full all-over coverage of every component together: armor, boltgun, backpack, base. No bare resin or plastic showing. This primer is the permanent shadow that lives in every recess for the rest of the paint job. Take it seriously.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~5% value — permanent recess shadow; do not thin
✦ TIP: Never thin primer. Apply undiluted. Thinning degrades adhesion and defeats the purpose of primer entirely. This coat is your deep shadow — the recesses stay this dark forever.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — The Entire Value Structure

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm, from directly above. Wide cone pass — one or two passes at most. The shoulder pad crowns, helmet dome, backpack top, and knee plate crowns receive near-white. The plate sides receive a natural grey gradient. The recesses and undersides stay black from Step 1. This zenithal gradient is your complete value architecture for the armor. Everything you do from Step 3 onward is refining this — not replacing it.

P-003 White Primer
Pro Acryl
~92% value — zenithal highlight; top surfaces only
✦ CRITICAL CHECK: Before continuing, photograph the figure and desaturate the image on your phone. You should see clear volumetric form: bright plate crowns fading to dark recesses, a visible implied light source from above. If it looks flat or patchy, redo the zenithal before touching brush to model. The Raven Guard palette has almost no saturated color to hide a poor value foundation.

Step 3 · Wash

Panel Line Wash — Sharpen All Recesses

Black Wash 200, undiluted, applied with a fine detail brush. Flow into every armor panel line and recess by touching the brush tip to the recess and letting surface tension pull the wash in. Do not scrub or drag across flat plate surfaces. This re-establishes the depth in any recesses that the zenithal may have partially filled with white, and sharpens the panel definition before you begin edge work. Wipe any pooling on flat plate faces immediately with a clean damp brush.

First Edge Highlight — Broad Warm Grey

Dark Warm Grey 016, thinned 1:2 with water. Edge highlight all armor plate borders with a slightly broader stroke than the final highlight — this transition line establishes the warm temperature shift that defines the Raven Guard. Every plate edge that catches zenithal light receives this: shoulder plate rims, helmet ridge, knee plate border, backpack corners, greave edges. The warm grey temperature is the key contrast point versus Iron Hands — their cold grey glaze vs this warm grey edge. Recesses and undersides stay dark.

Bright Warm Grey 015, thinned 1:3. A narrower, sharper line than the first — placed directly on the plate edge itself, inside the first highlight. The "warm" in Bright Warm Grey distinguishes this from the neutral or cool greys used on the Iron Hands guide at the same step.

Step 6 · Brush

Specular — Warm Ivory Point

Bright Ivory 022, thinned 1:4. A single catch-light point or very short stroke placed only at the absolute apex of each plate: the crown of the helmet, the top centre of each shoulder pad, the peak of knee plates. This is not a highlight line — it is a catch-light dot. The warm ivory temperature is the defining difference from Iron Hands (which uses Bright Neutral Grey 045 at this step). The ivory reads warm, dusty, and organic — the Raven Guard survivor's black, worn by the ash wastes, not the sterile cold steel of the Iron Hands. Should read at approximately 75% value.

Bright Ivory 022
Pro Acryl

✦ TIP: This specular is the moment the black armor "catches" and tells the viewer there is a light source above. If you cannot see it from arm's length, add a second dot. ⚠ LEGION DISTINCTION — RAVEN GUARD vs IRON HANDS: Both legions wear black armor. The visual difference is entirely in highlight temperature. Iron Hands: Payne's Grey cool glaze → Cool Grey → Bright Neutral Grey (cold, steel-blue). Raven Guard: no glaze → Dark Warm Grey → Bright Warm Grey → Bright Ivory (warm, dusty, ash-touched). If the two legions look identical at arm's length, the Raven Guard specular isn't warm enough — push the Bright Ivory more aggressively.

Eye Lenses

Red optical lenses — the one saturated color that breaks the monochrome palette and tells the viewer this is a living soldier, not rubble. The red-to-orange temperature gradient creates the illusion of luminosity. In the context of black armor and white shoulder pads, this small point of saturated red color carries enormous visual weight.

Red Lens — Step by Step
Step 1 · Brush

Socket Shadow — Full Black Base

Coal Black 002, undiluted. Fill the entire lens recess completely, including the socket housing around it. Solid and opaque — no streaks, no grey from the zenithal showing through. Let it dry fully before any subsequent step. This base is your permanent shadow zone at the lens rim and your darkest anchor for the gradient above.

This is the true saturated red — the main lens color at full intensity. At 28mm a single confident stroke of this color against the black armor reads with significant impact. Don't overwork it.

Step 6 · Finish

Gloss Seal — Lens Only

MPAM-003 Gloss Medium, thin application over the lens area only with a fine brush. Apply after all weathering is complete and the rest of the figure has received a matte varnish pass. Weathering

Shattered Legions mandatory heavy weathering — sponge chipping, scratch lines, ash grime wash, and dust on lower armor. The Raven Guard's warm black takes chips and scratches in the same warm grey tones established in the armor highlight progression, keeping the whole figure tonally coherent.

Sponge Chipping — Exposed Ceramite
Step 1 · Brush (Sponge)

Chip Color — Warm Grey Ceramite

Bright Warm Grey 015, thinned 1:2. Tear (do not cut) a small piece of blister foam to create an irregular edge. Dab into the paint, press onto a palette first to check the pattern — you want irregular micro-dots and short streaks, not blobs. Apply to armor edges, plate faces, and any surface that would receive impact or abrasion: shoulder pad rims, knee plates, helmet cheeks, backpack corners, shin guards. The warm grey chips read as exposed ceramite sub-layer — consistent with the warm tone of the armor highlight progression, not a random grey intruding from another color temperature.

Bright Warm Grey 015
Pro Acryl
~58% value — chip color; warm exposed ceramite
✦ TIP: Start with very light pressure — less pressure gives finer, more realistic chips. Heavier pressure gives larger battle damage. Begin with light chips on upper armor and increase intensity on lower sections (shins, boots receive more ground-level damage). Always test on a scrap surface before touching the figure. You can always add more chips; you cannot remove them.
Step 2 · Brush

Chip Shadow — Undercut Dark

Coal Black 002, thinned 1:3. Using a fine liner brush, place a very thin dark line on the lower or shadow edge of the larger sponge chips. This simulates the dark cavity beneath a paint chip — the undercut shadow. Not every chip needs this; focus on the largest, most prominent chips that read as actual material removal rather than surface scuffs. This detail is the difference between chips that look painted and chips that look real.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: needle not fully seated, or dried VMC metallic accumulation at the cup-to-needle junction. Fix: fully seat the needle, then disassemble and clean the junction with airbrush cleaner on a cotton swab. VMC metallic pigment accumulates here faster than standard paint — clean after every VMC session, not just when problems appear.

Zenithal Too Flat / No Gradient

Cause: held too close, too many passes, or used thinned primer. Fix: allow to dry and assess from arm's length. For future attempts — one pass, 20–25cm, 30+ PSI, directly above. Do not try to add more white once it looks good — overworking kills the gradient. If completely flat, re-prime black and redo the zenithal from scratch rather than trying to correct it in place.

VMC Metallic Spattering / Chunks

Cause: metallic pigment settled and was not agitated, or the cup dried between passes creating a skin. Fix: agitate the cup by tipping and swirling every 2–3 minutes. Between passes, add a drop of distilled water to the cup to keep it live. If chunks appear in the spatter, stop and clean immediately — do not try to paint through it. Chunks will scratch the surface on subsequent passes.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting black armor, building white from grey prime, sponge chipping, scratch lines, lens gradient, targeted washes.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Edge Highlighting Black Armor at 28mm

Load a size 0 or 1 round brush with the highlight color, thinned 1:2 to 1:3. Wipe to a fine point on your palette. Place the tip at the start of an armor plate edge and pull in one smooth, continuous stroke along the entire edge. At 28mm the edge of a shoulder plate is 3–4mm — one stroke. No scrubbing, no multiple passes on the same edge. The paint should flow from the brush tip with almost no pressure applied — if you're pressing down, the paint is too thick. Brace your brush hand against the figure or your other hand to steady against micro-tremors.

✦ TIP: The three-stage warm hierarchy (Dark Warm Grey 016 → Bright Warm Grey 015 → Bright Ivory 022) creates a more organic value ramp than a single flat highlight. Each stage can be applied in one session — let each step dry for 60 seconds before the next rather than waiting for full cure.
Technique · Building White

Building White From Dark Prime Without Chalky Results

Never go directly from black prime to white paint. The hierarchy is: grey base → working white → specular white only. Apply Bright Neutral Grey 045 first — it creates a surface white adheres to without pulling or separating. Then Heavy Warm White S18 as the working white in two thin passes. Reserve MEA-001 Titanium White (Expert Acrylics, tube) for a single final specular dot on the plate crown only. If the white looks chalky, it was applied too thick or too opaque — thin more aggressively (1:3 or thinner) and build with more passes rather than fewer thicker ones.

Technique · Sponge Chipping

Battle Damage with Blister Foam

Tear — not cut — a piece of blister foam to create an irregular edge. The torn edge produces naturally randomized cell patterns. Dab into Bright Warm Grey 015 thinned 1:2, then press onto palette and test: you want micro-dots and irregular short streaks, not a uniform blob. Apply with light pressure for fine chips; slightly more for larger damage. Wrists, lower legs, and any surface that would contact other objects get more damage than protected central armor. Consistent direction of chips (e.g. angled as if from impacts at a specific angle) reads more authentic than random omnidirectional chips.

Technique · Targeted Wash

Targeted Wash Without Tide Lines

Load a fine detail brush with wash, wipe off most of it on a paper towel (the brush should feel almost dry), then touch the tip to a recess. Surface tension pulls the wash in. For larger recesses, a slightly fuller load works — but never drag wash across a flat surface. If a tide mark appears, let it dry completely, then glaze over the edge with the surrounding color thinned 1:5 to 1:8. Patience while the wash dries is the critical skill here — touching wet wash disturbs the pool and creates the tide lines you were trying to avoid.
