Deathwing — The First Company — The oldest and most secretive warriors of the Dark Angels, clad in bone-white Terminator plate that carries the weight of ten thousand years of unspoken guilt. The Deathwing's bone armor reads entirely differently from the green of the standard Battle Brothers: warm, aged ivory against ceremonial gold trim, a deep wine-red tabard, and the stone-grey of the Crux Terminatus. The key challenge is keeping the bone looking warm and aged rather than chalky and cold. Values first: the armor needs genuine depth — near-black recesses against warm bone highlights — or the figure will read as a white blob at arm's length. Zenithal light from directly above. Priority surfaces: bone armor value range, gold trim, crux terminatus.

Bone Terminator Plate

Aged ivory — warm, ancient, and carrying ten millennia of ceremony and sin. The classic Deathwing scheme is not white armor; it's warm bone, somewhere between old parchment and yellowed ivory. Getting this right requires a warm zenithal base, a warm bone tint, and warm brown shadows. If anything in your palette reads as cold (blue-grey, neutral grey, cool white), it will look wrong against this scheme.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime — Full Coverage

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm. Full all-over coverage of every component: armor, weapon, base. No bare plastic showing. For Terminator armor, pay extra attention to the deep recesses around the shoulder plate connection, the chest eagle, and the Crux Terminatus knee detail — the black prime in those areas is the permanent shadow anchor. Prime the entire assembly together so every surface starts from the same point.

✦ TIP: Terminator armor has enormous flat plate surfaces that can look impressive or flat depending entirely on whether the value range is strong enough. The black prime establishing permanent deep recesses is the foundation that makes the final bone read as dimensional. Do not rush this step.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Generous Coverage

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. From directly above, two passes, slightly broader and more generous than you would do for dark armor. Because bone armor reads as a light color, you want the plate crowns to approach near-white — this gives maximum headroom for the bone tint in Step 3 to read as genuinely light on top. The plate sides should be mid-grey; recesses black. Rotate the model 45° between passes to ensure the shoulder plate tops, helmet dome, and knee plate crowns all receive full zenithal coverage.

P-003 White Primer
Pro Acryl
~92% value — zenithal; generous for bone scheme
✦ CRITICAL: Photograph the model now and desaturate the image. The plate crowns should be near-white, plate sides mid-grey, recesses near-black. This gradient IS your bone armor depth — all subsequent steps are tinting this gradient, not replacing it. If the gradient is weak, redo the zenithal before proceeding.
Bone Tone
Step 3 · Airbrush

Ivory Tint — Warm Bone Color

Ivory 023 + MPAM-001 Glaze & Wash Medium at 1:2.5. Semi-transparent — this is a color tint, not a base coat. Apply 2–3 passes at various angles (not just zenithal — all-round coverage so the bone color is even across all plate surfaces). Each pass dries before the next. The white zenithal areas tint into warm bone; the grey mid-tones become warm shadow-bone; the black recesses are nearly unaffected by the transparent paint. After this step the armor should look like ancient yellowed ivory — warm, slightly mottled, with dark recesses still visible. Use MPAM-001 medium rather than water: the medium preserves the Ivory's transparency without weakening adhesion.

Recess Shadow
Step 4 · Wash

Panel Lines & Recess Shadow

Brown Wash 201, thinned 1:2. Apply with a fine detail brush into all armor panel lines, the seam where shoulder plates meet the torso, around the Crux Terminatus knee pad, around belt buckles, and into any surface detail recesses. The warm brown reads as aged shadow in the crevices of old ceramite — organic and appropriate for a Chapter defined by its antiquity. Do not flood flat plate faces; flow by capillary action into recesses only. Wipe any pooling on flat surfaces immediately with a clean damp brush.

Brown Wash 201

✦ OPTIONAL DEPTH LAYER: For the very deepest recesses (where plates meet, around the neck join, under the shoulder pad overhang), apply a second targeted wash of Flesh Wash 202 (thinned 1:3) on top of the dried Brown Wash. The Flesh Wash has a warmer amber-orange undertone that adds extra depth and warmth — it reads as very old shadow that has collected in the same spot for ten thousand years.

At this stage the flat plate body should read as a warm bone color with dark recess lines — the value structure is now in place, and the highlight steps that follow will add the final tonal lift.

Step 6 · Brush

First Edge Highlight — Bright Bone

Bright Ivory 022, thinned 1:3. Edge highlight all armor plate borders — the rim of every plate where it catches zenithal light. For Terminator plate this includes the distinctive shoulder pauldron rims, the chest plate edges, knee plate border, greave edges, and the arm/hand plate transitions. The stroke should be slightly broader than the final specular line — this is the transition that makes the plate edge read with thickness and volume. Step 7 · Brush

Second Edge & Specular — Near-White Peak

Heavy Warm White S18, thinned 1:3. Two applications: first, a slightly narrower line inside the Bright Ivory 022 edge on the most light-facing plate edges (pauldron rim crown, chest plate upper edge, knee plate top). Then a final dot or very short stroke at the absolute apex of each plate — the peak of the pauldron, the crown of the helmet, the topmost point of each knee plate. The slight warmth of S18 is essential: a cool or neutral white would look like a different material against the warm bone palette. You want the specular to read as the same material — bone — just lit more intensely.

Heavy Warm White S18

⚠ WARM TONE THROUGHOUT: Every highlight in this hierarchy is warm-toned — Ivory 023, Bright Ivory 022, Heavy Warm White S18. Do not substitute a cool or neutral white at any stage. Cool whites (neutral titanium white, blue-white) will make the armor read as cold and unpleasant against the warm shadow tones. If in doubt, hold your paint up to the model before applying — if it looks "blue" compared to the armor, it's too cool.

Tabard & Red Cloth Details

The wine-red tabard hanging between the legs and any red cloth accents (loincloth, Chapter pennants) provide the DA's characteristic ceremonial red against the bone armor. This is a deep, desaturated crimson — not bright primary red, but the dark red of ancient robes worn in secret chambers. The contrast between bone and deep red is classic Deathwing iconography.

Eye Lenses

Red lenses — same recipe as the Primaris and Ravenwing. The glowing red optical sensor is the point of saturated color that signals a living warrior inside the ancient plate. Against bone armor the red reads with significant visual impact — even more so than against dark green or black, because the value contrast between the glowing lens and the warm bone is striking. Keep the lens small and precise.

Metals

Two distinct metals: warm ceremonial gold on the trim, aquila, and decorative elements — and cold utilitarian steel on the storm bolter. Both use the same VMC-first approach as the rest of the DA series. The gold on a Terminator is more prominent than on a standard marine: larger pauldron trim, more ornate chest decoration, additional iconographic elements.

Troubleshooting

Bone Looks Chalky / Too Cold

Cause: either the zenithal was too heavy (covering the black recesses and making the whole model near-white), or the Ivory 023 tint pass was applied without MPAM-001 medium (using water instead), or a cool highlight color was used at the specular step. Fix: if the recesses are not dark enough, let it dry, then apply targeted Brown Wash 201 (1:3) into the panel lines to re-establish depth. If the bone tint reads as cold, a very thin glaze of Flesh Wash 202 (1:5) over the whole armor adds warm amber undertone back into the surface.

Brown Wash Tide Marks on Bone Plates

Cause: wash applied too wet or too broadly across flat plate surfaces, then left to dry without wiping the plate face. The wash settles and leaves a ring where it dried on the flat surface. Fix: as soon as the wash is applied, wipe any wet wash off flat plate faces with a clean damp brush — let it stay only in recesses. If tide marks are already dry, let cure fully, then glaze over the affected area with a thin Ivory 023 + MPAM-001 (1:4) pass to soften the tide line. Do not try to remove it while damp; you will make it worse.

VMC Gold Uneven — Streaky or Patchy

Cause: VMC metallic pigment settled in the cup between passes, giving a medium-heavy first pass and a nearly pigment-free second pass. The uneven metallic deposit reads as patchy coverage. Fix: agitate the cup before every pass, not just before filling it. During use, swirl the cup every 2–3 minutes. If patches are already dry, a second full pass at the correct ratio (post-agitation) will level it. Unlike standard acrylic, VMC layers extremely flat on itself when properly agitated — a second pass will not cause thickening.

Technique · Building Bone

Warm Bone Hierarchy on Terminator Plate

Terminator plates are large flat surfaces — much larger than standard marine armor. This is both an advantage (easier to place a clean edge highlight) and a challenge (flat surfaces with no wash accumulation look featureless). The hierarchy is: airbrush tint glaze establishes tone, Brown Wash 201 establishes recess depth, Ivory 023 brush rebuilds the flat surface, Bright Ivory 022 adds the raised plate rim, Heavy Warm White S18 adds the plate crown specular. Every stage uses a warm-toned paint — substituting any cool color breaks the scheme. The Ivory-to-Bright Ivory-to-Warm White progression is deliberately modest in value range by warm-color standards; at arm's length the contrast is exactly right against the brown shadow.

✦ TIP: The broad flat faces of Terminator chest and shoulder plates need the Ivory 023 brush rebuild step (Step 5 in the Armor tab) to avoid looking like airbrushed grey-white. The brush rebuild gives the surface a slightly painted texture that reads correctly as ceramite at this scale. Thin to 1:2 and apply in two passes, letting the first dry to a satin finish before the second.
Technique · Cloth Folds

Red Tabard Fold Highlighting

On cloth, highlights follow the fold ridges — not the plate edges as on armor. Load the brush with Bold Pyrrole Red 003 thinned 1:3, wipe to a fine point, then drag along the top of each fabric fold in the direction the cloth hangs (usually downward on a tabard). The stroke follows the fold crest from top to bottom, getting lighter in pressure toward the bottom as the fold levels off. For a gathered/draped section, the highlights converge toward the point where fabric pulls tightest. On the Deathwing tabard, the fabric hangs between the leg armor — the folds are mostly vertical ridges, and the highlights should follow them vertically.

Technique · Targeted Wash on Flat Bone

Panel Line Wash on Large Flat Surfaces

The risk on Terminator bone armor: applying Brown Wash 201 into the panel lines and then having it wick across the large flat plate faces, leaving tide marks. To prevent this, apply the wash with a near-dry fine detail brush — drag almost all the paint off on a paper towel first, then run the near-dry brush along the panel line. The tiny amount of wash remaining is enough to flow into the recess by capillary action without any excess pooling on the flat surface. For panel lines that run across large flat areas (the chest plate division, the horizontal line across the upper leg), pre-load only the panel line itself; do not flood the area.

Technique · Fine Iconography

Crux Terminatus Detail Painting

At 28mm the Crux Terminatus skull is small, but it's the most iconic Terminator detail — get it right. Use a size 000 or 00 brush, a good light source, and magnification if available. The sequence is: fill the whole Crux area with Dark Neutral Grey 044 first (Step 1 in the Crux tab), then wash (Step 2), then rebuild the stone face with Bright Neutral Grey 045. Do NOT try to paint the skull separately in a lighter color — the entire pad is stone grey, and the skull detail reads from the shadow in its carved recesses, not from a separate color. The edge highlight (Step 4) on the outer Crux rim is what makes it read as a raised object, not just a painted circle.

Grimdark Weathering

The Deathwing have been fighting for ten thousand years. That bone-ivory plate hasn't seen a proper decontamination bay since the Horus Heresy. These techniques lean into that history — grime seeping into plate joints, verdigris blooming on ancient gold trim, old blood on the power fist, and the general texture of armour that has been in continuous service for a very, very long time. Two paths: Villainy Ink enamel for maximum effect, or John Blanche Masterclass acrylic if you'd prefer to keep things water-based.

⚠ ENAMEL WARNING — READ BEFORE OPENING A VILLAINY INK:
Villainy Inks are oil/enamel-based — they are NOT acrylic. Applied to bare or unsealed acrylic paint they will soften and lift it. You must seal the model with varnish before any Villainy Ink step. Cleanup is with enamel thinner or white spirit only. Dry time: 4–12 hours. Keep a dedicated enamel-only brush; never rinse it in your acrylic water cup.

Phase 4 — Final Seal (Enamel Path)
Step 7 · Spray

Lock the Enamel Inks

Once all Villainy Ink applications have cured (minimum 4 hours, ideally overnight), apply a final coat of MPAR-V01 Matte Varnish. This locks the enamel inks in place, protects the verdigris effect, and unifies the surface sheen across the whole model.

Trouble Shooting
Enamel lifted the bone armour paint
Cause: Applied Villainy Ink to an unsealed surface.
Fix: Let the enamel dry fully (4–12 hours). Touch up the lifted area with Ivory 023, re-shade with Brown Wash 201, and re-edge with Bright Ivory 022. Seal thoroughly before attempting enamel work again. The seal step is not optional.
Goon's Grime looks too green on the bone plate faces
Cause: Didn't remove enough ink from the raised plate surfaces.
Fix: Re-wet with a cotton swab barely dampened with enamel thinner and drag across the plate face — the ink should lift off the raised surfaces cleanly and stay in the recesses. If it's dried, re-wet the surface, wait 30 seconds, then wipe.
Verdigris covered too much of the gold trim
Cause: Applied VI-003 Coelia Green or WP4016 Verdigris Drips too broadly.
Fix: For the enamel version (VI-003), re-wet with enamel thinner and wipe off the raised gold surfaces, leaving only the recesses. For the acrylic version (WP4016), once dry, paint over the raised gold surfaces with Rich Gold 028 to restore the metallic surface; verdigris should remain only in the grooves.
Enamel thinner hazed the varnish
Cause: Used acetone-based thinner, or applied thinner before varnish was fully cured.
Fix: Use white spirit or dedicated enamel thinner only — never acetone. Apply sparingly. If frosting has occurred, a careful re-coat of matte varnish over the fully dried surface will usually rescue it.
