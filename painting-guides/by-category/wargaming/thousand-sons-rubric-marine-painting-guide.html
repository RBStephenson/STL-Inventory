Rubric Marines are hollow armour animated by warp-magic alone — the souls of the Thousand Sons consumed by Ahriman's Rubric ten millennia ago, leaving only dust inside enchanted plate. The Aspiring Sorcerer leading the squad is the only living member, distinguished by crimson robes and a force staff. Painting priority: the electric cerulean blue must be genuinely vivid and bright — this is not a dark armour like Dark Angels or Ultramarines, it is the saturated blue of Tzeentch's favour. Build the blue over a generous white zenithal for maximum chromatic intensity. Gold trim is opulent and warm, contrasting hard against the cool blue. Warp-fire lenses glow amber-orange — warm against the cold armour. Zenithal light from directly above throughout.

Armour

Thousand Sons blue — a vivid, electric cerulean that is the defining feature of the warband. This is a bright armour: the plate faces should read as rich royal blue at mid-value, with bright cerulean highlights on crowns and near-white blue on edge specular points. A white zenithal base is critical — it gives the transparent blue glaze its luminosity and prevents the armour looking dark or washed-out. If the blue reads as navy at arm's length, the zenithal wasn't bright enough or the glaze too opaque. Push the highlights further than feels comfortable.

Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm distance. Full all-over coverage of every component. No bare plastic showing. The black prime establishes your permanent recess colour and shadow anchor — all blue zenithal layers will leave black in the deepest recesses automatically.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~5% value — permanent recess anchor; never thin primer
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Maximum Brightness

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm, from directly above. Be generous with this step — a strong white zenithal is what makes the Transparent Blue glaze glow with cerulean luminosity rather than reading as a dark navy wash. Plate crowns should be near-white. Plate sides should receive significant grey graduation. Deep recesses stay black. If the zenithal is too timid, the final blue will look dark and flat. Push it.

P-003 White Primer
Pro Acryl
~92% value — zenithal; generous application critical for chromatic intensity
✦ TIP: After this step, photograph the model and desaturate the photo. You should see a clear gradient from near-white on plate crowns down to black in recesses. If the grey zone is too compressed, do another pass of white before proceeding. Once you glaze blue over a weak zenithal, there's no recovering the brightness.
Blue Colour Development
Step 3 · Airbrush

Transparent Blue Tint — Electric Cerulean Base

Transparent Blue 046 + MPAM-001 Glaze & Wash Medium at 1:2.5. Apply from all angles — not just zenithal — covering every armour surface 2–3 thin passes. Over the white zenithal areas this develops a bright, vibrant electric cerulean blue. Over the black recess areas, the transparent paint barely affects the dark — recesses stay near-black. This is the heart of the technique: the white zenithal becomes the light source for the blue, while the black establishes the shadows, without any further intervention.

Step 4 · Airbrush

Blue 005 Saturation Pass

Blue 005 + MPAM-001 at 1:2.5. Apply from all angles — this is not a zenithal pass, it's an all-round saturation layer. The Transparent Blue 046 created the value gradient; this step adds opacity and chromatic depth to the plate faces. Blue 005 is a more opaque royal blue that shifts the armour from "tinted white zenithal" into a genuinely substantial medium-blue surface. 1–2 passes is enough — you're building saturation, not coverage.

Sky Blue 012, thinned 1:3 with MPAM-001. From a steep zenithal angle — plate crowns only. This brightens the upper face of each armour plate from royal blue into cerulean, which is exactly where light would hit most directly. Keep the cone tight and use 50% trigger. The transition between crown cerulean and plate-face royal blue is the primary visual gradient that gives each armour plate three-dimensional form.

Edge Highlight — Sky Blue 012

Sky Blue 012, thinned 1:2 with water, with a fine brush (size 1 or 0). Apply along every armour plate edge — not just the top edges, all edges. Load the brush, wipe to a clean fine tip, and drag along the edge using the flat side of the bristle rather than the very tip. This creates a consistent edge line. At 28mm, edge highlights should be tight — 0.3–0.5mm wide maximum.

Gold & Scarabs

The Thousand Sons wear more gold trim than almost any other Space Marine warband — it speaks to their origins as scholars of ancient lore, their Egyptian aesthetic, and ten millennia of accumulated wealth and plunder. The gold should read as warm and opulent against the cool cerulean blue. Scarab beetles — the Thousand Sons' sacred symbol — appear on shoulder pads, chest plates, and iconography. These are traditionally blood red and gold, a three-colour composition with the blue armour that is the Thousand Sons' visual signature.

VMC Gold — Zenithal on All Trim

VMC 77.725 Gold, thinned 1:1 with distilled water. Apply at a zenithal angle — top-facing trim surfaces receive two full passes; trim along sides receives a single feathered pass. The black prime underneath acts as the gold's shadow value automatically — any trim surface that faces away from the zenithal angle stays near-black, creating automatic shadow on the gold. Agitate the cup every 2–3 minutes — VMC metallic pigment settles fast.

Lenses & Gems

A Rubric Marine has no eyes — the helmet lenses are windows into the warp-fire that animates an otherwise empty suit. The canonical effect is warm amber-orange glowing warp light: a deliberate warm contrast against the cool cerulean armour that makes the helmet the focal point of the model. The OSL (object source lighting) effect is subtle at 28mm but very effective even as a simple glow gradient rather than a full OSL treatment. The Aspiring Sorcerer's gemstones follow the same warm amber palette.

Helmet Eye Lens — Warp-Fire Glow
Step 1 · Brush

Lens Socket — Near-Black Base

Coal Black 002, thinned 1:2. Fill the entire lens recess with near-black. This is the deepest value point in the lens — the centre of the lens will be the brightest (the "source" of the light), so the socket edge staying dark creates the impression that the glow is coming from depth rather than just being painted on the surface.

Coal Black 002
Pro Acryl

Step 2 · Brush

Glow Base — Dark Crimson S19

Dark Crimson S19, thinned 1:2. Apply as a ring inside the lens, covering approximately the outer 60% of the lens area. Leave the near-black socket edge visible as a thin rim. This dark red reads as the base of the warp-fire — the deeper tones before it brightens toward the centre.

Dark Crimson S19
Pro Acryl Signature Series
~18% value — lens glow base; outer 60% of lens
Step 3 · Brush

Amber Glow — Orange 007

Orange 007, thinned 1:3. Apply to the inner 40% of the lens — a small oval in the upper-centre of the lens face. This shifts the glow from red toward warm amber-orange as it builds toward the "source." The transition from dark crimson to orange is the key gradient that makes the lens read as glowing rather than painted.

Hot Spot — Warm Yellow 072

Warm Yellow 072, thinned 1:4. A small dot in the upper portion of the orange area — the hottest point of the warp-fire. At 28mm this is barely 1mm across. The progression dark red → orange → yellow builds the convincing impression of a light source.

Pale Yellow 060, thinned 1:4. A single tiny dot — the specular catch-light — in the upper corner of the lens face. Not the same location as the yellow hot spot: the hot spot is the brightest glow centre, while this specular is the physical surface reflection of the lens itself. These are two different things on top of each other.

Pale Yellow 060

Robes

The Aspiring Sorcerer — the only living member of the squad — wears flowing robes that mark him as a practitioner of the arcane arts rather than a hollow automaton. The canonical colour is deep blood-crimson: a warm contrast to the cool blue armour that also ties visually to the red scarab details throughout the squad. The cloth should read as rich fabric with visible fold depth, not flat painted red. The Rubric Marines themselves have no robes — this tab applies to the Aspiring Sorcerer only.

Weapons

The Rubric Marine's standard weapon is the Inferno Bolter — a sorcery-enhanced boltgun that fires bolt rounds enchanted to bypass magical wards, destroying both body and spirit. The Aspiring Sorcerer wields a Force Staff for close combat — an arcane focus that channels warp-power. Both weapons should tie visually to the armour: blue body tones on the bolter echo the armour; the staff is gold with a warp-energy gem at its head.

Airbrush Skills

The Thousand Sons armour technique lives and dies by the zenithal sequence. The Transparent Blue 046 glaze over a white zenithal delivers chromatic saturation that no opaque blue can match. Understanding that sequence — and why it works — is the key to this guide.

Zenithal Sequence
Why White Zenithal Matters for Thousand Sons
✦ The Science: Transparent Blue 046 is a colour shift glaze — it tints whatever is beneath it. Over black recesses, it stays dark. Over the white zenithal zenith, it delivers a fully saturated electric cerulean that no opaque blue can replicate. The result is that value structure from the zenithal pre-highlight maps directly onto colour intensity: light areas are bright and saturated, dark areas are dark and desaturated — exactly what you want on a Thousand Sons Rubric Marine. If you skip the white zenithal and use only opaque blue, you lose this effect entirely.

Troubleshooting

Blue Glaze Looks Green on White Areas

Cause: Transparent Blue 046 has a slight cyan bias — too many passes shifts it toward teal on pure white. Fix: Limit glaze passes to two. Let each pass flash-dry before the next. The Blue 005 mid-tone layer on top corrects the temperature back toward royal/cerulean blue. If still too green, a single thin pass of Faded Ultramarine 013 (1:3) restores the warmth.

White Zenithal Looks Chalky / Rough

Cause: White primer applied too thick or too close. Fix: White zenithal should be three thin passes at 20–25cm, not one heavy pass. If it looks grainy or rough after drying, lightly wet-sand with a damp cotton bud before applying the blue glaze — rough texture reads as scratches at 28mm scale.

Gold Metallics Clogging the Nozzle

Cause: VMC metallics settle quickly and the metallic particles can cluster in the nozzle tip. Fix: Agitate the paint cup every 2–3 minutes during application. Keep a dedicated nozzle cleaner or bristle brush to clear the tip between passes. Run pure distilled water through the airbrush between colour changes to prevent metallic residue contaminating the blue layers.

Brush Skills

Thousand Sons reward patience. The blue armour is a gem-like smooth surface — it needs thin, confident edge highlights and controlled recessing washes. The robes benefit from wet blending. The gold requires patience on the sub-millimetre detail work that makes the Rubric Marine look like a walking piece of baroque sorcery.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Power Armour Edges — Control Over Speed

Thousand Sons armour has strong, clean geometric panels — the edge highlights have to be crisp and even to honour the model's aesthetic. Use a size 0 or 00 detail brush. Thin the highlight colour to 1:1 — thin enough to flow freely, not so thin it bleeds. Load the brush, wipe excess on a paper towel until the line you draw on the paper is clean and consistent. Place the brush tip on an armour edge and drag in one confident stroke — never go back over a wet line. Practise on spare sprue before the model. The Sky Blue 012 edges should read as a clean bright seam, not a thick smear.

✦ TIP: At 28mm, the difference between a "good" and "great" edge highlight is usually just consistency of line width — not brightness. A faint but perfectly even highlight reads better than a bright but wobbly one. Aim for even first.
Technique · Glazing

Deepening Recesses Without Hard Lines

Glazing is thinned transparent paint applied in multiple coats to deepen shadow or enrich colour. For the Thousand Sons armour recesses (the joins between torso and pauldrons, armpit vents, leg articulation), glaze in thinned Black Wash 200 (1:3 to 1:5) after the airbrush layers are dry. Apply to the recess only — capillary action pulls it in. Do not flood the surrounding panels. Two thin glaze passes beats one thick pass every time. Let each glaze dry completely before the next.

Technique · Wet Blending for Robes

Smooth Gradient on Fabric Without Layering

Wet blending on the robes produces a smooth shadow-to-highlight gradient in fewer passes than layering. The technique: apply the shadow colour (Dark Crimson S19 + Coal Black 002) to the robe folds and deepest recesses. While still wet, load the highlight colour (Bold Pyrrole Red 003) and apply to peaks and raised folds. Use a clean damp brush — dampened with water only, no paint — and drag across the boundary between the two wet colours in small, gentle strokes. The damp brush draws the pigments together. Work quickly: Pro Acryl has a reasonable open time but once it skins over, wet blending is finished. If it dries, switch to glazing to soften the boundary.

✦ TIP: If the robe fabric is small (28mm scale), wet blending a full robe at once isn't practical — the paint dries before you finish. Instead, wet-blend one robe section (one drape, one fold area) at a time, let it cure, then move to the next.
Technique · Targeted Washing

Gold Recesses — Panel Lines Without Flooding

The gold trim's Brown Wash 201 must go into recesses only — if it floods onto the raised gold surface it leaves a tide mark that dulls the metallic sheen. Use a fine liner brush, load with Brown Wash 201 at 1:1 or undiluted. Touch the brush to the base of a trim recess. Capillary action draws the wash in along the edge. Remove the brush. Check for any run-off onto raised trim and wick it away immediately with the dry corner of a clean brush. Work one section at a time — pauldron rim, chest eagle, helmet trim — so you can monitor each wash before it sets.

Technique · Fine Detail

Eyes, Symbols, Lenses — One Brush, One Pass

At 28mm, the eyes and helmet lenses are 1–2mm at most. You cannot correct a mistake in these areas without repainting the surrounding surface. The rule: mix your colour before you commit. Get the lens recipe (Coal Black → Dark Crimson → Orange → Yellow → White specular) ready on your palette before you start. Apply each colour in a single confident pass from the correct direction. For the lens highlight placement (upper-left quadrant of the lens), mentally rehearse the brush path before touching the model. A size 000 brush or a rigger/liner brush is appropriate here. Lighting: paint these under your brightest task light with a magnifier if needed.

Grimdark Weathering

Rubric Marines are hollow. There is no body inside the armour — only animated dust, the remnant of a Space Marine who fell to sorcery ten thousand years ago. They do not bleed. They do not eat. They do not rest. What accumulates on their armour is not human grime — it is the residue of the warp: caked psychic discharge at every joint, verdigris on gold trim aged ten millennia, the greasy patina of aether travel and unnatural existence. Two paths: Villainy Ink enamel for maximum effect, or John Blanche Masterclass acrylic for a simpler workflow.

⚠ ENAMEL SAFETY — Read Before Starting Path A
Villainy Inks (VI-001 through VI-007) are oil/enamel-based. They require enamel thinner (white spirit) for cleanup — not water. Apply only over a fully cured acrylic varnish seal (MPAR-V01 or equivalent). Dry time: 4–12 hours. Keep white spirit away from your regular acrylic brush water. Never apply over unsealed acrylics — the thinner will lift paint. Work in a ventilated space.
Path A — Villainy Ink (Enamel)
Step 0 · Before Starting

Seal with MPAR-V01 Matte Varnish

Before any enamel work, apply a full coat of MPAR-V01 Pro Acryl Matte Varnish (spray can) to all completed acrylic layers. Let cure fully — minimum 2 hours, ideally overnight. The varnish acts as a barrier between the enamel and your painted surface. Without this step, the enamel thinner used in cleanup will lift your acrylic paint. This step is not optional.

Step A · Enamel

VI-001 Goon's Grime — Warp Residue at Armour Joints

Apply VI-001 Goon's Grime to all armour joint recesses: the collar ring, pauldron hinges, torso-to-leg joins, knee articulation, and the vents on the backpack. The yellow-green tint of Goon's Grime reads as psychic discharge and warp-residue — on blue armour it has an uncanny, slightly wrong quality that suits the Thousand Sons perfectly. Apply undiluted with a fine brush into each recess. Let gravity pull it into the joint. Do not try to remove it — on this model, unlike mechanical weathering, you want the grime to sit visibly in all joint lines. Let dry 4–6 hours.

Step E · Seal & Finish

Final Seal — MPAR-V01 Matte Over MPAM-003 Gloss Spot

Once all enamel layers are fully cured (overnight is safest), apply a second full coat of MPAR-V01 Matte Varnish to lock in all enamel weathering. Trouble Shooting
Enamel lifted the blue armour paint
Cause: Applied Villainy Ink to an unsealed surface.
Fix: Let the enamel dry fully (4–12 hours). Touch up the lifted area with the relevant blue layer (Blue 005 for mid-tone, Sky Blue 012 for highlight). Seal thoroughly before any further enamel work. The seal step is not optional — it is the only thing between the enamel thinner and your acrylic layers.
Coelia Green verdigris covered too much gold
Cause: Applied too broadly or didn't remove excess from raised trim surfaces while still wet.
Fix (enamel — while still wet): Re-wet a cotton swab with a tiny amount of enamel thinner and drag lightly across the raised gold surfaces only. The enamel lifts off raised points, stays in recesses.
Fix (dried enamel): Carefully repaint raised gold surfaces with Rich Gold 028 (Pro Acryl), then re-edge with White Gold 029. The enamel in the recesses stays — only the raised surfaces need repainting.
Goon's Grime looks too obviously yellow-green on armour panels
Cause: Applied to flat armour panel surfaces instead of joint recesses only, or not selectively removed.
Fix: Re-wet with a cotton swab barely dampened with enamel thinner and drag across the flat panel face to lift the grime. On blue armour the yellow-green of Goon's Grime is very visible on plate faces — be deliberate about confining it to joint recesses only. In recesses, the yellow-green reads as warp residue. On plate faces, it just looks like a mistake.
Enamel thinner hazed or frosted the varnish coat
Cause: Used acetone-based thinner, applied thinner before varnish was fully cured, or used too much thinner.
Fix: Use white spirit or dedicated enamel thinner only — never acetone or isopropyl alcohol, which attack acrylic varnish. Apply thinner with a nearly-dry swab — you want the minimum amount that lifts the ink. If frosting has occurred, a careful re-coat of MPAR-V01 matte varnish over the fully dried frosted area will usually rescue it.
