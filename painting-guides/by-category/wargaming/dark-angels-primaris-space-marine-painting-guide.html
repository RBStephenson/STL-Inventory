Dark Angels — The Unforgiven — Ten thousand years of guilt painted in forest green and bone. The classic Dark Angels scheme balances a very dark near-black green armor against cream robes, warm gold trim, and occasional flashes of deep red — the palette of a Chapter that hides its shame behind ceremony and parchment seals. At 28mm the key is extreme value contrast: the green armor must read from near-black in the recesses to a noticeably lighter green on plate crowns, or it will disappear into visual mud. Gold trim and white/bone robes are the secondary reads that give the model its iconic silhouette. Zenithal light from directly above. Priority surfaces: armor green value range, bone robes, gold trim.

Armor

Dark forest green — the "Caliban Green" look. Near-black in recesses transitioning to a bright-ish green on plate crowns. At 28mm the value range needs to be aggressive: if it reads as a single dark green from arm's length, the highlights aren't bright enough. Push them further than feels comfortable.

Troubleshooting

Green Tint Too Opaque / Lost Zenithal

Cause: Dark Camo Green 036 mixed too thick or too many passes applied without waiting for dry time between. The tint coat covers the zenithal gradient, making the armor look uniformly dark. Fix: allow to dry, then assess. If the armor is uniformly dark with no lighter plate crowns visible, the tint was too heavy. Next time: mix 1:2.5 with MPAM-001, not water, and test on a scrap before the model. Water breaks down the green's transparency faster than medium does.

Uneven Gold Coverage / Tide Lines

Cause: VMC Gold applied at wrong ratio or the cup wasn't agitated before the pass. VMC metallic pigment settles quickly and the first cup content is mostly medium with little pigment — the pass will be near-transparent. Fix: shake the bottle thoroughly, swirl the cup, and agitate every 2–3 minutes during use. If tide lines appear (metallic depositing at the edge of the spray pattern), increase distance to 12–15cm and reduce PSI slightly to feather the edge.

Specular Pass Too Wide / Overshot

Cause: Yellow Green 065 specular triggered too hard or held too close, depositing the bright green across the whole plate face rather than just the crown. Fix: reduce trigger to 50% and increase distance slightly (10–12cm). Keep the cone narrow. If the plate faces are already too light, a thin glaze of Dark Camo Green 036 + medium (1:4) can recover some of the mid-tone depth — two thin passes over the plate body area while avoiding the edges.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting dark green armor, building bone from dark prime, fine liner work for scroll text, targeted wash on metallics.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Edge Highlighting Dark Green at 28mm

Load a size 0 or 1 round brush with the highlight color, thinned 1:2 to 1:3. Wipe to a fine point. Place the tip at the start of an armor plate edge and draw along the edge in one smooth continuous stroke. At 28mm this is a 3–5mm movement — one stroke, no going back. The highlight should be a thin, consistent line that sits directly on the plate edge. If it bleeds onto the plate face, the paint is too thin or the brush too loaded. If it doesn't flow at all, the paint is too thick. The correct ratio gives one smooth flowing stroke with minimal pressure.

✦ TIP: For the two-stage highlight (Camo Green 020 broad → Camo Green + Green 004 narrow), apply both in the same session while the first is still slightly tacky — the second stage sits more cleanly on a slightly textured surface than on a fully-dried glossy one.
Technique · Fine Liner Work

Scroll Text and Micro-Detail

Use a size 000 or 00 liner brush. Thin the paint to near-watercolor consistency — almost no pigment in the water, so the paint flows freely from the brush tip without any pressure. For scroll lines: load the brush, hold it nearly horizontal to the surface, and drag from one side to the other in a single uninterrupted stroke. Speed is your friend — a slow deliberate stroke produces a shaky line; a confident steady drag produces a clean one. Practice the motion before touching the figure. For lettering dots or punctuation marks, push the brush tip straight down and lift without dragging.

Technique · Targeted Wash on Metallics

Gold and Steel Recess Wash Without Tide Lines

The key difference between a wash on fabric and a wash on metal: metal surfaces are much smoother, so tide lines are more visible and the wash pools more aggressively. Load a fine detail brush with Brown Wash 201, wipe most of it off, then touch the tip to the recess. Let surface tension pull it in. For gold trim with complex moulded decoration, it helps to work on one small section at a time and immediately wipe any pooling on flat surfaces with a clean damp brush. On steel, be even more conservative — a targeted wash in panel lines only, not broadly applied over the barrel surface.

Grimdark Weathering

The Dark Angels may keep their secrets, but ten thousand years of war leaves marks on the armour. These techniques push your model from parade-ground to battlefield-worn — grime pooled in joints, rust on boltgun chambers, old blood on the boot treads. Two paths: the Villainy Ink enamel path for maximum realism, or the John Blanche Masterclass acrylic path if you'd prefer to skip enamel handling entirely.

⚠ ENAMEL WARNING — READ BEFORE OPENING A VILLAINY INK:
Villainy Inks are oil/enamel-based — they are NOT acrylic. Applied directly to bare or unsealed acrylic paint they will soften and lift the paint underneath. You must apply a barrier varnish before any Villainy Ink step. Cleanup is with enamel thinner or white spirit only — water does nothing. Dry time: 4–12 hours. Keep a dedicated enamel-only brush and never rinse it in your acrylic water cup.

Trouble Shooting
Enamel lifted my acrylic paint
Cause: Applied Villainy Ink to an unsealed surface.
Fix: Let the enamel dry fully (4–12 hours) — trying to wipe wet enamel off bare acrylic makes it worse. Once dry, touch up the lifted area with the original paint. Seal thoroughly before any further enamel work. The seal step is not optional.
Enamel dried before I could remove it from raised surfaces
Cause: Worked too slowly, or in a warm/dry environment that accelerated drying.
Fix: Dampen a cotton swab with enamel thinner and re-wet the dried surface. Let it sit 30 seconds to soften, then remove with a clean swab. Enamel can be re-wet and reworked even after fully drying — it just takes more effort than removing it while still wet.
Thinner hazed or frosted the varnish
Cause: Used acetone-based thinner (nail polish remover) or applied thinner to varnish that wasn't fully cured.
Fix: Use white spirit or dedicated enamel thinner only — never acetone near a model. Apply thinner sparingly. If frosting has already occurred, a careful re-coat of matte varnish over the fully dried surface will usually rescue it.
Villainy Ink contaminated my acrylic brush or rinse cup
Cause: Mixed enamel and acrylic brushes or rinse containers.
Fix: Keep one dedicated enamel-only brush and a separate enamel rinse container (small jar of white spirit). Even trace amounts of enamel thinner in your acrylic water will cause adhesion problems. Label the enamel jar clearly and physically separate it from your acrylic supplies.
