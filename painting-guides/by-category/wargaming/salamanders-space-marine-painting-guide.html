Salamanders Space Marine — The Salamanders chapter is defined by their relationship with fire and their Primarch Vulkan's teachings: endurance, protection, and strength forged in flame. The key visual challenge is the dark green power armor: this isn't a bright military green but a deep, near-black forest green that needs strong value structure to read at gaming distance. Green on green produces nothing — the value journey from dark shadow through rich midtone to crisp edge highlight is what separates a striking Salamander from a muddy blob. Light source is zenithal from above. This guide offers a Traditional track (Pro Acryl layering) and a Speed track (Speedpaint 2.0) for army batch painting.
✦ BATCH PAINTING NOTE: For an army, prime all models at once. Paint one color across all models before moving to the next. The speed track sub-tabs throughout this guide will keep you efficient without sacrificing the scheme's character.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

Full coverage black primer. Every surface covered — no bare grey plastic showing. The black base will naturally remain in the deepest recesses of the armor panels and undercuts, giving you instant shadow without any extra work.

Armor Color Build
Step 2 · Airbrush

Dark Green Shadow Base

Black Green 057 thinned 1:2 airbrushed broadly across all armor surfaces from a zenithal angle above. This is your darkest armor color — it should read in recesses between armor plates and on any surface facing downward. Aim for the black primer to still show through in the very deepest recesses and panel lines. This step does NOT need to be perfectly uniform — variation is good.

Black Green 057
Pro Acryl
~22% value — dark shadow base coat

Main Green — Upper Planes

Green 004 thinned 1:3 applied from directly above and slight front angle. This is your main armor color — it goes on all upper-facing and forward-facing armor surfaces. Leave Black Green 057 visible in recesses, panel lines, and all downward-facing surfaces. Multiple thin passes. At the end of this step, the armor should look like a real green Space Marine with strong natural shadow in recesses.

Green 004

S33 Bright Green thinned 1:4, concentrated only on the very top surfaces of each armor panel — the crowns of the shoulder pads, the top of the chest piece, the upper greaves. Reduce your spray area to just the uppermost 30% of each plate. This sets up where your manual edge highlight will go and gives the model three-dimensional value before you touch a brush.

Edge Highlights — The Game-Changer
Step 5 · Brush

Edge Highlight — Bright Green

Load a fine detail brush (size 1 or 0) with Bright Green S33 thinned 1:1. Draw a consistent thin line along every raised armor plate edge — the rim of the shoulder pad, the edge of the chest plate, every knee and greave panel edge. Keep your hand steady, rest your wrist on something stable. The line should be 1–2 paint widths wide. If you think you've highlighted enough edges, highlight two more. Every edge. No exceptions.

Bright Green S33
Pro Acryl
~52% value — primary edge highlight
✦ TIP: For batch painting, do ALL edge highlights across ALL models at once. It's tedious but the result is consistency across your whole squad. Thin the paint well — a floury paint makes fat, uneven edges.

Final Edge — Brighter Corners

On the very sharpest corners and highest-catching edges only — where two highlighted edges meet, or the very topmost ridge of a curved surface — add a smaller, brighter dot or very short line using a mix of Bright Green S33 with a touch of Bright Yellow Green 039. This is a specular accent, not a full pass. Keep it restrained: maybe 20% of the total edge surface area gets this treatment. It adds significant depth for very little effort.

Details & Trim

Gold chapter trim, purity seals, parchment, the chest aquila — the details that elevate the model from basecoated to finished.

Gold Chapter Trim
✦ SALAMANDERS NOTE: Salamanders use gold/brass as their trim color — shoulder pad rims, the chest aquila, belt buckle, knee pad studs, and decorative piping. This warmth against the dark green creates a powerful, classic look. Get the gold right and the model sings.
Step 1 · Brush

Gold Shadow Base

Dark Umber 019 thinned 1:1, applied as the basecoat over all trim areas. This warm dark brown sets the shadow value for the gold — it's what gives gold its depth and stops it reading as flat metallic paint. Work tidily; any that gets on the armor can be covered when you clean up edges later.

Dark Umber 019

Step 2 · Brush

Rich Gold — Main Layer

Rich Gold 028 thinned 1:2, applied over the trim in 2–3 thin coats. Leave Dark Umber in the very deepest recesses of any textured trim (rope/chain motifs, textured edging). On smooth trim like shoulder pad rims, a single coat of Rich Gold over Dark Umber is enough. Work one trim area at a time so you can control placement.

Rich Gold 028
Pro Acryl
~58% value — primary gold

Gold Shade — Recesses and Panel Lines

Army Painter Strong Tone WP3200 applied carefully into recesses of the gold trim only — around bolts, into textured areas, between trim sections. Apply with a fine brush and let capillary action pull it in. Keep it off flat trim surfaces. Strong Tone gives gold a richer, aged quality compared to a neutral wash.

Gold Edge Highlight

Bright Gold 031

Chapter Symbol & Markings
Step 5 · Brush

Decals / Transfers

The Salamanders Combat Patrol box includes chapter transfers — the fire drake on the left shoulder and company/squad markings for the right. Apply transfers over a gloss-varnished surface (MPAM-003 Gloss Varnish) to prevent silvering. Use Micro-Sol or similar setting solution to conform the decal to curved shoulder pad surfaces. Seal with matte varnish after application is fully dry.

✦ TIP: Apply gloss → transfer → setting solution → wait fully dry → matte seal. Skipping the gloss step causes the transfer film to show (silvering). This is the most common transfer mistake.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: PSI too high or nozzle cap too tight. Effect: paint blows back into the cup and bubbles. Fix: Reduce PSI to 15–18. Check nozzle cap is finger-tight — not over-tightened. At 28mm scale you rarely need high PSI once past priming.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: Moving too slowly or stopping trigger mid-pass. Effect: visible stripes across armor. Fix: Keep the airbrush moving at a consistent speed across the model. Trigger off before you stop moving — never stop with trigger pressed on the model.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: Over-thinned paint or too many passes without drying. Effect: paint beads or runs into recesses. Fix: Test on the palm of your hand first. For armor layers, 1:3 is the minimum recommended ratio. Let each pass flash-dry (30 seconds) before the next.

Brush Skills

Edge highlighting, drybrushing, and controlled washing — the three techniques that do most of the work in this guide.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

The Core 28mm Skill

Edge highlighting is the defining technique for 28mm power armor. Load a size 0 or 1 detail brush with thinned paint (1:1 to 1:2). Pull the loaded brush tip lightly across the paper towel until the paint flow is controlled but not dead. Place the very tip of the brush on the edge of an armor panel and drag along that edge in a single confident stroke — do not hesitate or go back over it while wet. Consistent, even pressure. A thin line is the goal — 1–2 paint widths. Rest your painting hand on something stable or brace your wrist against the other hand. Practice the motion empty before committing to the model.

✦ TIP: If your edge highlights are too thick, your paint is too thick or your brush is over-loaded. If they're broken/beading, your paint is too thin. The ratio and the brush load together determine the line quality — adjust both when troubleshooting.
Technique · Drybrushing

Bases, Texture, Rough Surfaces

Drybrushing is the fastest way to add value structure to textured surfaces — bases, fur, rough stone. Load a stiff flat brush with undiluted or barely thinned paint. Wipe vigorously on a paper towel until almost nothing transfers when you brush across it. Then rapidly drag across the surface — the bristles catch high spots only, depositing a feathered highlight. For the ash highlight on the volcanic base, this technique takes 30 seconds and has excellent results. For armor, drybrush is only appropriate for the steels/metals — the flat surfaces of power armor require edge highlighting instead.

Technique · Targeted Washing

Recesses, Panel Lines, Detail Depth

A wash applied to the whole model produces a blotchy, tide-marked result on smooth armor. A targeted wash applied only into recesses produces clean shadow without contaminating flat surfaces. Load a fine brush with well-thinned wash. Touch the brush to the recess opening — the paint flows in by capillary action. Remove the brush before the wash overflows onto flat surfaces. Work on one panel at a time. Targeted washing takes longer than flooding but the result is significantly cleaner.

✦ TIP: If a wash floods onto a flat surface accidentally, quickly wick it away with a dry brush corner or the tip of a clean damp brush before it dries. Act within 60 seconds.
Technique · Batch Consistency

Painting an Army, Not a Display Piece

For squad painting, consistency across 5–10 models matters more than perfection on any one model. Mix enough of each color for the whole squad at once. Paint the same step on every model before moving to the next (all black primed, then all shadow green, then all main green, etc.). This keeps color temperature and coverage identical. Photographing models as you go helps spot outliers in the batch — inconsistent highlights or missed panels are much more visible in a photo than in hand.

Grimdark Weathering

Nocturne is a volcanic death world. The Salamanders have been fighting in its caldera fields, ash plains, and forge-city ruins for ten millennia. Their armour should show it — volcanic ash packed into every joint, rust on every weapon surface that gets wet, panel lines choked with the particular grey-brown grime of a world that occasionally erupts underneath you. Two paths: Villainy Ink enamel for maximum effect, or John Blanche Masterclass acrylic for a simpler workflow.

⚠ ENAMEL WARNING — READ BEFORE OPENING A VILLAINY INK:
Villainy Inks are oil/enamel-based — they are NOT acrylic. Applied to bare or unsealed acrylic paint they will soften and lift it. You must seal the model with varnish before any Villainy Ink step. Cleanup is with enamel thinner or white spirit only. Dry time: 4–12 hours. Keep a dedicated enamel-only brush and never rinse it in your acrylic water cup.

Trouble Shooting
Enamel lifted the green armour paint
Cause: Applied Villainy Ink to an unsealed surface.
Fix: Let the enamel dry fully (4–12 hours). Touch up the lifted area with Dark Camo Green 036, re-shade with Brown Wash 201, and re-edge with the appropriate highlight colour. Seal thoroughly before any further enamel work. The seal step is not optional.
Volcanic ash effect on lower legs looks too dark, not ashy enough
Cause: VI-007 Dystopian Brown or WP4009 Filth Wash is too warm/brown compared to actual volcanic ash, which is pale grey.
Fix: Once the enamel/wash is fully cured, dry-brush MWP-05 Unbleached Titanium or MWP-01 Titanium White pigment lightly over the boot and lower greave. The pale pigment sits on the raised texture and reads as settled pale ash over the darker mud-grime base — the combination of both tones reads as volcanic ash more convincingly than either alone.
Goon's Grime looks yellow-green on the armour plate faces
Cause: Didn't remove excess from raised plate surfaces.
Fix: Re-wet with a cotton swab barely dampened with enamel thinner and drag across the plate face. The ink lifts off raised surfaces and stays in recesses. On dark green armour even a small amount of yellow-green on the plate face is visible — be thorough with the cleanup.
Enamel thinner hazed the varnish coat
Cause: Used acetone-based thinner or applied thinner before varnish was fully cured.
Fix: Use white spirit or dedicated enamel thinner only — never acetone. Apply thinner sparingly. If frosting has already occurred, a careful re-coat of matte varnish over the fully dried surface will usually rescue it.
