Sister of Battle — Order of Our Martyred Lady — This figure is a devotional display piece: martial, severe, and suffused with religious iconography. The artist's render interprets the Sister's skin as pale and cool — almost ghostly — with black sorrow-mark tears beneath the eyes, reflecting the Order's tradition of grief and sacrifice. The light source is zenithal with a subtle warm-gold accent from the Ecclesiarchy medallion. Priority materials are the near-black power armor with blue-grey edge lighting, deep crimson vestments, and full gold TMM for all iconography. Values must be wide: near-black shadows on the armor read from across a table; the pale skin and gold metallics provide the eye-path contrast.

Skin

Pale, cool, almost grey-green — a martyr's complexion. The artist's render deliberately strips warmth from the skin to reinforce the Order of Our Martyred Lady's tradition of sacrifice and suffering. Black sorrow-mark tears beneath the eyes and burgundy lips are the key character details. Finish is satin.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2A — Pinkle (Modified for Cool Skin) — The Sister is a display-quality 1:6 female figure; the Pinkle method's sub-surface warmth works in our favour even on pale cool skin, because the warm underpainting peeks through thin flesh layers to create depth. A Transparent Blue 046 glaze over the sealed surface then pushes the tone cool. Warm under, cool over — that layered tension is what makes the skin read as living rather than chalky grey.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Cool tone harder to achieve without a final glaze pass; can read flat without enough layers Speed priority, 28mm, first-time painters
✦ Recommended

Method 2A — Pinkle (Modified)

Black prime → white zenithal → restrained magenta all over → Transparent Red into recesses → cool-toned flesh layers → seal → Transparent Blue cool glaze to finish.

Sub-surface scattering creates depth; cool glaze achieves the grey-green martyr tone without chalky flatness Two-stage process (warm build then cool shift); magenta must be very thin or it overpowers Display 1:6, pale feminine figures, stylized or supernatural complexions

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (vein areas) → seal → red wash (capillary zones).

Most precise tonal mapping; the blue wash pass naturally achieves the cool skin tone Three varnish layers and dry times; slow process Maximum realism, time to invest, portrait-level display
No Freckling: The artist's render shows smooth, porcelain skin with no organic texture. Skip freckling entirely. The sorrow-mark tear lines beneath the eyes are deliberate painted marks — not organic texture. See Step 11 below and the Eyes section in Hair & Eyes.

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm distance. Full coverage from all angles. No bare resin. This is the absolute dark anchor for the entire figure.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~3% value — primer base
⚠ NOTE: Do NOT thin primer. Use undiluted at 30+ PSI with a 0.5mm nozzle.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Skin Areas

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 0.5mm nozzle, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Apply to exposed skin only (face, hands, neck). Spray exclusively from above. Near-white on the skull crown, brow ridge, cheekbone tops, nose bridge; grey gradient on vertical face planes; near-black retained in eye sockets, under chin, in all recesses. This zenithal IS the value structure — do not rush it.

✦ TIP: Photograph and desaturate immediately after zenithal. You need to see near-white on top planes, smooth grey mid-gradient, near-black in recesses. If it reads flat, redo before adding any color.

Magenta 011 thinned 1:6 — almost transparent. Apply evenly over all skin. You are not painting it pink; you are leaving a warm ghost beneath the surface that shows through thin flesh layers. One or two soft passes only. The zenithal must remain clearly visible underneath.

Cool Shift & Details
Step 9 · Finish

Satin Varnish Seal

Seal all skin with satin varnish. This isolates the flesh layers before the cool glaze — without this, Transparent Blue will lift soft layers beneath. Allow 30+ minutes to dry fully.

Step 10 · Filter

Cool Blue Glaze — The Grey-Green Shift

Transparent Blue 046 thinned 1:8 (near-watercolor). Single thin glaze pass over the entire skin surface. This shifts the overall tone toward the cool grey-green seen in the artist's render. Wipe back lightly with a barely damp flat brush if it pools. One pass — assess. The warm Pinkle underpainting should remain faintly visible as sub-surface depth beneath the cool surface.

Tiny dot of Heavy Warm White S18 on the center of the lower lip as a gloss catch-light. The deep burgundy against pale grey-green skin is a high-contrast pairing that reads strongly at display distance.

Final Skin Seal

Metals — TMM

Gold is the dominant metal: Aquila, sun-medallion, belt buckle, hem flames, armor trim. Steel/silver appears on armor panel detailing, bolt pistol hardware, and the sword guard. Both follow value-first TMM — near-black in recesses, primary metallic in mid-tones, near-white on apex edges.

✦ VALUE FIRST: Metallic paint reads flat without strong value structure. Near-black in recesses, mid-metallic as the mass, near-white on apex edges. This is as critical for gold as it is for skin.

Zenithal Sequence
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime — Full Coverage

P-002 Black Primer undiluted, 0.5mm, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Rotate and spray from all angles. Every surface covered — no bare resin under arms, behind knees, inner thighs. The black prime is both physical primer and the deepest shadow value anchor for all subsequent work.

⚠ NOTE: Do NOT thin primer — ever. Thinning breaks down the adhesion chemistry. Undiluted only, always.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Skin Areas Only

P-003 White Primer undiluted, 0.5mm, 30+ PSI, 20–25cm. Apply only to exposed skin (face, hands, neck). Spray exclusively from above — 45–90 degrees from horizontal. Skull top: near-white. Face sides: grey gradient. Under chin and in sockets: fully black. This zenithal powers the Pinkle Method underpainting.

Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spattering

Paint dries on needle tip causing spits and line breaks. Between passes, remove dried paint from the needle tip using a dry brush lightly dipped in airbrush cleaner — gentle strokes only, do not let the ferrule enter the cap. Add 5–10% flow improver to reduce frequency.

Blowback — Paint into Cup

Caused by blocking airflow at the tip (surface too close, finger over nozzle). Creates bubbling in the cup. Fix: never block the nozzle tip. Minimum 8cm from surface at all times. If it happens, clear the cup, clean the needle, re-mix.

Pink Goes Chalky

Common with magenta/pink pigments — thin layers over white look washed out. Fix: ensure black base is fully covering before adding magenta. If already chalky, seal with matte varnish, then re-glaze with Dark Magenta S10 at 1:6 to re-saturate shadow zones. Pink needs 3–4 thin passes over black to reach target saturation — this is normal.

Paint Too Thin — Runs

Over-thinning or too much paint per pass on resin. Resin surface tension is lower than metal — err thinner and do more passes. Reduce PSI 2–3 points, increase distance 3–5cm, use multiple thin feathered passes rather than one wet pass.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: edge highlighting (armor, gold, leather), controlled liner work (tear marks, marble veins, lip detail), wash capillary application, and two-eye symmetry protocol.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Loaded-and-Wiped Edge Drag

Used extensively across this figure — armor edges, leather belt edges, gold details, sword guard, pistol panels. Load a fine round (size 1 or 0) with thinned paint. Wipe the bristles on a paper towel until almost no color releases when tested against a white surface. Drag the nearly-dry brush along the raised edge in a single continuous stroke. Color releases only on contact with the edge's apex. A wide stripe means too much paint — wipe more before the next stroke. Practice on a spare card before touching the figure.

✦ TIP: A brush that's too dry can always have more paint loaded. A brush that's too wet ruins three strokes before you realize it. When in doubt, wipe more.
Technique · 10/0 Liner Work

Fine Detail Liner Technique

Used for sorrow-mark tear lines, lip definition, marble veins, and parchment text. Load a 10/0 liner fully, then touch the tip to your palette and draw the brush to a fine point. The loaded belly feeds the tip continuously for a long, even stroke. Hold the brush at the very end of the handle for maximum feel. Pull the brush toward you — do not push. For tear marks: single smooth pull from lower lid down the cheek, constant pressure. No correction strokes — if wrong, let it dry, paint over with skin tone, and retry.

Technique · Capillary Wash Application

Touch-and-Flow for Recesses

Used for all wash passes: armor panel lines, gold recesses, fabric fold shadows. Load a fine liner or size 0 round with the wash, and touch only the tip to the recess entry point. Surface tension draws the wash in automatically — do not brush back and forth. Move from recess to recess, touching and lifting. If a tide mark forms on the panel face, wipe with a barely-damp flat brush immediately before it dries.

⚠ NOTE: Brushing washes back and forth pushes them out of recesses and creates broad staining on panel faces. Touch and flow only.
Technique · Two-Eye Symmetry Protocol

Alternating Strokes for Matched Eyes

Never finish one eye before starting the other. One stroke on the left iris — matching stroke on the right. One pupil dot left — one right. One highlight dot left — one right. This prevents the visual drift that occurs when one eye is judged "finished" before the other begins. Use a magnifier or optivisor — at 1:6 scale, the iris diameter is approximately 5–6mm.

✦ GREYSCALE CHECK: At any point during painting, desaturate a photo of the figure in your phone. It should read clearly — light source visible, form readable, depth apparent. If it looks flat, values are too compressed. Fix values before correcting color. Applies equally to skin, armor, vestments, and metals.
