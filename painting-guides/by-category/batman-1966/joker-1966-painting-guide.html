The Joker (Cesar Romero, 1966) — The Prince of Crime is pure theatrical artifice: chalk-white greasepaint over everything (mustache and all), a hot pink/magenta suit that pops against the campy primary palette of the show, and a lime green wig that doubles as his shirt colour. The B3DSERK render is unpainted grey resin — the actual canonical colors are dramatically different from what the render suggests. Light source is zenithal overhead, matching the bright TV-studio lighting of the series. Priority materials in order: theatrical white face paint (cool shadows, not warm skin tones), vivid hot pink/magenta suit fabric (Magenta 011 family), and the lime green wig and shirt (Yellow Green 065 family). Values first — even on white, contrast defines the face.
⚠ COLOR ACCURACY NOTE: The artist's render shows unpainted grey resin. The canonical 1966 costume (verified: Heritage Auctions lot #89172, 66batmania.com) is: jacket/vest = hot pink/magenta · shirt = lime green · pants = pink/magenta with black piping stripes · tie = black silk · face = chalk white theatrical makeup · hair = lime green wig. The render is accurate for sculpt/form reference only.

Face & Makeup

Theatrical chalk-white greasepaint over the full face — cool grey shadows define form, red lips and black eye surround complete the clown. Romero's mustache is canonically visible under the white paint in close-ups: add as a subtle warm-grey undertone before the white layers.

⚠ NOTE: This is NOT standard flesh tone painting. The face is white theatrical makeup — treat it like painting white ceramic or white fabric. Shadows must be cool (blue-grey), not warm. Using warm tones here will look like sunburned skin under paint rather than stage makeup.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Adapted Method 1 (Cool-Shadow White) — Standard flesh methods don't apply here. The face is theatrical makeup — achromatic white with cool shadows. Use Method 1's layer-build logic but swap every warm flesh tone for a cool neutral grey hierarchy. The Pinkle and Red Zenithal methods would stain the makeup with colour, which is incorrect.

Method 1 — Adapted for White Makeup Recommended

Black prime → white zenithal → cool grey shadow mapping → build up working white layers → final specular dots. Each layer is semi-transparent; value builds gradually without flooding the sculpt.

Most controllable; easy to correct; matches the theatrical flat-makeup look Requires restraint — white layers over-applied early kill shadow structure This figure. White makeup, theatrical proportions, 1:6 display scale.

Method 2A — Pinkle

Not appropriate for this figure. The magenta underpainting mimics sub-surface scattering in real skin — applying it under white theatrical makeup produces a pinkish cast that reads as skin rather than greasepaint.

Produces warm, skin-like tones — incorrect for Joker's chalk-white stage makeup Use for figures with actual exposed flesh tones

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Not appropriate here. Red zenithal underpainting adds warm exertion tones — fine for rugged skin, catastrophic under white theatrical makeup. Would read as sunburn or a rash under white paint.

Warm red undertone incompatible with chalk-white makeup finish Use for rugged/military male figures with exposed skin

Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Prime

P-002 Black Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm distance. Full, even coverage with no bare resin showing. The black base is your deepest shadow and the zenithal contrast will be dramatic because white makeup needs a strong value range.

P-002 Black Primer
Pro Acryl
~3% value — prime base
✦ TIP: Do NOT thin primer. Apply undiluted. Thinning breaks down primer chemistry and turns it into regular paint that won't bond the same way.
Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal

P-003 White Primer — undiluted, 30+ PSI, 0.5mm nozzle, 20–25cm. Apply heavily from directly above. Fade to nothing by mid-face. The black retains in eye sockets, under the chin, and all recesses. The white top planes will become the final highlight zone — your zenithal IS your white makeup base.

P-003 White Primer
Pro Acryl
~92% value — zenithal white
✦ TIP: Photograph the head and desaturate it immediately after zenithal. You should see clear light-source structure: bright forehead and nose tip, graduated mid-tones on the cheeks, dark in the eye sockets and under the chin. If it looks flat, your zenithal was too perpendicular — repeat with more angle variety.
Shadow Mapping
Step 3 · Airbrush

Cool Shadow Mapping

Thin Payne's Grey S02 to 1:4 and apply as a targeted glaze over shadow zones: eye sockets, nose sides, under cheekbones, beneath the chin, and along jaw recesses. This is a tonal pass — you're reinforcing the zenithal with a cool-toned shadow that reads as depth rather than warmth. Multiple very thin passes. This should read at approximately 20–28% value in shadow areas; the black primer already handles anything darker.

Payne's Grey S02
Pro Acryl
~10% value — cool shadow glaze (thinned 1:4)
✦ TIP: Keep this glaze very thin. You want the cool grey to tint the shadow zones — not cover the zenithal structure. Two thin passes are better than one heavy pass.
Step 4 · Brush

Mustache Undertone (Canon Detail)

Cesar Romero's mustache is plainly visible under the white makeup in close-ups of the show — it's one of the most iconic details of his portrayal. Apply a thin glaze of Dark Neutral Grey 044 thinned 1:5 in a subtle horizontal band across the upper lip area, just below the nose. This reads as the hair follicle texture beneath the paint. Keep it subtle — it should be noticeable only in close-up, not from arm's length.

Dark Neutral Grey 044
Pro Acryl
~25% value — thin beard-shadow undertone
White Build
Step 5 · Airbrush

Working White — Mid-Tone Layer

Heavy Titanium White S21 thinned 1:3. Apply from zenithal angle over all upward-facing planes: forehead, nose bridge, cheekbones, chin. Leave shadow zones untouched. This is the working bulk of the white face — it should read at approximately 65–70% value on mid-planes. It will feel lighter than you think you need on the palette but will integrate with the underlying zenithal beautifully.

Heavy Titanium White S21
Pro Acryl
~68% value — working white mid-tone
✦ TIP: Heavy Titanium White S21 has a touch more body than Bold Titanium White 001 — excellent for building coverage without muddying. Thin thoroughly; this is a build pass, not a flood coat.
Step 6 · Brush

White Highlight — Raised Planes

Heavy Warm White S18 thinned 1:2, applied with a flat brush to the uppermost raised surfaces: forehead crown, nose tip, center of cheekbones, brow ridge, center of chin. This should push those zones to approximately 85% value — significantly brighter than the working white. Step back frequently; at 1:6 these highlights look like "too much" up close but read correctly at display distance.

Heavy Warm White S18

Keep edges deliberately theatrical and somewhat imprecise — this is stage makeup, not fine art illustration.

Red Lips

Step 10 · Brush

Final Specular Dots

Bold Titanium White 001 undiluted on the very tip of a size 000 brush. Place single small dots at the absolute peak of each raised surface: forehead center, nose tip, left and right cheekbone peaks. These are the hottest points of the overhead light source and should read at ~97% value — this is the permitted use of pure white. One dot per location. If it looks "too much" close up, it is almost certainly correct from arm's length.

Bold Titanium White 001
Pro Acryl

Step 11 · Finish

Varnish — Mixed Finish

Theatrical stage makeup has a specific finish: chalky-matte overall with a gloss highlight only on the eyes (from the natural moisture of the eye). Apply matte varnish overall, then a targeted gloss varnish on the sclera and lip center only. The contrast between the matte white face and the gloss eyes is an important realism cue.

✦ TIP: Use a very small brush to apply the targeted gloss on the sclera — any gloss spillage onto the white cheeks will look wet and odd.
✦ Brush-only workflow. Expert Acrylics are high-pigment heavy-body acrylics — re-wettable for wet-blending. No airbrush required. Work on a white-primed surface (skip black zenithal for this path).

This establishes the cool grey shadow base. While still damp, pull and feather edges toward the lighter mid zones.

The two paints blend at the meeting point. Expert Acrylics stay open long enough for a clean blend. Work quickly and in small sections.

Detail & Finish
Step 4 · Brush

Strand Definition

Bright Yellow Green 039 thinned 1:2 on a fine liner brush (size 1 or 0). Follow individual curl directions with thin, confident single strokes. Focus on the outer edge of the wig silhouette and any prominent curl ridges. These lines define the synthetic wig texture — slightly more regular than natural hair, with a consistent curl rhythm.

✦ TIP: A synthetic wig has a more uniform curl pattern than real hair. Keep your liner strokes rhythmic and follow the sculpted curl direction — no wild or erratic strokes. Think "regular coil" not "organic wave."

Suit & Vest

Hot pink/magenta cut-away tailcoat and matching vest — the most iconic element of the '66 Joker look. The original costume was magenta that has since faded to pink; paint the freshly-made costume version (vivid magenta) as the canonical intent. The lining inside the jacket is pink satin — slightly lighter and more reflective than the outer wool-blend exterior.

⚠ COLOR NOTE: The suit is HOT PINK/MAGENTA — not purple, not red. The canonical color is Magenta 011 family at mid-tone. The render shows grey because it is unpainted resin.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: paint thinner than airbrush pressure, or paint cup loose. Effect: paint bubbles back into cup or spatters. Fix: tighten the cup, reduce thinner slightly, confirm PSI matches the technique (not 30 PSI on a detail glaze pass).

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving too slow at constant distance creates thick bands; moving too fast creates light passes with visible lanes. Fix: maintain consistent speed AND distance simultaneously. Practice on card first. Build coverage in thin overlapping passes rather than sweeping once.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: over-thinned mix hitting a surface that is already damp from a prior pass. Effect: paint flows into recesses and pools. Fix: allow each pass to fully flash-dry (30 seconds) before the next. If pooling has occurred, let dry completely — do not try to wipe wet pooled paint, it spreads.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: liner work (eye surround, pants piping, card details), edge highlighting (base tiers, fabric edges), glaze layering (suit highlights, face shadows), stippling (money bag canvas texture), and targeted wash application.

Technique · Liner Work

Controlled Line Painting — Black Eye Surround & Piping

A size 000 liner brush loaded to a fine point. Thin paint to ink consistency (1:2 to 1:3) — too thick and the line has ragged edges; too thin and it won't cover in a single stroke. Stabilize your hand by resting your pinky on the figure or a nearby surface. Pull the brush steadily in a single direction — do not back-and-forth. If a line goes wrong, let it dry completely, overpaint the error with the surrounding color, then redo.

✦ TIP: The pants piping should be a single confident stroke down each leg seam. Load the brush, wipe to a fine tip, and commit. Hesitant strokes produce wobbly, irregular lines.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Fabric and Platform Edges

A flat or chisel-tip brush loaded with highlight colour and wiped almost-dry. Drag the edge of the brush along the corner or fold edge. The highlight deposits only on the physical peak of the edge — it reads as the light catching a sharp angle. For base platform tiers, drag along the top edge of each ring. For fabric (lapels, jacket hem, vest border), drag along the outer seam or fold.

✦ TIP: The brush should feel nearly dry when it touches the edge. If you see a thick stripe rather than a fine line, wipe more paint off the brush before applying.
Technique · Glaze Layering

Suit Highlight Build & Face Shadow Glazing

A glaze is paint thinned to near-transparency (1:4 or more) applied over a dry surface to shift hue or value gradually. For the suit: each highlight pass uses a slightly lighter mix than the last — multiple thin glazes build to the final value without losing the structure below. For the face shadows: Payne's Grey thinned 1:4 as a targeted cool tint over shadow zones. Key rule: the surface must be fully dry between glazes.

Technique · Stippling (Canvas Texture)

Money Bag Canvas Surface

Cut or pull a few bristles from an old brush to create an irregular bristle tip. Load with Khaki 061 thinned 1:1.5, wipe nearly dry, then dab/pounce the surface rather than dragging. Each pounce leaves an irregular micro-texture. Use this on the money bag face to suggest woven canvas weave. Work in small areas, assessing frequently — canvas texture should be subtle, not obvious.

Technique · Targeted Wash (Pin-Wash)

Seams, Recesses, Card Borders

Thin the wash 1:1 with water. Load a fine brush and touch the tip to the entry point of a recess (not the broad surface). Surface tension pulls the wash into the recess by capillary action. Do not brush over the surface — only target entries. If the wash spreads beyond the recess, remove it immediately with a clean damp brush pulled away from the recess toward the open surface.
