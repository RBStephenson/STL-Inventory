Bobby the Barbarian — the youngest and most fearless of the D&D kids, rendered here at 1:6 scale from Toon Studios' faithful cartoon-accurate sculpt. Color reference: canonical 1983 animated series design — fair warm skin with peachy-pink undertones, sandy golden-blonde hair, blue eyes, tawny orange-brown fur on loincloth/boots/helmet, cream-white horns, dark leather cross-harness with bronze studs, and a gnarled hardwood war cudgel. Light source: zenithal overhead with warm fill from the right — matching the cartoon's punchy heroic lighting. Priority materials: skin (luminous youth via Pinkle), fur (texture-forward drybrush layers), and the war club (wood grain reads at distance). Values go wider than feels comfortable — Bobby is small but he needs to read from a shelf.

Skin

Fair warm complexion — pale and peachy with pink undertones. Bobby reads as a young, energetic kid: bright, alive, sun-touched but not tanned. No freckling (cartoon stylization). Push the specular hard on the nose tip, cheekbones, and forehead — it reads as youthful vitality at this scale.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2A — The Pinkle — Bobby is a fair-skinned young protagonist, the fearless heart of the group. The Pinkle's magenta underpainting creates the luminous, lit-from-within quality that reads as youthful energy rather than flat paint. At 1:6 scale the sub-surface scattering effect is very visible — it's worth the setup. Cartoon stylization means smooth transitions rather than freckling; push the specular bright and the shadow deep for that graphic hero-light quality.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; easy to correct at any stage Reads flat without enough layers; easy to under-highlight First-time painters, 28mm scale, speed priority
Recommended

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → FW Crimson Ink into recesses → thin flesh layers building up.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; naturally warm, alive-looking skin Requires a solid zenithal — poor coverage produces muddy shadows Fair-skinned protagonist display figures, 1:6 and busts

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Same as 2A but skip the magenta — go straight to FW Crimson Ink at 1:2 over the white zenithal, then build flesh layers.

Natural warmth without the pink saturation of 2A Same zenithal dependency as 2A; slightly less luminous Rugged male, military, or darker-complexioned figures
No Freckling — Cartoon Stylization: Bobby is an 80s animated character. His skin should be clean and graphic — smooth transitions with bold value contrast, no organic texture. Skip the freckle pass entirely. The high specular on the nose and cheekbones does the same work of making skin feel alive without the realism-breaking detail.

Outfit

Base & Groundwork

Adventure terrain — the D&D kids were always tearing through dungeons, forests, and rocky wilderness. This recipe covers outdoor adventure ground: dark earth base with scattered stone, dirt highlight, and tufted grass. Neutral enough not to compete with Bobby's warm orange palette.

Prime Base Surface

Zenithal Sequence
Step 1 · Airbrush

Black Foundation — Full Coverage

Pro Acryl Primer Black, 1:1, PSI 22, 20–25cm. Full rotation. No bare resin showing. Deep recesses — under helmet rim, between fingers, under chin — get a targeted pass at 45°. This layer does not come off. Get it right before continuing.

Step 2 · Airbrush

White Zenithal — Top Down

Primer White or Heavy Warm White S18, 1:2, PSI 15, 15–18cm. Point straight down at the figure. Heavy white on all top planes. Fade to nothing before reaching the sides. Black fully retained in recesses and undercuts.

If it reads flat, add more zenithal. Do not start the Pinkle pass until the greyscale looks right — color cannot fix value problems.
Step 3 · Airbrush

Magenta 011 at 1:4, PSI 15, 15cm. Single thin pass from all angles over skin surfaces — not a zenithal, a full tint. Nozzle nearly closed, light mist, keep moving continuously. White zenithal must still show through clearly. Tinting the zenithal pink, not coating it.

Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spitting

Paint drying on the needle tip causes spatter. Cause: pausing mid-pass with thin paint, or Pro Acryl drying fast in dry ambient conditions. Fix: keep moving at all times; dip needle tip in water between passes; add 5% flow improver to the mix.

Pinkle Too Opaque — Muddy Result

Magenta overcoat fills the zenithal and creates flat purple-pink. Cause: paint ratio too thick or too many passes. Fix: let dry, hit with a light white zenithal to recover highlights, then re-attempt the Pinkle thinner (1:5 or 1:6). There's no shortcut to undoing an overcoat.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Visible bands or lines in the zenithal gradient. Cause: inconsistent movement speed or stopping mid-pass. Fix: practice gradient motion on card first. Smooth arc-like passes at consistent speed. Overlapping passes at lower PSI smooth out banding.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Paint pooling in recesses and running down the figure. Cause: too much volume (PSI too high or too close) with very thin paint. Fix: increase distance to 20cm, reduce PSI by 3–5, or let each pass flash-dry before adding another.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: layering (skin and hair), drybrushing (fur and base), glazing (skin transparents and leather), edge highlighting (leather straps and club ridges), and eye painting.

Technique · Brush Loading

The Two-Stage Load

Load fully, then drag sideways twice across a dry palette section to shape the tip and remove excess. Almost all control problems in brush painting are loading problems — too much paint floods, bleeds, and kills tip precision. Pro Acryl flows easily when thin; err on less paint and more passes. Check load on the back of your non-painting hand: visible mark immediately = correct. Paint floods out = wipe once more.

Technique · Drybrushing

Fur Drybrush Control

Load a flat or fan brush, wipe on paper towel until no visible wet mark transfers. Sweep across fur in short multi-directional strokes to catch texture peaks. Two light passes build more cleanly than one heavy pass. If you over-drybrush (filled-in texture, no shadow contrast), thin a dark wash and float it back over the surface — it will re-establish recesses. Used in: Outfit Steps 3–5, Base Steps 4–6.

Technique · Glazing

Building Depth with Transparent Layers

Glaze = paint thinned to near-transparency (1:4 to 1:8) applied in multiple thin passes. Builds color and depth without covering work beneath. Used in: skin (FW Crimson Ink recesses, unifying glaze), leather (Expert Acrylics Sepia build). Let each glaze dry fully before the next — stacking wet glazes creates muddy pools. Multiple thin passes beat one medium pass every time.

✦ TIP: If a glaze leaves tide marks, the paint was too thick or the surface wasn't fully cured (not just surface-dry). Wait 5+ minutes between passes, not 1–2.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Leather Straps and Club Ridges

Hold the brush near-perpendicular to the surface. Rest your painting hand against the figure or a stable surface for support. A continuous stroke along an edge is better than short dabs — maintains even pressure and width. Intentional slight imperfection is correct on worn surfaces; Bobby's harness is years old and well-used. Used in: Outfit Steps 12–13, Weapon Steps 4–5.

Technique · Eye Painting

Layered Eye Sequence

Work both eyes simultaneously. Size 000 round brush. Sequence: socket shadow → sclera → iris circle → dark upper iris shadow → pupil dot → catch-light dot → gloss varnish over iris. Let each step dry fully — the whole sequence takes 20–30 minutes with dry time. The catch-light dot (upper-left quadrant, overlapping iris and pupil) is the single mark that brings the eye to life. Don't rush the pupil dot; a smeared pupil is the quickest way to make a figure look unfinished.

Technique · Washes vs. Glazes

When to Use Each

Wash: thin, high-flow, pools in recesses by capillary action. Apply and leave it — fighting a wash mid-flow leaves brush marks. Used: base ground (Step 3). Glaze: very thin, applied with brush control to specific zones. Shifts color temperature and value without covering. Used: FW Crimson Ink recess tinting, leather Expert Acrylics build, unifying skin glaze. The key difference: washes self-locate in recesses; glazes go exactly where you put them.
