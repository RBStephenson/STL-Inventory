Hank the Ranger — The team leader: optimistic, athletic, sun-kissed. Paint him with strong zenithal contrast from directly overhead (forest canopy light). Priority order: skin → corduroy green tunic (texture-first) → brown leather armor → steel plate shoulder → golden energy bow. The corduroy ribbing only reads at display distance when ridge highlights are pushed hard — don't hold back. Values first in every section; the golden bow is the accent that ties everything together.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Red Zenithal — Hank is fair-to-medium skinned, outdoorsy, and physically active. The red zenithal builds natural warmth and sun-exertion flush without the luminous softness of Method 2A, which would read as too delicate for the Ranger class. His complexion sits between "fair hero" and "weathered warrior" — Method 2B lands perfectly in the middle.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering: prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers First-time painters, 28mm, speed priority

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta wash all over → FW Crimson Ink into recesses → thin flesh layers.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers needed Poor zenithal = muddy result; harder to correct Fair/feminine, protagonist display figures

Dirty Down Moss

Adds hyper-realistic organic moss to rocks, roots, and shaded ground areas. Works best in recesses and crevices where real moss would actually grow — avoid flat top surfaces and highlight edges.

Step 1 · Brush Effects

Undercoat preparation

Ensure rocks and roots are fully painted and sealed. If your rock shadows include Hunter Green (Folk Art) in the crevices, that acts as a natural undercoat — the Dirty Down will grab it and appear deeper and richer. If not, dab a very thin coat of Hunter Green into target crevices and let dry before proceeding.

Step 2 · Brush Effects

Stipple Dirty Down Moss into crevices

Using a stiff, older brush (or cut-down brush), stipple Dirty Down Moss into rock crevices, along root edges, and into shaded ground areas. Use a pouncing/stippling motion — do NOT paint it on like a wash or it will flow everywhere. Work in small areas.

Step 3 · Brush Effects

Shape while wet

While still wet, use a damp brush to push the product around — pull it up crevice walls, thin it at the edges so it fades naturally, and remove any excess from areas that should stay clean. Dirty Down stays workable for several minutes.

Step 4 · Finish Effects

Organic patching — keep it uneven

Real moss doesn't carpet a surface uniformly. Aim for 30–40% crevice coverage — irregular patches and clumps look far more believable than even coverage. Leave rock peaks and flat stone tops completely clean.

Step 5 · Finish

Allow Dirty Down Moss to cure for at least 2–3 hours (overnight is ideal) before applying your final matte varnish. Varnishing too early can reactivate the product and cause streaking or cloudiness. The moss will retain a natural, slightly textured look through matte varnish.

Troubleshooting

Tip Dry / Spattering

Paint drying on the needle tip causes spatter. Cause: pausing mid-pass with thin paint, especially with Pro Acryl which dries fast. Fix: keep moving at all times; wipe needle tip with a brush lightly dipped in airbrush cleaner between passes; add 5% flow improver to the mix.

Crimson Ink Too Dark / Orange Cast

FW Crimson Ink at full opacity reads orange-red and kills the zenithal. Cause: ratio too thick or too many passes without drying. Fix: thin to 1:3 and apply in one pass from all angles. The goal is a warm tint over the grey, not a red coat. If it goes too dark, a very light white zenithal from directly above recovers the top-plane values.

Freckling — Dots Too Large

Cause: needle too open, distance too close, or PSI too high. Fix: move back to 5–6cm, reduce PSI to 8, and close the needle further. Practice on card until you get true micro-dots before approaching the figure.

Uneven Zenithal / Banding

Visible bands or lines in the gradient. Cause: stopping mid-pass or inconsistent movement speed. Fix: smooth arc passes at consistent speed. Overlap each pass by 30%. Reduce PSI by 3–5 and increase distance to help feather.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: layering (skin and hair), edge highlighting (leather armor and quiver), drybrushing (base terrain), glazing (skin and leather depth), wash control, and eye painting.

Technique · Brush Loading

The Two-Stage Load

Load fully from the palette, then drag sideways twice across a dry palette section to shape the tip and remove excess. Almost all control problems in brush painting are loading problems — too much paint floods, bleeds, and kills tip precision. Pro Acryl flows easily when thin; err on less paint and more passes. Check load on the back of your non-painting hand: paint visible immediately = correct. Paint floods out = wipe once more.

Technique · Layering

Thin Layering for Skin and Hair

Every brush layer should be thin — 1:1 to 1:2 paint:water minimum. Thin layers are transparent enough to let the airbrushed value structure show through while confirming the color. Stack 2–3 thin layers rather than one opaque coat. Each layer dries in 60–90 seconds; patience prevents muddying. Used throughout: skin flesh build, hair mid-tone and highlight layers.

❖ TIP: Test thinness on the back of your hand — the paint should flow off the brush in a thin stream, not sit in a blob. If it runs like water it’s too thin.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Leather Armor and Quiver Edges

Hold the brush near-perpendicular to the surface. Rest your painting hand against the figure or a stable surface for support. One continuous stroke along an edge is better than short dabs — maintains even pressure and width. Hank’s leather is worn field gear, so slight imperfection reads correctly. Used in: Leather & Armor highlight steps, bow and quiver edge work.

Technique · Drybrushing

Base Terrain — Rock and Soil

Load a flat or fan brush, wipe on paper towel until no visible wet mark transfers. Sweep across stone and soil texture with light directional strokes to catch texture peaks. Two light passes build more cleanly than one heavy pass. Drybrush should reveal texture — if it fills the texture in, the brush is too loaded. Used in: Base steps for rock highlights.

❖ TIP: The correct level of brush dryness is drier than you think. Test on the back of your hand — you should barely see the stroke before touching the figure.
Technique · Glazing

Building Depth on Skin and Leather

Glaze = paint thinned to near-transparency (1:5 to 1:10) applied over a sealed surface to shift color or temperature without obscuring detail. Used for: FW Crimson Ink recess tinting on skin, Expert Acrylics Sepia depth builds on leather. Let each glaze dry fully before the next — stacking wet glazes creates muddy pools. Multiple thin passes beat one medium pass every time.

Technique · Eye Painting

Layered Eye Sequence

Work both eyes simultaneously to keep them balanced. Size 000 round brush with a fine, intact tip. Sequence: socket shadow → sclera base → iris circle → dark upper iris shadow → pupil dot → catch-light dot → gloss varnish over iris. Let each step dry fully. The catch-light dot (upper-left quadrant, overlapping iris and pupil) is the mark that brings the eye to life. If a dot goes wrong, immediately wipe with a clean damp brush and retry.

❖ GREYSCALE CHECK: At any stage, desaturate a photo of the figure in your phone’s editor. It should show a clear light source, readable form, and depth. If it looks flat, values are too compressed. Fix values before correcting color.
