Eric the Cavalier — the party's reluctant shield wall, rendered in unflinching realism. This figure demands saturated heraldic contrast: the crimson cape must billow with life, the gold armor must gleam against dark chainmail, and the weathered shield must show every close call Eric refuses to acknowledge. Light source is zenithal — overhead, with warm bounce light off the gold armor casting onto adjacent skin and fabric. Priority surfaces: the gold breastplate (his identity), the crimson cape (his drama), and the aged shield (his honesty). Values first — push highlights harder than feels comfortable at every stage.

Method Selection
Recommended: Method 2A — The Pinkle Method. Eric is the party's protagonist — young, slightly pampered skin that should look lit from within. The magenta underpainting delivers the sub-surface scatter that reads as noble-but-flustered warmth. His furrowed brow and anxious expression will land beautifully against a luminous pinkle base.

Method 1 — Basic

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, speed priority
★ Recommended

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → FW Crimson Ink into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; warm natural shadows with fewer layers Poor zenithal = muddy result; harder to correct once set Fair hero / protagonist, display 1:6, luminous complexion

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Same as 2A but skip magenta — FW Crimson Ink (1:2) or Flesh Wash 202 directly over white zenithal.

Natural exertion flush; works on darker complexions Same zenithal dependency as 2A Rugged male, military, weathered or world-worn figures

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base → satin seal → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (veins) → seal → red wash (capillary zones).

Most realistic tonal mapping; three distinct undertone zones Slowest method — 3 varnish layers; one wash too thick = muddy Historical, portrait, maximum-realism display. Not for 28mm or anime.
Freckling / Speckling: After skin is sealed, thin Burnt Sienna 059 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots, not lines. Nose, cheeks, backs of hands only. One pass → step back → assess. Eric is a noble — go light.

Troubleshooting

Magenta Tint Too Heavy

If the Pinkle tint covers the white zenithal beneath, thin further (1:6 or 1:8) and increase distance to 20cm. The white zenithal value structure must remain visible through the tint. If it's gone opaque, re-seal and start the tint pass again.

Gold Metallic Pooling

Metallic paints pool at low PSI or thick ratios. Increase distance to 15cm, reduce PSI to 10, thin to 1:4. Apply in short trigger pulls from the side rather than straight-on. Multiple thin passes over flat top planes.

Cape Gradient Banding

Hard bands instead of smooth gradient: moving too slowly. Sweep the full cape length in under 2 seconds per pass. Overlap by 50% of spray cone width. Thin the paint one ratio further if bands persist.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: wet blending with Expert Acrylics (skin), controlled layering (cape), directional drybrushing (chainmail), liner-brush NMM gradients, and targeted wash placement.

Technique · Wet Blending (Expert Acrylics)

Blending While Paint Is Open

Expert Acrylics stay workable 3–6 minutes. Apply two adjacent colour bands while both are still wet, then use a clean barely-damp flat brush to blend at the edge with short perpendicular strokes. Speed is the technique — get both colours down before either dries. If blending tears the layer, it has dried: stop, let it cure, and glaze over the top instead.

✦ TIP: Work in small sections — one cheek, one shoulder. The dried zone beneath provides a clean edge to work from on the next section.
Technique · Directional Drybrushing (Chainmail)

Texture Through Controlled Dry-Brush Direction

For chainmail, drybrush direction follows the major contour — horizontal across the torso. Load brush, wipe aggressively until nearly no paint transfers, then apply with short strokes across the rings. Texture builds through many light passes. Never use a fully loaded brush — you will fill the recesses between rings and lose the texture entirely.

✦ TIP: The final Magnesium S42 specular pass should be almost completely dry — it should only catch the outermost ring peaks, not the sides of the links.
Technique · NMM Gradient Layering

Building Steel Without Metallic Paint

NMM gradients are built through successive transparent layers of grey, each slightly lighter than the last, applied in progressively smaller zones toward the light source. Each pass covers roughly 70% of the previous pass width. The final specular line covers only 5–10% of the blade face. Do not rush to the white edge — build the mid-tone foundation first.

✦ TIP: If the gradient shows distinct bands, apply a thin glaze of Grey Blue 055 at 1:6 over the whole blade to unify without destroying the value structure.
Technique · Targeted Wash Placement

Recess Washes Without Flooding

Apply washes with a fine detail brush to specific recesses rather than flooding surfaces. Touch the brush tip to the recess and let capillary action pull the wash along the groove — do not drag. For larger surfaces, apply at recesses only and let it migrate. Wipe excess that pools on flat surfaces before it dries.
