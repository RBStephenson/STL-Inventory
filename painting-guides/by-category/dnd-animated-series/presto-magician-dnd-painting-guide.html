Presto the Magician — the bumbling-but-brave teen wizard of the 1983 cartoon, reimagined as a grounded realistic young mage caught mid-conjuration. Two light stories share the figure: a warm, soft zenithal key from above establishes form across the whole model, and a localized cool blue secondary glow spills off the conjured magic near his cuff. Paint the figure fully under the warm key first, then add the optional blue OSL as a restrained final layer — it should look like the magic is lighting the robe, not like blue paint was splashed on. Priority materials: the deep green wool robe (the hero surface), fair freckled ginger skin, and the translucent blue conjuration. References: the Toon Studios sculpt render and the original Marvel/D&D cartoon character design.

Method Selection
Method 2A — The Pinkle Method (recommended). Presto reads as a young, heroic spellcaster with fair, slightly flushed skin. The magenta-into-flesh underpainting gives that "lit from within" luminosity that suits a protagonist face and plays beautifully against the cool blue magic glow. His mid-tone anchor is Warm Flesh 073 (~58% value) — fair/warm band. Freckling is part of the character, so keep it (realistic figure, not cartoon).

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, anime/cartoon (2–3 layers, no freckling)
★ Recommended

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → Transparent Red into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; reads "lit from within" Poor zenithal = muddy result; harder to correct Fair / heroic / protagonist figures, display 1:6 / 75mm / busts

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Same as 2A but skip magenta — use FW Crimson Ink (1:2) directly over the white zenithal.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; works on darker complexions Same zenithal dependency as 2A Rugged male, military, survivor, weathered figures
Freckling / Speckling: Presto is a ginger — freckling sells the character. After skin is sealed, thin Burnt Sienna 059 to near-watercolor. PSI 8–12, airbrush 3–5cm from surface, needle almost fully closed. Short trigger bursts — micro-dots, not lines. Target nose, cheeks, brow, and the backs of both hands. One pass → step back → assess. Easy to overdo; practice on card first.

Magic & OSL

The translucent blue conjuration pulled from his sleeve — and the optional object-source-lighting it casts. The cartoon's magic pours from Presto's hat; on this sculpt it manifests as a glowing creature at his cuff. Paint it last, after everything else is sealed.

A · The Conjured Glow (always paint this)
✦ READ FIRST: Treat the magic element as a light bulb. Its core is the brightest, near-white; the colour saturates as it moves outward; the form turns translucent at the edges. Build from a light base up, the opposite of normal value-from-dark.
Step 1 · Brush

Bright Core Base

Base the whole conjured element in White Blue S04 — a pale luminous blue-white. This is the light itself; everything else is a glaze over it.

Step 2 · Filter / Glaze

Build the Blue Outward

Glaze Transparent Blue 046 from the outer edges inward in thin layers, leaving the core pale. Each pass deepens the silhouette while the center keeps glowing. Two or three glazes, not one heavy coat.

Transparent Blue 046
Pro Acryl
~45% value — body glaze
Step 3 · Brush

Deep Edge & Wisps

Ultramarine S35 at the deepest outer extremities and the trailing wisps where the magic dissolves into air. Keep it transparent — the form should never go fully opaque.

Ultramarine S35
Pro Acryl
~30% value — deep edge
Step 4 · Brush

Hotspot & Sparks

A final pinpoint of Bold Titanium White 001 at the very brightest core, plus a few tiny spark dots. Optional: a whisper of Fluorescent Green F04 mixed into the white core gives the magic an unnatural energy under daylight.

Bold Titanium White 001
Pro Acryl
~98% value — hotspot
Step 5 · Finish

Gloss the Magic

Gloss varnish the entire conjured element only. The wet shine amplifies the translucency and visually separates the magic from the matte cloth around it.

B · Optional OSL — the Glow Spilling Onto the Figure
✦ OPTIONAL: You can stop after section A and the figure looks great. OSL ties the magic into the model but is unforgiving — commit only if you're comfortable, and keep it subtle. The whole figure was painted under a warm key; the blue is a secondary light, so it only touches surfaces that directly face the glow.
Step 1 · Brush — Glaze

Map the Reach

Decide what the glow can actually see: the inner cuff and sleeve around the magic, the inner edge of the robe nearest it, the palm and underside of the near hand, and the underside of the jaw/cheek on that side. Nothing the glow can't reach gets touched.

⚠ NOTE: OSL fails when it's everywhere. If both sides of the figure are blue, there's no light direction. Keep it to the surfaces directly facing the conjuration.
Step 2 · Filter / Glaze

First Blue Glaze

Transparent Blue 046 thinned heavily (1:6+) or Blue 005 as a glaze, floated onto those mapped surfaces in the thinnest passes — strongest closest to the source, fading to nothing within a short distance. Multiple whisper-thin passes; never one solid coat.

Transparent Blue 046
Pro Acryl
OSL glaze — graduated
Step 3 · Brush

Bounce Highlight

On the surfaces closest to and directly facing the magic — the cuff edge, a knuckle or two, the very edge of the jaw — add a tiny tinted highlight: Sky Blue 012 + White Blue S04. This is the glow physically lighting those edges. Keep it to a handful of touches.

Sky Blue 012 + White Blue S04
Pro Acryl
~80% value — lit edge
✦ TIP: Photograph in greyscale after OSL too. The blue-lit areas should genuinely read as lighter in value, not just bluer — that's the difference between real OSL and a blue smudge.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cap loose or paint too thick → air pushes back into the cup and bubbles. Tighten the nozzle cap, thin the paint, and check the cup isn't overfilled.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Moving too fast or inconsistent distance → striped zenithal. Keep a steady 12–15cm, overlap passes 50%, and build the gradient slowly rather than in one heavy sweep.

Pinkle Too Strong

Magenta went on too heavy and the zenithal disappeared → muddy pink face. Thin further (1:5+), mist from further back, and let the white glow through. Recover by re-floating thin Warm Flesh 073 over it.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Over-thinned paint pools and runs in recesses. Drop to lower PSI, more passes, and let each flash off before the next. Wick a run immediately with a dry brush tip.

Brush Skills

The techniques this guide leans on: edge highlighting the robe folds, glazing for the OSL, and controlled wisp work on the magic.

Technique · Edge Highlighting

Catching the Fold Crests

The green robe's drama lives in its sweeping folds. Load a fine brush, wipe it to near-dry on a paper towel, and drag the side of the tip along the very top edge of each fold. The near-dry brush deposits paint only on the raised ridge and skips the recess — instant crisp highlight. Build in two passes: a broad first highlight, then a tighter brighter line on the sharpest edges only.

✦ TIP: If the paint blobs instead of catching the edge, it's too wet or the brush is too loaded. Wipe more.
Technique · Glazing

Building Translucent Light

Both the OSL and the magic element are built from glazes — paint thinned until it's a coloured film. A glaze shifts colour and value a little at a time without leaving a hard edge, which is exactly how light behaves. Thin to 1:6 or more, apply, let it dry, assess, repeat. Patience here is the whole technique: five thin glazes look like light; one thick coat looks like paint.

✦ TIP: Keep a separate clean, damp brush handy to soften any glaze edge before it dries.
Technique · Wisp & Spark Work

Painting Energy

For the trailing edges of the conjuration, use a fine liner with thinned paint and let the stroke taper to nothing — lift the brush as you pull. Sparks are single tapped dots of near-white. Irregular spacing reads as energy; evenly placed dots read as polka dots, so vary size and gap.
