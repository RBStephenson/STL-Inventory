Skeletor — Eternia's undying lord of destruction demands stark, theatrical contrast: ivory bone bleached by ancient power, blue-grey musculature pushed far brighter on the peaks than feels natural, deep purple shadow pooling in every fabric fold. The green magical energy isn't just a prop — it's the secondary light source for the entire scene, casting cool emerald OSL upward onto the lower figure and washing the diorama base in arcane glow. Paint this in the order it lights: establish dark values everywhere first, then build the green light source last so it unifies everything beneath it. Transparent FX — Two Methods

The spirit creature circling the base, the torch flames, the mini He-Man figure — all are transparent glowing green effects. Choose your method based on how you printed them: Method A for standard opaque/white resin (paint the color on); Method B for transparent clear resin (tint the existing translucency). Both methods produce vivid green glow — just through different routes.

Method A — Standard Resin + Paint
Method B — Transparent Resin Tinted
✦ METHOD A — Standard opaque/white resin. The color is built entirely from paint layers. White prime is essential — without it, the transparent greens read dark and muddy. The key is building toward the brightest green at the tips and thinnest extremities, and finishing with gloss varnish to amplify the luminous quality.
Foundation
Step 1 · Airbrush

White Prime — All Transparent FX Pieces

P-003 White Primer undiluted on all spirit/torch/He-Man parts. Crucial — white base makes transparent and semi-transparent greens glow rather than muddy. 30+ PSI, 0.5mm, even full coverage. Leave no grey or bare resin showing. This is the luminosity foundation for everything that follows.

P-003 White Primer
Pro Acryl
~95% — luminosity base
Step 2 · Airbrush

Transparent Green — Base Tint

Technique · Airbrush

Dark Purple Primer as Pre-Shade

For Skeletor's blue-grey body, P-023 Dark Purple Primer (undiluted, 0.5mm, 30 PSI) is applied between the black prime and the blue-grey color layers. Technique · Airbrush

Low-PSI OSL Glazing

OSL glazes require a fundamentally different airbrush approach than coverage passes. At 8–12 PSI with a 0.2mm nozzle at 3–5cm, the paint barely exits as a near-mist. The goal is accumulation of color temperature, not coverage. Keep the trigger barely open — a quick pass, then stop. The most common mistake is over-applying in one session and then fighting to fix over-saturated OSL that looks painted rather than light. One session = one or two passes maximum. Reassess 24 hours later under controlled lighting before adding more.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: air pressure too high relative to paint viscosity, or nozzle partially clogged forcing a backpressure bubble. Fix: reduce PSI by 5, check for dried paint around the needle tip (clean with a dry brush lightly dipped in cleaner), and confirm the needle seal washer is seated.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: inconsistent trigger pull, moving too slow at the start and end of each pass, or paint drying too fast in the cup. Fix: keep the airbrush moving before and after triggering — never stop over the surface with trigger open. Add a drop of flow improver if paint dries in the cup within a few minutes.

Paint Too Thin / Runs on Stone Base

Cause: over-thinned base coat on a horizontal surface (stone base faces up and collects pooling paint). Fix: reduce to one or two very thin passes and let dry between each. For horizontal surfaces, reduce PSI further to 12 and increase distance to 15cm to reduce paint volume per pass. Let vertical figure pieces dry first, then base last.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: glazing for OSL and color filters, wet blending for muscle transitions (Expert Acrylics path), edge highlighting for metallic armor, dry brushing for stone texture, and Speedpaint 2.0 flow-on application for transparent resin tinting.

Technique · Filter

Transparent Paint Glazing

Used throughout this guide for OSL (Transparent Green 048), bone warmth (Transparent Brown 052), and color filters. Key rule: the glaze should be so thin that its contribution in one pass is barely visible. Thin to 1:5 minimum for filters, 1:3 for color-building glazes. Apply with a soft flat brush — load, then wipe one side on a palette or paper towel, leaving paint on the other side only. This "side-loaded" application feathers naturally rather than flooding. Let each pass fully dry before assessing whether more is needed. Glazing is cumulative — you can always add more, but you cannot subtract.

Technique · Brush

Edge Highlighting — Metal and Fabric

Used on armor edges (Chrome specular), cloak fold ridges (Faded Plum 063), and green clothing edges (Grey Green + Camo Green). The technique: load a fine round or liner brush, pull most of the paint off onto a palette until almost dry, then draw the brush along the target edge using the very tip only. The bone-dry bristle deposits a thin, consistent line without flooding or running. Practice on card first — a consistent edge highlight requires very little paint and a nearly-dry brush. If the first stroke leaves a visible, wide line, the brush is still too wet.

Technique · Brush

Speedpaint 2.0 — Flow-On for Transparent Resin

Used in Method B of the Transparent FX section. Unlike standard acrylics, Speedpaint 2.0 has a self-leveling agent that activates with overworking. The correct technique is: load a soft round brush generously, touch it to the sealed resin surface, and let capillary action pull the paint into position. Move the brush only to direct the flow to new areas — never back-and-forth brushing. For controlled small-area application (recess tinting), use a fine round brush loaded half-way, touch to the recess entry point, and let it pool naturally. If it doesn't flow, add more Speedpaint Medium (not water) to the mix.

Technique · Brush

Drybrushing — Stone Texture

Used on the base stone. Load a stiff flat or fan brush with paint, then wipe aggressively on a paper towel until almost no paint transfers on the towel surface — then wipe once more. This "almost-dry" state is critical. Apply with a rapid back-and-forth scrubbing motion across the stone surface at a nearly-perpendicular angle to the raised texture. The paint catches only the very tallest texture points, building up with multiple passes to create a realistic stone highlight without filling in the dark crevices below. Check frequently — drybrushing looks subtler while wet than after drying.
