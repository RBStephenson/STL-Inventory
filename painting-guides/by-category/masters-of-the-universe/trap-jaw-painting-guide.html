Blue Skin

Trap Jaw's body is a vivid, saturated mid-value blue — not electric, not navy. The methods below are adapted for non-human skin: value structure first, hue second. Shadows push toward near-black blue; highlights push to pale blue-white. The blue is the canvas.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 2B — Dark Blue Zenithal — Trap Jaw's blue skin is non-human and needs to read as vivid and otherworldly even in shadow. A dark blue zenithal over black prime establishes form before any color goes down; subsequent transparent blue layers let the zenithal value read through without flat opaque coverage killing the depth. The Pinkle (Method 2A) is not applicable — there is no sub-surface scattering or flesh warmth to recreate for a monstrous cyborg.

Method 1 — Basic (Adapted)

Black or grey prime → opaque dark blue base coat across all skin → Blue 005 mid-tone layers → Sky Blue 012 highlights → White Blue S04 specular. No zenithal complexity required.

Most forgiving; easy to correct; straightforward execution Can read flat without deliberate, consistent highlight placement First session on this figure, speed priority, or 1:12 scale non-human blue
Recommended

Method 2B — Dark Blue Zenithal

Black prime → dark blue zenithal (Dark Blue 034 thinned 1:2, from above) → vivid Blue 005 layers on light-facing planes → Sky Blue 012 raised-plane highlights → White Blue S04 specular. Value comes from the zenithal; color comes from subsequent layers.

Inherent value structure; vivid saturation without flatness; naturally deep shadows Zenithal quality matters — poor execution produces muddy results Display 1:6, rugged non-human, vivid fantasy or sci-fi skin tones

Method 3 — Transparent Glaze Build

Grey zenithal (full black-to-white) → seal with satin varnish → multiple passes of Transparent Blue 046 (1:5) building up chroma while preserving the grey value map beneath. Final highlights: White Blue S04 mixed glazes only.

Maximum colour saturation and translucency; value map stays clean and precise Slowest method; requires patience and proper varnish isolation between passes Display max-realism, painters with time to invest in layers
No freckling. Trap Jaw is a cyborg villain — his blue skin should read as unnaturally smooth and inhuman. Freckling is a human organic texture element; omit it entirely here.

Vivid Mid-Tone — Blue 005

Mechanical Arm

The mechanical arm is the visual anchor of the figure — a massive dark-steel cybernetic limb that should read as near-dead black in shadow but catch strong metallic specular on every edge. Uses the 1:6 Gloss Black Base Method with Vallejo Metal Color for all airbrush steps.

✦ VALUE FIRST — METALLICS: You are not covering the arm in metallic paint. You are painting the light that hits the metal, leaving the gloss black base as the permanent shadow. Restraint reads far more convincingly than full coverage. Deep recesses near-black; broad mid-planes use dark gunmetal; only apex edges catch chrome specular.
1:6 Gloss Black Base Method
Step 1 · Airbrush

Gloss Black Base

Satin Black S39 thinned 1:2 with a drop of gloss medium, applied over the primed arm. Smooth, even, glossy surface. Dry fully — the glossy ground is critical; it amplifies metallic reflectivity in the layers above. Not primer — needs to be glossy.

Satin Black S39
Pro Acryl
~5% value — permanent shadow layer
Step 2 · Airbrush

Dark Gunmetal — Broad Mid-Plane

VMC 77.720 Gunmetal Grey thinned 1:1 with distilled water, 0.4mm nozzle, 14–16 PSI, 12cm. Apply only to the broad mid-tone planes — large panel faces, upper cylinder surfaces. Do not touch recesses, joints, or undercuts. The gloss black remains everywhere else. Multiple thin passes.

VMC 77.720 Gunmetal Grey

~35% value — dark mid metallic
✦ TIP: VMC is pre-thinned for 0.4mm — do not add flow improver. Agitate frequently; metallic pigment settles within minutes. Do not over-thin: VMC can spider at fine nozzles if reduced past 1:1.5.
Step 3 · Airbrush

Duraluminium — Light-Facing Planes

VMC 77.702 Duraluminium thinned 1:1, 0.4mm nozzle, 12–14 PSI. Apply only to the light-facing surfaces — top of cannon housing, arm cowl crown, upper cylinder rows. Feather into the Gunmetal Grey. The lighter, slightly warmer Duraluminium creates a clear value step that reads as strong three-dimensional form.

Step 4 · Airbrush

Chrome Specular Edge Lines

Plate Armor

The plate armor uses the same dark steel workflow as the mechanical arm but adds orange-red lava OSL bleeding upward from the base into recesses and undersides. Paint both metal surfaces in the same session for consistency; apply OSL after the steel is complete.

Dark Steel — Same Method as Mechanical Arm
✦ CONSISTENCY: Execute Steps 1–5 here simultaneously with the Mechanical Arm sequence. Both metal surfaces should read as the same unified material — same gunmetal mid-tone, same chrome edge intensity, same panel line wash.

Step 9 · Finish

Varnish — Matte Rock, Gloss Lava

Matte varnish across all rock surfaces — stone is non-reflective. Then apply gloss varnish selectively into each lava channel with a small brush. The gloss amplifies the glow effect dramatically. Avoid getting gloss on the rock surface.

✦ TIP: The contrast between matte rock and gloss lava channels is one of the most effective finish tricks on a lava base. The glossy channels look wet and alive; the matte rock looks ancient and dead.

OSL Technique — Lava Glow from Below
Technique · Airbrush

Upward OSL — Painting Light from a Ground Source

Most figures are lit from above; OSL from a lava base reverses this. Rotate the figure so you are looking at it from the side. Aim the airbrush upward at approximately 45° from below the figure. Transparent Orange 050 thinned 1:5 — spray in short bursts, building the glow gradient gradually. The undersides of armor plates, cloth, and the base rock surface near lava channels are the primary targets. The OSL should be strongest directly above the lava and fade to nothing a few centimetres up the figure.

✦ TIP: Paint the base lava channels before finalising the figure's underside OSL. You need to see both together to calibrate the intensity — the figure's orange glow should match the lava's tone, not look brighter or more saturated than the source.
⚠ NOTE: Fluorescent Orange F02 at the lava peaks photographs very bright. Apply a test pass, photograph, then assess — you may need less than you think.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Paint in the air cap or needle housing is being pushed back into the cup. Cause: too much pressure with the trigger held open without spraying. Fix: keep the airbrush moving at all times while the trigger is depressed. Never hold the trigger open while stationary.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Visible horizontal stripes on base coats. Cause: moving too slowly or inconsistent speed across passes. Fix: maintain consistent movement speed and overlap each pass by 50%. Thin the paint slightly if banding persists — this is usually a coverage issue, not a technique issue.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Paint runs or pools on the surface. Cause: paint thinned too far, applied too heavily in one pass, or surface is not primed. Fix: build in more passes at less trigger pull. For metallics — VMC is pre-thinned; adding too much water causes runs and metallic spiderwebbing. Start at 1:1 and adjust.

Brush Skills

Brush techniques used in this guide: wet blending (Expert Acrylics sub-tabs), drybrush (rock base), controlled wash application (lava channels and cloth), transparent glaze layering (green lenses, FW Ink jaw flesh, verdigris medallion), and foam chip weathering.

Technique · Brush

Wet Blending — Expert Acrylics

Expert Acrylics stay open longer than standard acrylics, making wet blending possible on small areas like the face and cloth. Apply the shadow colour and, while still slightly tacky, brush the next tone adjacent to it. Use a clean, slightly damp flat brush to push and feather the boundary between the two wet colours. Work quickly and decisively — if the paint dries before you blend it, add a tiny amount of retarder or rewet with a damp brush. The goal is a gradual value transition, not a muddy mix.

✦ TIP: Rewet the palette edge of the paint, not the surface. Wet blending on the figure itself risks disturbing dried layers below. Apply fresh paint, then blend the fresh edge.
Technique · Brush

Drybrush — Volcanic Rock Texture

Load the brush with Warm Grey 074 from the palette, then wipe most of it off on a paper towel until almost nothing transfers onto paper from a light stroke. Drag the near-dry brush lightly across all rock surfaces — it catches only the highest texture points, leaving recesses untouched. One or two passes, then stop. Overdoing drybrush makes the surface chalky and grey — it should enhance texture, not cover it. A stiff flat brush works better than a round for this technique.

⚠ NOTE: The drybrush highlight should read clearly as grey stone, not white chalk. If the result looks washed out, the brush had too much paint — wipe more aggressively next time.
Technique · Brush

Transparent Glaze — FW Ink and Transparent Paints

Transparent glazes (FW Crimson Ink 513 for the jaw, Transparent Green 048 for lenses, Yellow Green 065 for verdigris medallion) tint the surface without obliterating what's below. Thin well — the tint should be so transparent that you need multiple passes to build it up. Load a medium-sized brush, touch it to the surface in the centre of the area, and let the paint flow outward. Do not brush it on like an opaque colour — let surface tension do the work. One pass, then let it dry before assessing whether another pass is needed.

⚠ NOTE: FW Crimson Ink 513 must be used instead of PA Transparent Red 047 for any thinned red glaze application. PA Transparent Red shifts to magenta when diluted. FW Crimson Ink maintains its pure red hue at any dilution ratio.
Technique · Brush

Foam Chipping — Rust and Paint Wear

Tear a small piece of blister foam or regular foam sponge into an irregular shape. Load with Burnt Red 008 + Dark Umber 019 mix from palette, blot on paper until almost dry. Dab lightly onto edge zones, grip surfaces, and high-wear areas of the arm and armor. The foam's irregular cell structure creates randomised small chips that are nearly impossible to replicate with a brush. Keep chips small and sparse — a few well-placed chips read better than a covered surface.

✦ TIP: Test the foam on a piece of paper first every session — the wetness of the foam and paint changes how it transfers. Always dab, never drag.
