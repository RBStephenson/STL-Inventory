Hatsune Miku — Rock Diva — An anime-aesthetic figure that rewards bold, graphic painting over fine blending: clean hard-edge highlights, maximum saturation on the teal hair, and confident contrast. The canonical hair color is Crypton Future Media's official Miku teal (~#39C5BB), matched to Pro Acryl Turquoise 054 as the primary pigment. The light source is zenithal-above with a strong three-quarter key from the front. Two absolute priority materials: the teal twin-tails and the red SG guitar body — nail these and the whole figure commands the stage.

Method Selection
RECOMMENDED: Method 1 — Basic / Anime Simplified — Miku is a graphic, stylized anime figure. The intended look is clean porcelain skin, not organic realism. Two or three thin layers with bold, defined highlight edges are more authentic to her aesthetic than soft blending or sub-surface scattering. Skip freckling entirely — smooth is accurate here.
★ Recommended for Miku

Method 1 — Basic / Anime Simplified

Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → 1–2 highlight layers with clean confident edges. No magenta underpainting, no freckling. Keep transitions sharp and graphic — that is the aesthetic.

Fastest; clean anime aesthetic; most accurate to Miku's visual identity Can look flat without sufficient value spread — push highlights harder than feels comfortable Anime / cartoon figures, 1:6 display, all scales with stylized intent

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → Magenta 011 all over (thin) → FW Crimson Ink 513 into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; warm and glowing Works against the clean anime look; magenta undertone muddies light flesh tones Fair feminine figures, protagonist display pieces, realistic 1:6 / 75mm

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink 513 (1:2) or Flesh Wash 202 all over → thin flesh layers.

Natural underlying warmth; exertion flush reads naturally Adds organic warmth that pulls away from Miku's idealized anime look Rugged male, military, survivor, weathered figures

Method 3 — Wash Tinting

Mid-tone base coat → satin varnish seal → yellow wash (fatty areas) → seal → blue wash (vein zones) → seal → red wash (capillary zones). Each layer wiped back and isolated with varnish between passes.

Maximum realistic undertone depth; professional portrait results Organic realism works against Miku's idealized anime aesthetic; three varnish layers; slow Realistic portrait figures, historical, display 1:6 / 75mm. Not recommended for anime — Method 1 simplified is correct for Miku.
No Freckling: Smooth graphic highlight approach only. Anime skin reads as porcelain and idealized — no organic texture or freckling. Bold value transitions with clean edges are the correct technique for this figure.

Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: Needle pulled back too far while trigger is up, or cup has too much paint for the PSI. Fix: Reduce paint in cup. Lower PSI slightly. Pull trigger for air before opening needle. Keep airbrush moving while triggering — never trigger while stationary over the figure.

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: Inconsistent speed across the figure, or PSI too high for the distance causing paint to bounce off. Fix: Maintain a consistent wrist speed. Lower PSI by 2–3. Increase distance to 15cm and use more passes to build coverage rather than one heavy pass.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: Over-thinned paint or holding the airbrush too close to the surface too long. Fix: Keep moving. Test on paper card — if it runs on paper, it will run on the figure. Add 1–2 drops of paint to the cup to thicken the ratio. Runs can be lifted while wet with a clean brush tip.

Brush Skills

Techniques used in this guide: liner work for hair strands and tartan pattern, near-dry brush for edge highlights and leather specular, and targeted varnish application for material differentiation.

Technique · Liner Brush — Hair Strands & Fine Lines

Confident Single-Stroke Lines

The most important skill for this figure. Load a fine liner brush fully, then test a stroke on your palette card. The stroke should flow without breaking. Now commit — pull from the crown in the direction of the hair flow in a single fluid motion. Don't stop, don't lift, don't correct mid-stroke. The brush should lift off naturally at the end of the strand. Practice this on a card at the same scale as the figure until you can produce 5 consistent strokes in a row before touching Miku.

✦ TIP: A dry palette card at the side of your workspace is not optional — it's where you test every liner stroke before it goes on the figure. Test, commit, stroke, lift.
Technique · Near-Dry Brush — Edge Highlights & Leather

Loading, Wiping, and Edge Control

Load the brush (a flat or round brush works), then wipe on the palette until you think there's no paint left. There should still be a small amount in the ferrule. Drag this across the edge of the figure — the raised edge of a leather strap, the rim of a metal ring. The near-zero paint load means only the absolute apex of the surface catches color. If paint smears beyond the edge, wipe more before the next stroke.

✦ TIP: For leather specifically, use a flat shader brush with near-dry technique and drag it across the strap face rather than the edge. This deposits color on the raised texture while leaving the recesses dark — mimics the look of surface leather grain.
Technique · Wet Blending — Expert Acrylics

Working Paint While Wet

Expert Acrylics stay workable for 5–10 minutes (longer in humid conditions). Apply the base color, then immediately apply the second color adjacent to or overlapping the first. Use a clean, barely-damp brush to pull the transition line between the two colors — feathering one color into the other. Work on one panel at a time so you don't run out of open time. If the paint starts to drag or lift, it has dried — let it cure fully and glaze over the top rather than trying to re-blend a dry layer.

Technique · Targeted Varnish Application

Surface-by-Surface Finish Control

The contrast between finish types is what makes a figure look composed from real materials rather than uniformly plastic. This figure requires three finish levels in close proximity: gloss (guitar body, eyes, gem, patent boots), satin (harness, stockings, skin), and matte (jacket, skirt, hair ties, speakers, cases). Apply each varnish type with a clean flat brush, working in small sections. Clean the brush thoroughly between varnish types — gloss contaminating a matte surface ruins the effect. Label your varnish bottles clearly if using unmarked dropper bottles.

✦ TIP: When in doubt about a finish, satin is the safest default — it splits the difference between matte and gloss and rarely looks wrong. Reserve true matte for fabric and rough materials; true gloss for lacquered and wet surfaces only.
