Geralt of Rivia — the mutated monster-hunter reads as cold, gaunt, and weather-beaten, not heroic-bright. Two signatures carry the whole figure: the ash-white hair/beard and the burning amber eyes — push the value gap between those and the muted, desaturated everything-else. Light source is a cool zenithal from upper-front, so highlights shift toward a pale neutral rather than warm cream (the warmth is reserved for skin flush and the brass medallion). Priority materials: cold-steel TMM on the swords, mail, and studs (gloss-black-base method at this scale), and heavily weathered wolf-school leather. Keep the palette low-chroma so the eyes and the red mushrooms on the base do the talking.

Method Selection
Method 2B — Red Zenithal (recommended). Geralt is the textbook rugged, weathered male face — deep nasolabial folds, a hard brow, a vertical scar, and decades of monster-hunting. The red zenithal builds genuine sub-surface warmth and an exertion flush under the skin without the risk of over-pinking that the magenta Pinkle would bring on a masculine face. Lean it slightly cool overall: this is a mutant who doesn't quite look healthy. Add a whisper of Payne's Grey S02 glaze in the temples, under-eye, and beard shadow to give that gaunt witcher pallor.

Method 1 — Basic

Classic layering. Prime → shadow undercoat → base coat → mid-tone → highlight → optional unifying glaze.

Most predictable; straightforward to correct Can read flat without enough layers Tabletop / 28mm, first-time painters, anime/cartoon

Method 2A — Pinkle

Black prime → white zenithal → magenta all over → Transparent Red into recesses → thin flesh layers over top.

Luminous sub-surface scattering; fewer layers needed Poor zenithal = muddy; can over-pink a masculine face Fair / feminine, protagonist / hero figures, display
★ Recommended

Method 2B — Red Zenithal

Black prime → white zenithal → FW Crimson Ink (1:2) over the whole face → thin flesh layers over the top, leaving warmth in the recesses.

Natural warmth and exertion flush; rugged, weathered read Same zenithal dependency as 2A — get the value map right first Rugged male, survivor, world-worn figures — Geralt exactly
Freckling / Speckling: Keep it subtle and weathering-focused rather than youthful freckles. After the skin is sealed, thin Petroleum Brown S14 to near-watercolor, PSI 8–12, needle almost closed, 3–5cm. One light pass across the cheekbones, bridge of the nose, and forehead to suggest sun-and-wind damage. Step back and assess before any second pass — the gap between "weathered" and "dirty" is about two seconds of trigger time.

Metals & Mail

Cold worn steel — twin swords (steel + silver), chainmail at waist and shoulders, studded/spiked gauntlets, buckles, and the brass wolf medallion. Gloss-black-base TMM at 1:6; one warm metal for the medallion.

✦ VALUE FIRST: At 1:6 you are painting the light that strikes the metal, not coating it in silver. The gloss black base is the shadow — leave it showing on every plane that turns away from the light. Restraint reads as polished steel; full coverage reads as toy chrome.

Technique — Gloss-Black-Base TMM
Technique · TMM Light-Mapping

Spraying Light, Not Coverage

The whole steel approach lives or dies on restraint. After the gloss black, angle the figure so you can see which planes face your light, and spray VMC only onto those — pull the trigger in short bursts as you track the light planes, never flooding the surface. Re-check from multiple angles: any plane that should be in shadow must keep its gloss black. Chrome touches only the cutting edges and apex points.

✦ TIP: Spray VMC over a glossy ground for maximum reflectivity — VMC metallics level smoother and shine harder over gloss black than over matte.
Troubleshooting

Blowback / Bubbling

Cause: paint cup loose or nozzle blocked → air pushes back into the cup and bubbles. Fix: check the nozzle is seated, clear any dried paint, and don't over-thin VMC (it's ready near 1:1).

Uneven Coverage / Banding

Cause: moving too fast or too close on the zenithal → striped light map. Fix: increase distance to ~15cm, keep the airbrush moving in smooth overlapping passes, build value gradually.

Paint Too Thin / Runs

Cause: over-thinned skin filter pooling in recesses. Fix: drop to 2–3 very light passes, let each flash off; if it pools, stop and let it dry before continuing.

VMC Looks Dull/Grainy

Cause: sprayed over matte or at too low PSI → grain instead of shine. Fix: ensure the gloss black ground, raise PSI slightly, and lay thin even coats — VMC self-levels to a mirror over gloss.

Brush Skills

The brush techniques this guide leans on: controlled drybrushing (mail, beard, rock), edge highlighting (leather, steel), targeted glazing (cold pallor, blade temperature), and fine detail control (eyes, scar, mushroom spots).

Technique · Controlled Drybrush

Mail, Beard & Rock

Load a stiff brush, wipe almost all the paint off onto a towel until it's nearly dry, then drag lightly across raised texture so only the high points catch. Build in several light passes rather than one heavy one — you can always add more, but a loaded drybrush dumps chalk into the recesses you worked to keep dark.

✦ TIP: For chainmail, drag the brush across the rings at a shallow angle so it skips the tops and skips the recesses entirely.
Technique · Edge Highlighting

Leather & Steel Edges

Side-load a fine brush (paint on one edge, blot the other), then run the loaded edge along a raised lip — strap edges, panel seams, blade spines, buckle rims. Let the line break naturally at worn points rather than tracing a perfect unbroken edge; broken highlights read as used gear.

Technique · Targeted Glazing

Cold Pallor & Blade Temperature

A glaze is paint thinned to near-water (1:6–1:8) applied in many invisible layers to shift color or value without obscuring detail. Used here for the Payne's Grey witcher pallor, the brass aging, and cooling the silver blade below the steel. Always build a glaze in multiple passes and let each dry — one heavy glaze stains and streaks.

✦ TIP: Keep a clean damp brush in the other hand to pull a glaze back off a high point if it starts to pool.
Technique · Fine Detail Control

Eyes, Scar & Mushroom Spots

For the smallest marks — the iris glow, the catch-light, the scar line, the cap spots — use the very tip of a well-pointed fine brush with paint at milk consistency. Steady your painting hand against your other hand, and let the brush tip do the work in single deliberate touches rather than strokes.
